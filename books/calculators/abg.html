pH

Negative logarithm of [H⁺] concentration. Reflects net acid-base balance of the body.

RangeInterpretation
<6.80Incompatible with life
6.80–7.10Severe acidosis
7.10–7.35Acidosis
7.35–7.45Normal
7.45–7.60Alkalosis
>7.60Severe alkalosis
>7.80Incompatible with life
Key: pH <7.35 = acidemia. pH >7.45 = alkalemia. The condition causing it = acidosis/alkalosis. A patient can have acidosis + alkalosis simultaneously with normal pH.

PaCO₂ — Respiratory Component

Partial pressure of arterial CO₂. Controlled by alveolar ventilation. Primary respiratory variable.

ValueInterpretation
<35 mmHgHypocarbia → Respiratory alkalosis (hyperventilation)
35–45 mmHgNormal ventilation
>45 mmHgHypercarbia → Respiratory acidosis (hypoventilation)
>60 mmHgSevere respiratory failure
>80 mmHgCritical hypercapnia — acute respiratory failure
Rule: CO₂ moves opposite to pH. ↑PaCO₂ → ↓pH (acidosis). ↓PaCO₂ → ↑pH (alkalosis). CO₂ = "respiratory acid."

HCO₃⁻ — Metabolic Component

Serum bicarbonate. Primary metabolic buffer. Regulated by kidneys (hours to days). Calculated from Henderson-Hasselbalch equation in most ABG analyzers.

ValueInterpretation
<18 mEq/LMetabolic acidosis
18–22 mEq/LLow-normal / mild metabolic acidosis
22–26 mEq/LNormal
26–30 mEq/LHigh-normal / mild metabolic alkalosis
>30 mEq/LMetabolic alkalosis
>40 mEq/LSevere metabolic alkalosis

Base Excess (BE)

Amount of acid or base (in mEq/L) needed to titrate 1 L of blood to pH 7.40 at PaCO₂ 40 mmHg, 37°C. A pure metabolic index — independent of respiratory component.

BE rangeInterpretation
< −10Severe metabolic acidosis (massive base deficit)
−2 to −10Metabolic acidosis (base deficit)
−2 to +2Normal
+2 to +10Metabolic alkalosis (base excess)
> +10Severe metabolic alkalosis
Clinical use: Base deficit in trauma = volume of bicarbonate to give: HCO₃ (mEq) = 0.3 × weight (kg) × |BE|. In cardiac surgery: BE <−5 = significant metabolic acidosis requiring source control.

Anion Gap (AG)

Calculated difference between measured cations and anions. Reveals unmeasured anions (lactate, ketoacids, toxins, uremia).

Formula:
AG = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻)
Normal: 8–12 mEq/L (or 3–11 if albumin-corrected)
Albumin correction: AGcorrected = AG + 2.5 × (4 − albumin g/dL)
AGCause of metabolic acidosis
<12Normal AG (hyperchloremic) — diarrhea, RTA, saline excess
12–20Mildly elevated — lactic acidosis (early), ketoacidosis (mild)
>20High AG — MUDPILES (see below)
>30Almost always organic acidosis (lactic, keto, toxic)
MUDPILES mnemonic: Methanol · Uremia · Diabetic ketoacidosis · Propylene glycol / Paracetamol · Isoniazid / Iron · Lactic acidosis · Ethylene glycol · Salicylates

📐 5-Step Systematic Interpretation

1
Is pH acidemic or alkalemic?
pH <7.35 = acidemia · pH >7.45 = alkalemia · pH 7.35–7.45 = normal (may still have disorder)
2
Identify primary disorder
Acidemia + ↑CO₂ = Resp. acidosis · Acidemia + ↓HCO₃⁻ = Met. acidosis
Alkalemia + ↓CO₂ = Resp. alkalosis · Alkalemia + ↑HCO₃⁻ = Met. alkalosis
3
Is compensation appropriate?
Apply compensation formulas. If not appropriate → mixed disorder.
4
Calculate Anion Gap (if metabolic acidosis)
AG = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻). If elevated → MUDPILES. If normal → HARDUP (hyperchloremic).
5
Delta-delta ratio (if high AG acidosis)
Δ/Δ = (AG − 12) / (24 − HCO₃⁻). <0.4: hyperchloremic co-existing. 0.4–1: mixed high AG + normal AG. 1–2: pure high AG acidosis. >2: high AG + concurrent metabolic alkalosis.

🔬 Acid-Base Conditions

Complete reference for all primary and mixed acid-base disorders with causes, compensation, and clinical context.

🔴 Respiratory Acidosis

pH <7.35 · PaCO₂ >45 mmHg · HCO₃⁻ normal (acute) or elevated (chronic)

Mechanism: Inadequate alveolar ventilation → CO₂ retention → ↑H₂CO₃ → ↑H⁺ → ↓pH
Causes:
  • CNS depression: opioids, benzos, anesthesia, stroke, head trauma
  • Neuromuscular: NMJ blockers, GBS, myasthenia, cervical cord injury
  • Airway obstruction: COPD exacerbation, severe asthma, foreign body
  • Chest wall: flail chest, kyphoscoliosis, morbid obesity
  • Pulmonary: severe pneumonia, ARDS, pulmonary edema
  • Post-CS: residual NMJ blockade, phrenic nerve injury, pain splinting, obesity
TypepHPaCO₂HCO₃⁻
Acute<7.35↑↑↑ 1/10 mmHg
Chronic7.33–7.40↑↑↑ 3.5/10 mmHg
Treatment:
• Acute: ↑ventilation (intubation/MV/NIV, reverse sedation)
• Chronic: treat underlying cause; avoid over-correction (post-hypercapnic alkalosis)
• Post-CS: ensure adequate reversal of NMJ blockade, analgesia, early mobilization

🔵 Respiratory Alkalosis

pH >7.45 · PaCO₂ <35 mmHg · HCO₃⁻ normal (acute) or decreased (chronic)

Mechanism: Hyperventilation → excessive CO₂ washout → ↓H⁺ → ↑pH
Causes:
  • Hypoxia-driven: PE, pulmonary edema, high altitude, severe anemia
  • CNS: anxiety/panic, pain, fever, CNS disease, hepatic encephalopathy
  • Mechanical ventilation: excessive minute volume (post-CS most common)
  • Pregnancy: progesterone-driven physiological hyperventilation
  • Sepsis (early): hyperdynamic phase → hyperventilation
  • Drugs: salicylates (early), methylxanthines, progesterone
TypeHCO₃⁻ fall
Acute↓2 mEq/L per 10 mmHg ↓CO₂
Chronic↓5 mEq/L per 10 mmHg ↓CO₂
Treatment:
• Identify and treat underlying cause
• MV: ↓respiratory rate and/or tidal volume
• Permissive hypercapnia if needed
• Treat pain/anxiety
• Alkalosis >7.55: risk of arrhythmias, ↓ionized Ca²⁺, cerebral vasoconstriction

🟡 Metabolic Acidosis

pH <7.35 · HCO₃⁻ <22 mEq/L · PaCO₂ ↓ (compensatory)

Mechanism: Gain of H⁺ or loss of HCO₃⁻ → ↓pH. Lungs compensate: Kussmaul breathing (↑RR, ↑VT) → ↓CO₂
High AG causes — MUDPILES:
  • Lactic acidosis (L) — most common in ICU (↓O₂ delivery, sepsis, shock, post-CPB)
  • Ketoacidosis — DKA, alcoholic, starvation
  • Uremic acidosis — AKI, CKD end-stage
  • Toxic ingestions — methanol, ethylene glycol, salicylates, isoniazid, metformin
Normal AG (hyperchloremic) — HARDUP:
  • Hyperalimentation · Acetazolamide · Renal tubular acidosis
  • Diarrhea · Ureterosigmoidostomy · Pancreatic fistula
  • Saline excess (dilutional) — common post-CPB with large NS volumes
AGTypeKey cause
Normal (8–12)HyperchloremicDiarrhea, RTA, NS
High (>12)Organic/toxicLactic, keto, uremia
>20High AGOrganic acid accumulation
>30SevereAlmost always organic
Expected PaCO₂ (Winter's formula):
PaCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃⁻ + 8 ± 2
If measured CO₂ > expected → additional respiratory acidosis
If measured CO₂ < expected → additional respiratory alkalosis
Post-cardiac surgery lactic acidosis: Most common cause of metabolic acidosis in cardiac ICU. Type A: ↓oxygen delivery (↓CO, tamponade, graft occlusion). Type B: hepatic dysfunction, metformin, epinephrine (β₂). Lactate >10 mmol/L = very high mortality.

🟢 Metabolic Alkalosis

pH >7.45 · HCO₃⁻ >26 mEq/L · PaCO₂ ↑ (compensatory hypoventilation)

Mechanism: Loss of H⁺ or gain of HCO₃⁻. Respiratory compensation: hypoventilation → ↑CO₂ to ↓pH (limited — hypoxia is a brake on hypoventilation)
Causes by chloride responsiveness:
  • Chloride-responsive (Ur Cl⁻ <20): vomiting, NGT suction, diuretics (loop/thiazide), post-hypercapnic alkalosis (common post-MV weaning in COPD), contraction alkalosis
  • Chloride-resistant (Ur Cl⁻ >20): hyperaldosteronism, Cushing's, Bartter/Gitelman, severe hypokalemia, exogenous mineralocorticoids
  • Post-cardiac surgery: massive blood transfusion (citrate → HCO₃⁻), diuresis, NGT suction, excessive IV NaHCO₃
Expected PaCO₂ compensation:
PaCO₂ = 0.7 × HCO₃⁻ + 21 (±2)
Max compensation ≈ 55–60 mmHg (hypoxia limits hypoventilation)
Treatment:
• Chloride-responsive: IV NaCl, correct hypokalemia, stop diuretics
• Severe (pH >7.60): acetazolamide 250–500 mg IV (↑renal HCO₃⁻ excretion)
• HCl 0.1N infusion (very severe, refractory cases)
• Post-CS: avoid excess NaHCO₃ administration; diuresis with acetazolamide

🚨 Lactic Acidosis

Type A (tissue hypoxia): Shock (cardiogenic, septic, hypovolemic), cardiac arrest, severe anemia, CO poisoning, limb ischemia
Type B (no hypoxia): Metformin, liver failure, thiamine deficiency, malignancy, epinephrine infusion, mitochondrial disease
Severity (lactate mmol/L):
  • 2–4: Mild — identify cause, monitor
  • 4–8: Significant — aggressive resuscitation
  • 8–15: Severe — high mortality (>50%)
  • >15: Critical — >80% mortality without MCS

⚡ Post-CPB Acid-Base Patterns

  • Dilutional metabolic acidosis: Hemodilution with non-bicarbonate prime → ↓HCO₃⁻, normal AG
  • Respiratory alkalosis: Overzealous mechanical ventilation post-bypass
  • Metabolic alkalosis: Massive transfusion (citrate), loop diuretics, NGT
  • High-AG lactic acidosis: ↓CO, graft failure, vasoconstriction → tissue hypoperfusion
  • Hyperchloremic acidosis: Large volumes of normal saline during/after bypass
  • Mixed disorders: Multiple concurrent mechanisms — common in complex cases
Normalization of lactate <2 mmol/L within 24h is a key prognostic marker post-CS. Persistent lactate >4 mmol/L at 24h = consider tamponade, graft failure, ECMO/IABP.
