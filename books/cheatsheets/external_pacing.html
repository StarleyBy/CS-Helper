Temporary External Pacing Clinical Reference 2025

Modes · Parameters · Thresholds · Troubleshooting · Emergency use · Post-cardiac surgery

Indications for temporary pacing
Bradyarrhythmias (Class I / IIa)
  • Haemodynamically unstable bradycardia unresponsive to atropine / chronotropic drugs
  • Complete (3rd degree) AV block with ventricular escape <45 bpm and hypotension
  • Mobitz II second-degree AV block with wide QRS or haemodynamic compromise
  • Alternating bundle branch block / bilateral BBB with syncope
  • Asystole >3 s or symptomatic sinus pauses
Transvenous pacing is preferred for sustained therapy; transcutaneous pacing is a bridge until transvenous/epicardial access.
Post-cardiac surgery & epicardial pacing
  • Transient conduction disturbances after valve surgery or coronary revascularisation
  • AV junctional bradycardia, accelerated idioventricular rhythm, or complete heart block
  • Supraventricular tachycardias (overdrive pacing)
  • Atrial pacing to restore AV synchrony in haemodynamically compromised junctional rhythm
  • Prophylactic backup pacing when epicardial wires are placed (most centres)
Capture thresholds often rise progressively after surgery. Epicardial wires are commonly removed within several days; threshold >10 mA by day 4–5 is not unusual.
Other indications & overdrive pacing
  • Overdrive pacing for SVT (atrial flutter, AVNRT)
  • Drug-refractory VT suppression (overdrive via ventricular lead — rarely used)
  • HCM: LVOT gradient reduction via short AV delay (historical; specialist guidance required)
  • Pause-dependent torsade de pointes (increase rate to 90–110 bpm)
Temporary pacing is not indicated for asystole refractory to CPR unless a reversible cause is identified.

Setup, threshold checks, daily verification
Initial set-up & capture threshold
  1. Connect leads to colour-coded ports (red = ventricular, yellow = atrial).
  2. Set mode DDD or VVI. Rate 70–80 bpm.
  3. Set output to a high starting value (10–20 mA ventricular, 10 mA atrial) to ensure reliable capture from the outset.
  4. Confirm capture: pacing spike followed by wide QRS (ventricular) or P-wave (atrial).
  5. Gradually decrease output until capture is lost → record capture threshold (mA). Titrating down from a high value is the standard safer approach.
  6. Set working output to ≥2× capture threshold, per institutional practice.
Typical thresholds: transvenous RV apex 0.5–1.5 mA; epicardial wires 1–5 mA; may exceed 10 mA after day 4–5.
Sensitivity testing
  1. Set rate 10 bpm below intrinsic rate to allow intrinsic beats to emerge.
  2. If clinically safe (no pacing dependency), output may be temporarily reduced to facilitate sensing assessment. Never do this in complete AV block or pacing-dependent patients.
  3. Increase sensitivity (counter-clockwise / higher mV) until sense indicator stops flashing → undersensing point.
  4. Increase sensitivity (clockwise / lower mV) until indicator flashes with every intrinsic beat → sensing threshold.
  5. Set final sensitivity to ½ the threshold value (e.g., 2 mV threshold → set 1 mV).
Oversensing causes inappropriate inhibition — catastrophic in pacing-dependent patients. Undersensing causes asynchronous pacing and R-on-T risk.
Daily checks & documentation
  • Check underlying rhythm by lowering rate (do NOT turn output down).
  • Verify capture threshold — confirm output still ≥2× threshold.
  • Verify sensing threshold — confirm margin is ½ of threshold.
  • Inspect lead connections; check battery (9 V; replace weekly for alkaline).
  • Document AV delay, PVARP, any fusion or asynchrony.
Failure to perform daily threshold checks is a common cause of unrecognised loss of capture.
AV synchrony — why it matters
Physiological importance
  • Atrial contraction contributes 20–30% of ventricular filling (“atrial kick”) — critical in diastolic dysfunction or reduced compliance.
  • Loss of AV synchrony (e.g. VVI pacing) reduces cardiac output by 15–25% in some patients.
  • After cardiac surgery the heart is stiff (ischaemia-reperfusion, cardioplegia) — reliance on atrial filling is increased.
  • Junctional rhythm post-surgery is common; atrial pacing just above junctional rate restores AV synchrony without ventricular pacing.
DDD vs VVI — clinical decision
  • DDD preferred: intact sinus node, AV block, haemodynamic compromise, post-cardiac surgery with epicardial wires available.
  • VVI acceptable: ventricular backup only, no atrial lead, or atrial arrhythmias present.
  • Post-AVR / CABG with complete heart block: DDD strongly preferred.
Optimise AV delay 150–180 ms. Too short: truncates atrial filling. Too long: intrinsic conduction overtakes and fusion occurs.
Electrical capture vs mechanical capture
Electrical capture
  • Pacing spike followed by wide QRS (ventricular) or P-wave (atrial) on ECG.
  • Confirms myocardial depolarisation has occurred.
  • Does not guarantee effective contraction or cardiac output.
Mechanical capture
  • Actual myocardial contraction producing cardiac output.
  • Must be confirmed separately in haemodynamically unstable patients.
  • Confirm via: palpable pulse, arterial line waveform, Doppler NIBP, or point-of-care echo.
⚠️ In PEA, electrical capture may be present without mechanical output. A wide QRS on the monitor is not sufficient — always verify mechanical capture by pulse or arterial waveform.

Clinical use per scenario
Sick sinus syndrome (preserved AV conduction)
  • Mode: AAI or DDD (if AV conduction borderline)
  • Rate: 60–80 bpm
  • Atrial output: ≥2× threshold (usually 5–8 mA)
  • Atrial sensitivity: 0.5–1.5 mV (½ of sensed P-wave)
  • AV delay: long (200 ms) to allow intrinsic ventricular conduction
Complete heart block
  • Mode: DDD (preferred) or VVI (simple backup)
  • Rate: 70–90 bpm; adjust to haemodynamic stability
  • AV delay: 120–180 ms (capture ventricle before escape)
  • Upper rate: 110–120 bpm (prevent 2:1 block)
  • PMT → extend PVARP to 350 ms or switch to DDI
Post-cardiac surgery (epicardial leads)
  • Mode: DDD or DDI
  • Rate: 80 bpm initially, then wean
  • AV delay: 150 ms (auto mode often suitable)
  • Output: start 10 mA; final ≥2× threshold
  • Atrial output limit: 10 mA (device maximum)
Overdrive pacing for atrial flutter / SVT
  • Mode: AOO via Menu #3 (burst 80–800 min⁻¹)
  • Set burst rate 10–30% above intrinsic atrial rate
  • Deliver burst 5–15 s; re-evaluate rhythm
Risk of rapid ventricular response or AF induction. Defibrillator must be immediately available.

Special clinical situations
Paediatric pacing
  • Smaller transvenous leads (4–5 Fr) or epicardial wires
  • Higher basal rates: neonate 120–150, infant 100–140, child 80–100 bpm
  • Lower output threshold — start 2 mA ventricular
  • Adjust sensitivity carefully — avoid oversensing of high-amplitude paediatric QRS
  • VVI or DDD if AV sequential pacing required
Electrocautery / EMI
  • Electrocautery may cause oversensing (inhibition) or trigger asynchronous pacing
  • Switch to VOO or DOO before electrocautery
  • Manual mode selection preferred; magnet behaviour is device-dependent and unreliable in external temporary generators
  • After electrocautery, revert to demand mode and re-assess capture and sensing
DOO prevents inhibition but carries R-on-T risk. Use only when necessary.
IABP interaction & MRI safety
  • IABP may misinterpret pacing spikes as QRS → trigger at wrong timing; switch IABP to arterial pressure trigger
  • External pacemaker generators and epicardial wires are MRI-unsafe; never enter MRI suite with generator attached
  • Epicardial wires in situ: retrieve or cut close to skin before MRI
Transcutaneous pacing — bridge to transvenous/epicardial access
Technique & setup
  • Anterior-posterior pad placement preferred (lower capture threshold); anterior-lateral is acceptable
  • Start at 40–80 mA; increase by 10 mA until wide QRS on ECG (electrical capture)
  • Typical capture 50–100 mA; may reach 200 mA
  • Rate: 60–80 bpm for bradycardia; 90–110 bpm for torsades
Key clinical points
  • Confirm mechanical capture by pulse or arterial waveform — chest wall contractions can mimic QRS on ECG
  • Provide analgesia/sedation (e.g., morphine 1–2 mg + midazolam 1–2 mg IV) — transcutaneous pacing is painful
  • Temporising measure only; arrange definitive pacing promptly
  • May be ineffective in severe hypothermia (<30°C)
Skeletal muscle contractions can mimic ECG capture. Always confirm mechanical capture by pulse or arterial line.
Post-cardiac surgery pacing & torsades de pointes
Post-cardiac surgery (CABG, valve, TAVI)
  • AV block after AVR: Common; DDD pacing restores AV synchrony and cardiac output
  • Junctional rhythm: Atrial pacing at 80–90 bpm (just above junctional rate) restores AV synchrony post-bypass
  • After TAVI: New LBBB or complete AV block in up to 25%; temporary transvenous pacing until recovery or permanent pacemaker
  • Epicardial wire removal:
    • Steady continuous traction — stop if significant resistance (risk of laceration/haemopericardium)
    • Observe 2–4 h post-removal for tamponade
    • Have pericardiocentesis equipment available
Torsades de pointes (TdP) — temporary pacing
  • Increase pacing rate to 90–110 bpm (ventricular) to shorten QT and suppress pause-dependent TdP
  • Mode: VVI or DDD depending on AV status
  • Simultaneously treat underlying causes:
    • IV magnesium sulphate 2 g over 10–15 min (correct hypomagnesaemia)
    • Target K⁺ ≥4.5 mmol/L (correct hypokalaemia)
    • Withdraw / replace all QT-prolonging drugs
  • Pacing is a bridge — not a cure. Address the underlying QT abnormality.
Emergency asynchronous pacing (DOO) & burst RAP
Emergency key (DOO mode)
  • Press EMERGENCY key → immediately switches to DOO at 80 bpm / 20 mA
  • Use when capture is lost or in profound bradycardia / asystole
  • DOO ignores all sensing → R-on-T risk. Use only in genuine emergency.
  • To exit DOO: press key again or select another mode
  • Confirm mechanical capture by pulse or arterial waveform after activating
Rapid Atrial Pacing (RAP) — burst mode
  • Access via Menu #3 (RAP menu)
  • Rate 80–800 min⁻¹ in AOO or DDD mode
  • Overdrive pacing for atrial flutter / SVT termination
  • Burst 5–15 s; then re-evaluate rhythm
  • Defibrillator must be immediately available
Battery: replace 9 V when low-battery indicator appears (alkaline 7–9 days; lithium 16 days). Always use fresh battery for each new patient.
Safety, microshock precautions & surveillance
Microshock prevention
  • Patients with intracardiac leads risk VF from currents as low as 10 µA delivered directly to the myocardium
  • Use only patient-isolated pacing cables
  • All electrical equipment must share common ground
  • Cover exposed wire terminals with non-conductive caps when not connected
  • Discharge static before handling terminals (touch bed frame first)
Defibrillation with pacemaker in situ
  • External defibrillation is safe with precautions
  • Place paddles/pads ≥15 cm from generator and leads
  • Disconnect pacemaker before shock if feasible
  • Re-assess capture and sensing after defibrillation — thresholds may transiently rise
  • External generator has built-in defibrillation protection
Perform full pacemaker check (capture, sensing, battery, connections) after every patient transport, procedure, and defibrillation event.
