Indications for temporary pacing (external / transvenous / epicardial)
Bradyarrhythmias (Class I/IIa)
  • Hemodynamically unstable bradycardia unresponsive to atropine / chronotropic drugs
  • Complete (3rd degree) AV block with ventricular escape <45 bpm and hypotension
  • Mobitz II second-degree AV block with wide QRS or haemodynamic compromise
  • Alternating bundle branch block / bilateral BBB with syncope
  • Asystole >3 s or symptomatic sinus pauses
Transvenous pacing is preferred for sustained therapy; transcutaneous pacing is a bridge until transvenous/epicardial access.
Post-cardiac surgery & epicardial pacing
  • Transient conduction disturbances after valve surgery or coronary revascularisation
  • AV junctional bradycardia, accelerated idioventricular rhythm, or complete heart block
  • Supraventricular tachycardias (overdrive pacing)
  • Atrial pacing to restore AV synchrony in haemodynamically compromised junctional rhythm
  • Prophylactic backup pacing when epicardial wires are placed (most centres)
Capture thresholds often rise progressively after surgery. Epicardial wires are commonly removed within several days when no longer needed; threshold >10 mA by day 4–5 is not unusual.
Other indications & overdrive pacing
  • Overdrive pacing for supraventricular tachycardia (atrial flutter, AVNRT)
  • Suppression of drug-refractory VT (overdrive pacing via ventricular lead, rarely used)
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (LVOT gradient reduction via short AV delay)
  • Pause-dependent torsade de pointes (increase basal rate to 90-110 bpm)
Temporary pacing is not indicated for asystole refractory to CPR unless a reversible cause is identified.

Step-by-step: connection, threshold checks, daily verification
Initial set-up & capture threshold
  1. Connect atrial and ventricular leads to color-coded ports (red = ventricular, yellow/atrial).
  2. Set mode to DDD (or VVI for simple ventricular backup).
  3. Set rate 70-80 bpm.
  4. Set output to a high starting value (10–20 mA for ventricle, 10 mA for atrium) to ensure reliable capture.
  5. Confirm capture (pacing spike followed by wide QRS or P-wave) while observing ECG continuously.
  6. Gradually decrease output until capture is lost → record the capture threshold (mA). Starting high and titrating downward is safer and the standard approach.
  7. Set working output to at least 2× the capture threshold, or according to institutional practice; a fixed minimum of 5 mA is not universally required (e.g., fresh epicardial threshold 0.7 mA → 1.5–2 mA may suffice).
Typical capture thresholds: transvenous RV apex 0.5-1.5 mA; epicardial wires 1-5 mA; after 4 days may rise above 10 mA.
Sensitivity testing (avoid undersensing/oversensing)
  1. Set mode to VVI, AAI, or DDD (sensing-dependent mode).
  2. Set rate 10 bpm below patient's intrinsic rate to allow the intrinsic rhythm to emerge.
  3. If clinically safe (no significant pacing dependency), the pacing output may be temporarily reduced or the rate lowered further to facilitate sensing assessment. Do not reduce output in pacing-dependent patients or complete AV block.
  4. Increase sensitivity (turn sensitivity dial counter-clockwise – higher mV) until the sense indicator stops flashing -> point of undersensing.
  5. Now increase sensitivity (clockwise, lower mV) until sense indicator flashes with each intrinsic beat -> this is the sensitivity threshold.
  6. Set sensitivity to half the threshold value (e.g., threshold 2 mV -> set 1 mV).
Oversensing (sensitivity too high) causes inappropriate inhibition of pacing — potentially catastrophic in pacing-dependent patients — as well as competitive asynchronous pacing and inappropriate mode switches. Undersensing leads to asynchronous pacing (risk of R-on-T).
Daily checks & documentation
  • Check underlying rhythm -> decrease rate (do NOT turn output down).
  • Verify capture threshold and output margin (still 2x threshold).
  • Verify sensing threshold (sensitivity margin: half of measured threshold).
  • Inspect lead connections, battery level indicator (9 V, replace weekly for alkaline).
  • Check AV delay and refractory settings (especially in DDD mode).
  • Document pacing parameters and any asynchrony (e.g., fusion, pseudofusion).
Failure to perform daily threshold checks is a common cause of unrecognised loss of capture.
Clinical use per scenario (how to choose mode & settings)
Sick sinus syndrome (preserved AV conduction)
  • Mode: AAI or DDD (if AV conduction is borderline).
  • Rate: 60-80 bpm.
  • Output: Atrial 2x threshold (usually 5-8 mA).
  • Sensitivity: Atrial 0.5-1.5 mV (half of sensed P-wave).
  • AV delay: Long (200 ms) to allow intrinsic conduction.
Complete heart block (AV dissociation)
  • Mode: DDD (preferred for AV synchrony) or VVI (simple backup).
  • Rate: 70-90 bpm, adjust to haemodynamic stability.
  • AV delay: 120-180 ms (short enough to capture ventricle before intrinsic escape).
  • Upper rate: 110-120 bpm to avoid 2:1 block.
  • Check for pacemaker-mediated tachycardia (PMT) – if occurs, extend PVARP or switch to DDI.
Post-cardiac surgery (epicardial leads)
  • Mode: DDD or DDI (AV sequential pacing).
  • Rate: 80 bpm initially, then wean as tolerated.
  • AV delay: 150 ms (auto mode often works).
  • Output: Start 10 mA and test capture; final = 2x threshold.
  • Atrial output limit: 10 mA maximum (box limit).
  • If failure of capture after day 4, consider replacing leads or switching to transvenous system.
Overdrive pacing for atrial flutter / SVT
  • Mode: AOO (asynchronous atrial) via Menu #3 for burst pacing (80-800 min−1).
  • Set burst rate 10-30% above intrinsic atrial rate.
  • Deliver burst for 5-15 sec, observe ECG for termination.
  • Caution: Risk of rapid ventricular response or induction of AF. Have defibrillator ready.
Rapid atrial pacing may induce atrial fibrillation or ventricular arrhythmia. Ensure defibrillation is immediately available.

AV synchrony — why it matters (especially post-cardiac surgery)
Physiological importance
  • Atrial contraction contributes up to 20–30% of ventricular filling ("atrial kick") — critical in patients with diastolic dysfunction or reduced compliance.
  • Loss of AV synchrony (e.g., VVI pacing) reduces cardiac output by 15–25% in some patients.
  • After cardiac surgery, AV synchrony is especially important: the heart is often stiff (ischaemia-reperfusion, cardioplegia), relying more heavily on atrial filling.
  • Junctional rhythm after cardiac surgery is common; atrial pacing at a rate slightly above junctional rate restores AV synchrony without ventricular pacing.
DDD vs VVI — clinical decision
  • DDD preferred when: intact sinus node, AV block, haemodynamic compromise, post-cardiac surgery with epicardial wires available.
  • VVI acceptable when: ventricular backup only needed, atrial lead not available, or atrial arrhythmias present.
  • Post-AVR or post-CABG with complete heart block: DDD is strongly preferred to maintain AV synchrony and optimise output.
Optimise AV delay (typically 150–180 ms) to maximise ventricular filling time. Too short: truncates filling; too long: intrinsic conduction takes over, may cause fusion.
Electrical capture vs mechanical capture — a critical distinction
Electrical capture
  • Pacing spike followed by a wide QRS complex (ventricular) or P-wave (atrial) on ECG.
  • Confirms that the myocardium has been depolarised electrically.
  • Does not guarantee effective mechanical contraction or cardiac output.
Mechanical capture
  • Actual myocardial contraction producing cardiac output.
  • Must be confirmed separately — especially in haemodynamically unstable patients.
  • Confirm via: palpable pulse, arterial line waveform synchronous with pacing, Doppler NIBP, or point-of-care echocardiography.
⚠️ Do not assume that a wide QRS after each pacing spike means the patient is being effectively resuscitated. In pulseless electrical activity (PEA), electrical capture may be present without mechanical output. Always verify mechanical capture by pulse or arterial waveform in any haemodynamically unstable patient.

Special clinical situations (paediatrics, MRI, electrocautery, IABP)
Paediatric pacing (Medtronic 5388)
  • Same device, but use smaller transvenous leads (4 Fr or 5 Fr) or epicardial wires.
  • Higher basal rate: neonate 120-150 bpm, infant 100-140 bpm, child 80-100 bpm.
  • Lower output threshold often sufficient (start 2 mA for ventricle).
  • Sensitivity should be carefully adjusted (avoid oversensing of high-amplitude paediatric QRS).
  • Use VVI or DDD if AV sequential pacing required for cardiac output.
During surgery / electrocautery (EMI)
  • Electrocautery may cause oversensing (inhibition) or trigger asynchronous pacing.
  • Switch to VOO or DOO mode (asynchronous) before electrocautery use.
  • Alternatively, manually switch the generator to DOO (asynchronous) mode. Note: magnet behaviour is device-dependent and not universally applicable to external temporary generators — manual mode selection is preferred.
  • After electrocautery, revert to demand mode and re-assess capture and sensing.
DOO mode prevents inhibition but carries risk of R-on-T. Use only when absolutely required.
IABP interaction & MRI safety
  • IABP can misinterpret pacing spikes as QRS complexes -> trigger counter-pulsation at wrong timing.
  • Solution: Set IABP to trigger from arterial pressure waveform or enable high-frequency filter (bypasses pacing artifacts).
  • MRI unsafe – Medtronic 5388 contains ferromagnetic material; never enter MRI suite with generator attached.
  • Epicardial wires in situ are also MRI-unsafe; retrieve or cut close to skin before MRI.
Emergency asynchronous pacing (DOO) and high-rate burst (RAP)
Emergency key (DOO mode)
  • Press EMERGENCY key -> immediately switches to DOO (asynchronous dual-chamber) at rate of 80 bpm with high output (20 mA).
  • Use when capture is lost or in profound bradycardia / asystole.
  • DOO mode ignores sensing; therefore risk of R-on-T (ventricular fibrillation). Use only in emergency.
  • To exit DOO, press the key again or select another mode.
Rapid Atrial Pacing (RAP) – burst mode
  • Access through Menu #3 (RAP menu).
  • Rate adjustable from 80-800 min−1 in AOO or DDD.
  • Primarily used for termination of atrial flutter / SVT (overdrive pacing).
  • Safety measures: Ensure defibrillator is nearby, monitor for rapid ventricular response.
  • Deliver burst for 5-15 s, then re-evaluate rhythm.
Battery indicator: When low battery symbol appears, replace 9 V battery immediately (alkaline: 7-9 days, lithium: 16 days). Always use fresh battery for each new patient.
Transcutaneous pacing — bridge to transvenous/epicardial access
Technique & setup
  • Place pads in anterior-posterior or anterior-lateral position. Anterior-posterior preferred for better capture thresholds.
  • Start output at 40–80 mA; increase by 10 mA increments until electrical capture (wide QRS on ECG).
  • Typical capture: 50–100 mA; some patients require up to 200 mA.
  • Set rate to 60–80 bpm for bradycardia, 90–110 bpm for torsades.
Key clinical points
  • Always confirm mechanical capture by palpating pulse or arterial waveform — chest wall muscle contractions can mimic QRS complexes on ECG.
  • Provide analgesia and sedation (e.g., morphine 1–2 mg IV + midazolam 1–2 mg IV) as transcutaneous pacing is painful.
  • Transcutaneous pacing is a temporising measure only; arrange definitive transvenous or epicardial pacing promptly.
  • Ineffective in patients with hypothermia (core temp <30°C) — may require rewarming before capture is achievable.
Skeletal muscle contractions during transcutaneous pacing can obscure ECG and mimic capture. Always confirm mechanical capture by pulse or arterial line — not ECG alone.
Post-cardiac surgery pacing & temporary pacing in torsades de pointes
Post-cardiac surgery (CABG, valve, TAVI)
  • AV block after AVR: Common after aortic valve surgery due to proximity to the conduction system; DDD pacing restores AV synchrony and cardiac output.
  • Junctional rhythm: Atrial pacing at 80–90 bpm (just above junctional rate) restores AV synchrony and significantly improves haemodynamics post-cardiopulmonary bypass.
  • After TAVI: New left bundle branch block or complete AV block occurs in up to 25% of cases. Temporary transvenous pacing until recovery or permanent pacemaker implantation.
  • Epicardial wire removal: Remove with steady continuous traction — do not pull if significant resistance felt (risk of laceration). Monitor for 2–4 hours after removal (risk of haemopericardium / tamponade). Have pericardiocentesis equipment available.
Temporary pacing for torsades de pointes
  • Increase pacing rate to 90–110 bpm (ventricular) to shorten QT interval and suppress pause-dependent torsades.
  • Mode: VVI or DDD depending on AV status.
  • Simultaneously treat underlying causes:
    • Correct hypomagnesaemia (IV magnesium sulphate 2 g over 10–15 min).
    • Correct hypokalaemia (target K⁺ ≥4.5 mmol/L).
    • Withdraw or replace all QT-prolonging drugs (anti-arrhythmics, antibiotics, antiemetics, antipsychotics).
  • Pacing is a bridge — not a cure. Address the underlying QT abnormality.
Safety, microshock precautions, and daily surveillance
Microshock prevention (electrical safety)
  • Patients with temporary pacemakers are at risk of microshock (ventricular fibrillation from stray currents).
  • Use only patient-isolated pacing cables (Medtronic 5433A, M5433V).
  • All electrical equipment (ECG, monitors, bed) must share common ground.
  • Wires should be covered with non-conductive caps (e.g., syringe barrel) when not connected.
  • Touch the patient's bed frame before handling exposed pacing terminals to discharge static.
Defibrillation with pacemaker in situ
  • Defibrillation is safe but requires precautions:
  • Place paddles/pads at least 15 cm away from pacemaker generator and leads.
  • Disconnect the pacemaker from the patient before shock (if feasible).
  • If disconnected, re-assess capture and thresholds after defibrillation.
  • Medtronic 5388 has built-in defibrillation protection (device will not be damaged by external shock).
Perform a full pacemaker check (capture, sensing, battery, connections) after each patient transport, procedure, and defibrillation event.
