⚡ Indications & Pacing Modes

When to pace, which mode to choose, and how to read the NBG code.

Indications for temporary pacing

🚨 Emergency indications

  • Haemodynamically unstable bradycardia unresponsive to atropine / chronotropic drugs
  • Complete AV block with ventricular escape <45 bpm and hypotension
  • Asystole >3 s or symptomatic sinus pauses
  • Mobitz II with wide QRS or haemodynamic compromise
  • Alternating BBB / bilateral BBB with syncope
Note: Temporary pacing is not indicated for asystole refractory to CPR unless a reversible cause is identified.

🏥 Post-cardiac surgery

  • Transient conduction disturbances after valve surgery or CABG
  • AV junctional bradycardia, accelerated idioventricular rhythm, complete heart block
  • Junctional rhythm — atrial pacing restores AV synchrony and cardiac output
  • Supraventricular tachycardias (overdrive pacing)
  • Prophylactic backup via epicardial wires (most centres)
  • New LBBB / AV block after TAVI (up to 25% of cases)
Epicardial wires: Capture thresholds often rise progressively after surgery — commonly removed within several days when no longer needed. Threshold >10 mA by day 4–5 is not unusual.

🎯 Other indications

  • Overdrive pacing: Atrial flutter, AVNRT (AOO mode, up to 800 min⁻¹)
  • Pause-dependent torsade de pointes — increase rate to 90–110 bpm to shorten QT and suppress TdP
  • HCM: Short AV delay for LVOT gradient reduction (historical; specialist guidance required)
  • VT suppression: Overdrive via ventricular lead (rarely used)

Initial setup — capture threshold determination
Step-by-step — Capture Threshold
  1. Connect leads to colour-coded ports (red = ventricular, yellow = atrial).
  2. Set mode DDD or VVI. Rate 70–80 bpm.
  3. Set output to a high starting value (10–20 mA ventricular, 10 mA atrial) to ensure reliable capture from the outset.
  4. Confirm capture: pacing spike followed by wide QRS (ventricular) or P-wave (atrial) on ECG.
  5. Gradually decrease output until capture is lost → record capture threshold (mA). Titrating down from a high value is the standard, safer approach.
  6. Set working output to ≥2× capture threshold, per institutional practice.
Typical thresholds: Transvenous RV apex 0.5–1.5 mA · Epicardial wires 1–5 mA · May exceed 10 mA after day 4–5.
setup
Step-by-step — Sensitivity Testing
  1. Set rate 10 bpm below intrinsic rate to allow intrinsic beats to emerge.
  2. If clinically safe (no pacing dependency), output may be temporarily reduced to facilitate sensing assessment. Never do this in complete AV block or pacing-dependent patients.
  3. Increase sensitivity threshold (counter-clockwise / higher mV) until sense indicator stops flashing → undersensing point.
  4. Increase sensitivity (clockwise / lower mV) until indicator flashes with every intrinsic beat → sensing threshold.
  5. Set final sensitivity to ½ the threshold value (e.g., 2 mV threshold → set 1 mV).
Oversensing causes inappropriate inhibition — catastrophic in pacing-dependent patients. Undersensing causes asynchronous pacing and R-on-T risk.
Daily checks & documentation

📋 Daily pacemaker check protocol

  • Check underlying rhythm by lowering rate (do NOT turn output down)
  • Verify capture threshold — confirm output still ≥2× threshold
  • Verify sensing threshold — confirm margin is ½ of threshold
  • Inspect lead connections (intact, well-secured)
  • Check battery level (9 V; replace weekly for alkaline, every 16 days for lithium)
  • Document AV delay, PVARP, any fusion or asynchrony
  • Perform full check after every transport, procedure, and defibrillation event
  • Perform check after any lead repositioning
Failure to perform daily threshold checks is the most common cause of unrecognised loss of capture.

🫀 AV Synchrony & Capture

The physiology of AV synchrony, and the critical distinction between electrical and mechanical capture.

Why AV synchrony matters — especially post-cardiac surgery
Physiological Importance
  • Atrial kick: Atrial contraction contributes 20–30% of ventricular filling — critical in diastolic dysfunction or reduced compliance.
  • Cardiac output: Loss of AV synchrony (e.g., VVI pacing) reduces cardiac output by 15–25% in some patients.
  • Post-surgery: After cardiac surgery the heart is stiff (ischaemia-reperfusion, cardioplegia) — reliance on atrial filling is increased.
  • Junctional rhythm: Very common post-surgery; atrial pacing just above junctional rate restores AV synchrony without ventricular pacing and significantly improves haemodynamics.
AV sync
DDD vs VVI — Clinical Decision
  • DDD preferred: Intact sinus node, AV block, haemodynamic compromise, post-cardiac surgery with epicardial wires available, post-AVR or post-CABG with complete heart block.
  • VVI acceptable: Ventricular backup only needed, no atrial lead available, or atrial arrhythmias present.
Optimise AV delay to 150–180 ms. Too short: truncates atrial filling. Too long: intrinsic conduction overtakes and fusion or pseudofusion occurs.
After TAVI: New LBBB or complete AV block in up to 25% of cases → temporary transvenous pacing until recovery or permanent pacemaker implantation.
Electrical capture vs mechanical capture — a critical distinction

⚠️ The most important concept in pacing management

⚡ Electrical capture

  • Pacing spike followed by wide QRS (ventricular) or P-wave (atrial) on ECG
  • Confirms myocardial depolarisation has occurred
  • Does NOT guarantee effective contraction or cardiac output

💓 Mechanical capture

  • Actual myocardial contraction producing cardiac output
  • Must be confirmed separately in haemodynamically unstable patients
  • Confirm via: palpable pulse, arterial line waveform, Doppler NIBP, or point-of-care echo
⚠️ In PEA, electrical capture may be present without mechanical output. A wide QRS on the monitor is not sufficient — always verify mechanical capture by pulse or arterial waveform in any haemodynamically unstable patient.
Transcutaneous pacing — bridge to definitive access
Technique & Setup
  • Pad placement: Anterior-posterior preferred (lower capture threshold); anterior-lateral acceptable
  • Start at 40–80 mA; increase by 10 mA until wide QRS on ECG
  • Typical capture: 50–100 mA; may require up to 200 mA
  • Rate: 60–80 bpm for bradycardia; 90–110 bpm for torsades
  • May be ineffective in severe hypothermia (<30°C) — rewarming may be required
TCP
Key Clinical Points
  • Always confirm mechanical capture by pulse or arterial waveform — chest wall muscle contractions can mimic QRS on ECG
  • Provide analgesia and sedation (morphine 1–2 mg + midazolam 1–2 mg IV) — transcutaneous pacing is painful
  • Transcutaneous pacing is a temporising measure — arrange definitive pacing promptly
Skeletal muscle contractions during transcutaneous pacing can mimic QRS complexes and falsely suggest capture. Confirm mechanical capture by pulse or arterial line — not ECG alone.

Clinical use per scenario

🫀 Sick sinus syndrome (intact AV conduction)

  • Mode: AAI or DDD (if AV conduction borderline)
  • Rate: 60–80 bpm
  • Atrial output: ≥2× threshold (usually 5–8 mA)
  • Atrial sensitivity: 0.5–1.5 mV (½ of sensed P-wave)
  • AV delay: long (200 ms) to allow intrinsic ventricular conduction

🫀 Complete heart block (AV dissociation)

  • Mode: DDD (preferred) or VVI (simple backup)
  • Rate: 70–90 bpm; adjust to haemodynamic stability
  • AV delay: 120–180 ms (capture ventricle before escape)
  • Upper rate: 110–120 bpm (prevent 2:1 block)
  • PMT → extend PVARP to 350 ms or switch to DDI

🏥 Post-cardiac surgery (epicardial leads)

  • Mode: DDD or DDI (AV sequential pacing)
  • Rate: 80 bpm initially, then wean as tolerated
  • AV delay: 150 ms (auto mode often suitable)
  • Output: start 10 mA; final ≥2× threshold; atrial maximum 10 mA

⚡ Overdrive pacing — atrial flutter / SVT

  • Mode: AOO via Menu #3 (burst 80–800 min⁻¹)
  • Set burst rate 10–30% above intrinsic atrial rate
  • Deliver burst 5–15 s; re-evaluate rhythm
Risk of rapid ventricular response or AF induction. Defibrillator must be immediately available.

🏥 Special Cases, Emergency Pacing & Safety

Post-cardiac surgery, torsades, emergency DOO mode, microshock prevention, and defibrillation.

Post-cardiac surgery — specific situations
AV Block, Junctional Rhythm, TAVI
  • AV block after AVR: Common due to proximity of conduction system to the aortic annulus. DDD pacing restores AV synchrony and cardiac output.
  • Junctional rhythm: Atrial pacing at 80–90 bpm (just above junctional rate) restores AV synchrony and significantly improves haemodynamics post-bypass.
  • After TAVI: New LBBB or complete AV block in up to 25% of cases — temporary transvenous pacing until recovery or permanent pacemaker.
post-op
Epicardial Wire Removal
  • Apply steady continuous traction — stop if significant resistance (risk of laceration or haemopericardium)
  • Observe for 2–4 hours post-removal for signs of tamponade (hypotension, rising JVP, muffled heart sounds)
  • Have pericardiocentesis equipment immediately available
  • Confirm with echo if haemodynamic instability occurs after removal
Risk of cardiac tamponade after epicardial wire removal is low but potentially fatal. Never pull forcefully against resistance.
Temporary pacing for torsades de pointes (TdP)

⚡ Pause-dependent TdP — pacing protocol

Pacing strategy

  • Increase pacing rate to 90–110 bpm (ventricular) to shorten QT and suppress pause-dependent TdP
  • Mode: VVI or DDD depending on AV status
  • Pacing is a bridge — not a cure; address the underlying QT abnormality

Simultaneously treat underlying causes

  • Hypomagnesaemia: IV magnesium sulphate 2 g over 10–15 min
  • Hypokalaemia: Target K⁺ ≥4.5 mmol/L
  • QT-prolonging drugs: Withdraw or replace all offending agents (anti-arrhythmics, antibiotics, antiemetics, antipsychotics)
Emergency asynchronous pacing (DOO) & rapid atrial pacing (RAP)
Emergency Key — DOO Mode
  • Press EMERGENCY key → immediately switches to DOO at 80 bpm / 20 mA
  • Use when capture is lost or in profound bradycardia / asystole
  • DOO ignores all sensing → R-on-T risk (VF). Use only in genuine emergency.
  • To exit DOO: press key again or select another mode
  • Always confirm mechanical capture by pulse or arterial waveform after activating
DOO is not an asynchronous mode for routine bradycardia. It is an emergency mode. R-on-T can induce VF. Use only when sensing is entirely unreliable.
emerg
RAP — Rapid Atrial Pacing
  • Access via Menu #3 (RAP menu)
  • Rate adjustable 80–800 min⁻¹ in AOO or DDD mode
  • Overdrive pacing for atrial flutter / SVT termination
  • Deliver burst 5–15 s; then re-evaluate rhythm
  • Defibrillator must be immediately available — risk of rapid ventricular response or AF induction
Battery: Replace 9 V when low-battery indicator appears (alkaline: 7–9 days; lithium: 16 days). Always use a fresh battery for each new patient.
Special situations

👶 Paediatric pacing

  • Smaller leads (4–5 Fr) or epicardial wires
  • Higher basal rates: neonate 120–150, infant 100–140, child 80–100 bpm
  • Lower output threshold — start 2 mA ventricular
  • Adjust sensitivity carefully — avoid oversensing of high-amplitude paediatric QRS

⚡ Electrocautery / EMI

  • Switch to VOO or DOO before electrocautery
  • Manual mode selection preferred — magnet behaviour is device-dependent and unreliable in external temporary generators
  • Revert to demand mode and re-assess capture/sensing after electrocautery

🏥 IABP & MRI safety

  • IABP may misinterpret pacing spikes as QRS → switch to arterial pressure trigger
  • External generators and epicardial wires: MRI-unsafe
  • Cut epicardial wires close to skin before MRI if removal is not possible
Safety & microshock prevention

⚠️ Microshock prevention & defibrillation

Microshock prevention

  • Patients with intracardiac leads risk VF from currents as low as 10 μA delivered directly to the myocardium
  • Use only patient-isolated pacing cables
  • All electrical equipment must share common ground
  • Cover exposed wire terminals with non-conductive caps when not connected
  • Discharge static before handling terminals (touch bed frame first)

Defibrillation with pacemaker in situ

  • Place paddles / pads ≥15 cm from generator and leads
  • Disconnect pacemaker before shock if feasible
  • Re-assess capture and sensing after defibrillation — thresholds may transiently rise
  • External generator has built-in defibrillation protection
