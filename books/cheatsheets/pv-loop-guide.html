⟳ PV Loop Atlas Complete Cardiac Physiology Reference

🫀 Normal Pressure–Volume Loop

The PV loop is the fundamental graphical description of left ventricular mechanics during a single cardiac cycle, relating instantaneous chamber pressure to instantaneous chamber volume.

📐 Annotated Anatomy of the Normal LV PV Loop
Stroke Work (SW) = area enclosed by the PV loop = external work performed by the LV each beat. Normal ≈ 8–10 J. The larger the area, the more energy expended per cycle.
Four Phases of the Cardiac Cycle
①

Isovolumetric Contraction

Both mitral and aortic valves are closed. Pressure rises rapidly with no change in volume. On the PV loop: right vertical limb (A→B).

MV: closed · AoV: closed
②

Rapid Ejection (Systole)

Aortic valve opens when LV pressure exceeds aortic diastolic pressure. Volume falls, pressure peaks at ESP. On loop: top horizontal (B→C), endpoint on ESPVR.

MV: closed · AoV: open
③

Isovolumetric Relaxation

Aortic valve closes at ESP. LV pressure drops rapidly with no change in volume. Mitral valve not yet open. On loop: left vertical limb (C→D).

MV: closed · AoV: closed
④

Diastolic Filling

Mitral valve opens when LV pressure falls below LA pressure. Volume rises along the EDPVR at low pressure. Ends at EDV. On loop: bottom (D→A).

MV: open · AoV: closed
📏 ESPVR — End-Systolic Pressure-Volume Relation

A straight line connecting all end-systolic points at varying loads. Its slope Ees (end-systolic elastance) is the gold-standard index of intrinsic contractility, load-independent.

Ees = ESP / (ESV − V₀) [mmHg/mL]
  • Normal Ees ≈ 1.5–2.5 mmHg/mL
  • ↓ Ees = reduced contractility (HFrEF, DCM)
  • ↑ Ees = hyperdynamic (catecholamines, HCM)
📏 EDPVR — End-Diastolic Pressure-Volume Relation

An exponential curve reflecting LV diastolic compliance (passive stiffness). Its steepness determines how filling pressure rises with volume.

  • Normal: flat at low volumes, steeper at high volumes
  • Shifted left/up = diastolic dysfunction, hypertrophy, fibrosis
  • Shifted right/down = dilated LV, eccentric hypertrophy

⚡ Ventriculo-Arterial Coupling

The ratio Ea/Ees describes efficiency of energy transfer from LV to arterial circulation.

  • Ea/Ees ≈ 1: Optimal mechanical efficiency (normal)
  • Ea/Ees > 1.5: Uncoupling — excess afterload or reduced contractility → energy waste, reduced SW
  • Ea/Ees < 0.5: Hyperdynamic — inotropes, sepsis, thyrotoxicosis
Coupling ratio = Ea / Ees

📈 Preload

Defined as LV end-diastolic wall stress (clinically approximated by EDV or EDP). Governed by Frank-Starling Law: within physiologic limits, ↑ preload → ↑ SV via increased cross-bridge formation.

  • Determinants: venous return, blood volume, heart rate (filling time), atrial contraction
  • Loop effect: right shift, SV ↑, EF unchanged if contractility/afterload normal
  • Clinical index: CVP, PCWP, E/e' ratio on echo
Frank-Starling: SV ∝ LVEDV (up to optimal preload)
🩸 Afterload

The wall stress the LV must overcome during ejection. Best described by Ea (effective arterial elastance) = ESP/SV. Clinically: SVR, aortic impedance, valve stenosis.

  • ↑ Afterload: ↑ESV, ↓SV, ↑MVO₂ — loop rises, narrows
  • LV compensation: concentric hypertrophy (↑wall thickness, ↓radius, normalizes wall stress by Laplace)
  • Laplace Law: σ = P × r / (2h)
Ea = ESP / SV [mmHg/mL] Normal ≈ 2 mmHg/mL
💪 Contractility (Inotropy)

The intrinsic force-generating capacity of the myocardium, independent of load. Best measured by Ees (ESPVR slope) or Preload Recruitable Stroke Work (PRSW).

  • ↑ Inotropy: catecholamines, Ca²⁺ sensitizers, cardiac glycosides
  • ↓ Inotropy: ischemia, acidosis, β-blockers, negative inotropes
  • Mechanism: increased [Ca²⁺]i → more cross-bridges, faster cycling
Ees = (ESP − P₀) / (ESV − V₀) Normal ≈ 1.5–2.5 mmHg/mL
🫀 Frank-Starling Mechanism

The intrinsic ability of the heart to adapt its SV to changes in venous return, without neural or humoral input. Operates via:

  • Sarcomere length: optimal overlap at ~2.2 µm
  • Ca²⁺ sensitivity: stretch increases myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity (titin-based)
  • Clinical limit: in HFrEF, Starling curve is flat and depressed — no reserve
The PV loop rightward shift with ↑ preload reflects Frank-Starling: more filling → more SV, until the curve flattens (descending limb).

❤️ Cardiomyopathies

Distinct structural and functional alterations of the myocardium produce characteristic PV loop deformations — each reflecting specific systolic and/or diastolic impairments.

📍 Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM)
Systolic dysfunction
↓ Contractility
Eccentric hypertrophy

Myocyte loss, fibrosis, and reduced inotropy lead to LV dilation and global hypokinesis.

  • ESPVR: flattened and shifted right (↓Ees)
  • EDV/ESV: both markedly ↑↑
  • EF: ↓↓ (<40%, often <25% in severe)
  • EDPVR: shifted right (dilated, compliant LV)
  • Loop: wide, rightward, low and flat
  • Causes: ischemic, viral myocarditis, alcohol, idiopathic, LBBB (LMNA, TTN mutations)
📍 Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)
Diastolic dysfunction
Preserved EF (↑)
LVOTO (obstructive variant)

Asymmetric LV hypertrophy (usually septal) → small cavity, impaired relaxation, ± dynamic LVOTO.

  • ESPVR: steep (↑↑Ees — hyperdynamic)
  • EDV: ↓ (small, stiff LV cavity)
  • EDPVR: shifted up/left (poor compliance, ↑↑EDP)
  • EF: ↑↑ (often 70–80%, hypercontractile)
  • HCM-obstructive: dynamic gradient ↑ with Valsalva, dehydration, tachycardia; ↓ with β-blockers
📍 Restrictive Cardiomyopathy (RCM)
Severe diastolic dysfunction
Normal EF
↑↑ EDP

Myocardial infiltration (amyloid, hemochromatosis, sarcoid) → rigid, non-compliant LV. Rapid early filling with abrupt pressure rise.

  • EDPVR: steep — small volume → ↑↑EDP. "Square root" sign on Cath
  • EDV: normal or ↓
  • EF: often preserved (≥50%)
  • Amyloid: worse prognosis; ESPVR also affected in late disease
  • Echo: E/A ≫2.0, short DT, E/e' ≫15

Mainly RV
Fatty fibrous replacement

Fibro-fatty replacement of RV myocardium → RV dilation, aneurysms, arrhythmias. LV PV loop may be relatively preserved in early disease, but biventricular involvement occurs in ~10%.

  • LV loop: often normal until advanced disease
  • RV dysfunction: ↑RV volume, reduced RV EF, paradoxical septal motion
  • High risk: exercise-induced VT/VF in young athletes — SCN5A, DSP, PKP2 mutations
  • Diagnosis: Task Force Criteria (CMR, ECG, genetics)

💎 Clinical Pearls & Applied Physiology

High-yield concepts and clinical correlations that connect PV loop theory to bedside practice.

💡 EF is Load-Dependent

EF is not a pure contractility index. It changes with loading conditions independent of inotropy:

  • ↑ Preload → ↑ SV → ↑ EF (without change in Ees)
  • ↓ Afterload (MR) → ↑ EF despite impaired contractility
  • ↑ Afterload (AS, HTN) → ↓ EF without change in Ees
  • True contractility = Ees (load-independent)
💡 Starling Curve in HFrEF

In systolic HF, the Frank-Starling curve is depressed and flat:

  • Compensatory ↑ EDV moves the heart up a flattened curve — small SV gain
  • Further ↑ preload → congestion without SV benefit
  • Diuretics reduce EDV → relief of congestion, small SV reduction (often tolerated)
  • Inotropes shift curve up — restore reserve
💡 Cardiac Output in Shock

PV loop framework predicts haemodynamic patterns in different shock types:

  • Cardiogenic: ↓Ees, ↑EDV, ↑EDP, ↓SV — loop right-shifted, narrow, high EDP
  • Distributive (sepsis): ↓Ea (vasodilation), ↑SV, ↓ESP — wide loop, low ESP
  • Hypovolaemic: ↓EDV — loop shifts left, narrow
  • Obstructive (PE/tamponade): ↓EDV, ↑RV afterload — LV loop small, underfilled
💡 Cardiac Tamponade

Pericardial fluid → ↑ intrapericardial pressure → ↑ chamber-filling pressure → ↓ EDV:

  • Both RV and LV loops shrink (↓ EDV)
  • Ventricular interdependence: RV/LV filling compete within fixed pericardial space
  • Inspiration → ↑ RV filling → septal shift left → ↓ LV EDV → pulsus paradoxus
  • Physiology = opposite to constrictive pericarditis (equalization of pressures in both)
💡 Laplace Law & LV Hypertrophy
Wall stress σ = (P × r) / (2h)

LV responds to chronically ↑ wall stress by hypertrophy:

  • Concentric (pressure overload — AS, HTN): ↑h relative to r → normalizes σ at high P
  • Eccentric (volume overload — AR, MR): ↑r proportional ↑h → normalized σ despite large volume
  • Both → diastolic dysfunction, ↑ MVO₂, ischemia risk
💡 IABP and the PV Loop

Intra-aortic balloon pump (counterpulsation) produces characteristic dual effects on the PV loop:

  • Diastolic augmentation: balloon inflates → ↑ diastolic aortic P → ↑ coronary perfusion
  • Systolic unloading: balloon deflates before systole → ↓ afterload (Ea ↓) → ↓ ESP → ↓ MVO₂
  • Net effect: loop narrows, shifts left — ↓ SW (less cardiac work) + ↑ coronary supply
  • Contraindicated: severe AR (worsens regurgitation)
💡 Mechanical Circulatory Support

LVADs and Impella further modify the PV loop:

  • LVAD: direct LV volume unloading → markedly ↓ EDV, loop shrinks dramatically. Effective afterload ↓
  • Impella: continuous axial pump from LV to aorta → ↓ LV EDV and ESP → ↓ MVO₂ → myocardial recovery
  • ECMO (VA): ↑ afterload (Ea ↑) → ↑ LV work if LV doesn't decompress — may need venting!
💡 Oxygen Consumption & PV Loop
MVO₂ ∝ Heart Rate × Peak LV Wall Stress

Myocardial oxygen demand correlates with:

  • Stroke work (SW) = area of PV loop ≈ external work
  • Potential energy (PE) = area to the left of ESPVR above loop
  • Total mechanical energy (PVA) = SW + PE ∝ MVO₂
  • ↑ Afterload → ↑ PE without ↑ SW → ↑ MVO₂ inefficiently
  • Inotropes → ↑ SW, modest ↑ PE — more efficient than afterload
PVA = SW + PE = total mechanical energy per beat

🫁 Right Ventricular Physiology & PV Loop

The RV operates under fundamentally different mechanical constraints than the LV. Its PV loop has a distinctive triangular morphology reflecting its unique afterload sensitivity.

📐 RV vs LV PV Loop Morphology

The RV loop is triangular (vs LV rectangular) because the RV lacks a true isovolumetric contraction phase — it begins ejecting while still generating pressure, and the low-impedance pulmonary circuit allows ejection to continue until late in relaxation.

⚠️ Acute RV Failure — PV Loop

The RV is exquisitely sensitive to acute increases in afterload (pulmonary vascular resistance). Unlike the LV, it cannot sustain high pressure chronically without decompensation.

  • Acute PE: sudden ↑ PVR → RV loop becomes rectangular (failed ejection), ↑RV EDP, ↓SV
  • RV dilation: septal shift leftward (D-sign on echo) → ↓LV EDV → ↓CO (ventricular interdependence)
  • RV ischemia: RCA territory — ST elevation III>II, V4R; RV wall motion abnormality
  • Treatment: optimize RV preload (cautious fluids), ↓PVR (NO, prostacyclins, PDE5i), RV inotropes (dobutamine, levosimendan), avoid ↑ afterload
RV failure after cardiac surgery is a major cause of post-op mortality. LCOS, high CVP, low CO, ↑TR — PV loop shifts: ↑RV EDV, ↓SV, rectangular loop (failing to eject against elevated PVR post-CPB).
🫀 Ventricular Interdependence

The two ventricles share the pericardium, septum, and myocardial fibers. Changes in one directly affect the other:

  • RV dilation → LV impairment: septal shift left → ↓LV compliance → ↓LV EDV → ↓CO
  • LV hypertrophy → RV restriction: shared septum stiffens RV cavity
  • Tamponade: classic interdependence — inspiration ↑RV at expense of LV
  • Pericardial constraint: both loops shrink when intrapericardial P rises
Clinical implication: Treating isolated RV failure with aggressive fluid loading can worsen LV output via septal shift — haemodynamic monitoring essential.
🫁 Pulmonary Hypertension (PAH)

Chronic ↑ PVR → chronic RV pressure overload. RV PV loop progressively transforms:

  • Early: RV concentric hypertrophy, maintained coupling (Ea/Ees ≈1), loop tallens
  • Mid: RV dilation begins, Ees starts to plateau, Ea/Ees >1
  • Late: Severe uncoupling Ea/Ees >2 — loop wide, flat, large EDV, ↓SV → RV failure
mPAP > 20 mmHg = Pulmonary Hypertension (ESC 2022)

Groups: Group 1 (PAH), Group 2 (left heart disease — most common), Group 3 (lung disease), Group 4 (CTEPH), Group 5 (unclear/multifactorial)

📊 RV Afterload Sensitivity

The RV operates at a much lower Ea/Ees ratio at baseline, meaning it has less reserve against afterload increases:

  • Doubling PVR in a normal RV → ↓RV SV by ~40% (vs ~15% for LV with equivalent Ea increase)
  • RV Ees is 3–4× lower than LV Ees — less contractile reserve to recruit
  • This is why massive PE causes immediate cardiovascular collapse: RV cannot acutely hypertrophy
Key principle: The LV is a pressure-generating pump; the RV is a volume pump. The RV thrives in low-impedance circuits and fails rapidly when PVR rises acutely.

🏥 Perioperative Hemodynamics & PV Loop

Understanding PV loop mechanics is essential for the cardiac surgery team — from anesthetic induction to post-CPB management and ICU care.

💉 Anesthetic Induction Effects

❄️ Cardiopulmonary Bypass (CPB) Effects

During CPB, the native heart is arrested and the PV loop effectively disappears — the machine replaces cardiac function. Post-CPB management targets restoration of optimal loop mechanics:

  • Post-CPB stunning: ↓ Ees (transient contractile dysfunction from ischemia-reperfusion), ↑EDV — needs inotropic support
  • Systemic inflammatory response: ↓ SVR → ↓Ea → low ESP despite high CO — needs vasopressors
  • Volume status: CPB priming dilutes blood, fluid shifts — EDV may be low or high depending on management
  • Post-CPB RV failure: protamine, air embolism, surgical damage — ↑ PVR, ↓ RV Ees → acute RV loop collapse

🚨 Low Cardiac Output Syndrome (LCOS)

CI < 2.2 L/min/m² post-cardiac surgery. PV loop analysis directs treatment:

  • ↓ Ees dominant (systolic failure): ↑EDV, ↑ESV, flat loop → inotropes (dobutamine, milrinone, levosimendan), consider IABP/Impella
  • ↑ Ea dominant (vasoconstricton): high ESP, narrow loop, ↓SV → vasodilators (nitroprusside) if BP tolerates
  • ↓ EDV dominant (hypovolemia): small loop, low EDP → cautious fluid loading
  • RV failure component: ↑CVP, ↓PCWP, ↑RAP/PCWP ratio >0.63 → RV-targeted therapy
Treating LCOS requires identifying the primary loop deformity: giving inotropes to a hypovolemic patient or fluids to a patient with ↓ Ees will worsen outcomes. Goal-directed therapy based on PV loop principles.

🧊 Myocardial Protection & Cardioplegia — PV Loop Perspective

Cardioplegia arrests the heart in diastole — the PV loop goal on resuming cardiac activity is to restore optimal loop morphology with minimal ischemia-reperfusion injury.

Factors preserving post-arrest loop quality:
  • Adequate cardioplegia distribution (antegrade + retrograde)
  • Short cross-clamp time — minimizes ischemia
  • Tepid/warm blood cardioplegia — metabolic support
  • Controlled reperfusion — prevents calcium overload
  • Substrate enrichment (glutamate/aspartate) in severely impaired LV
Post-arrest stunning — PV loop effects:
  • ↓ Ees (transient — resolves in 24–48h with good protection)
  • ↑ EDV (compensatory dilation)
  • ↑ EDP (diastolic dysfunction, Ca²⁺ overload)
  • ↓ SV → LCOS risk in first 6–12h post-op

Q1. A patient has an EDV of 150 mL and ESV of 90 mL. What is the ejection fraction?

✅ A — 40%
EF = SV/EDV × 100 = (EDV−ESV)/EDV × 100 = (150−90)/150 × 100 = 60/150 × 100 = 40%. This is in the HFrEF range (<40%) — borderline, indicating significantly impaired systolic function.

Q2. On the PV loop, which phase is represented by the LEFT vertical limb (pressure dropping at constant volume)?

✅ C — Isovolumetric relaxation
After aortic valve closure (end-systole, at ESV), both valves are closed and pressure drops rapidly at constant volume. This is Phase 3 — the left vertical limb of the loop, descending from ESP to near zero.

Q3. A 58-year-old man has severe aortic regurgitation. His echo shows EF 68%, EDV 220 mL, ESV 70 mL. Which PV loop change is most characteristic?

✅ B — Wide rightward loop, large SV, low ESP
Aortic regurgitation causes volume overload: ↑↑EDV (220 mL), large total SV (150 mL), hyperdynamic EF, and low effective afterload (diastolic pressure low due to regurgitation). The loop shifts right and widens. Option D describes MR (absent IC). Option A describes AS (pressure overload). Option C describes HFpEF/MS.

Q4. Which of the following BEST represents a load-independent measure of myocardial contractility?

✅ B — End-systolic elastance (Ees)
Ees is the slope of the ESPVR — it defines the relationship between ESP and ESV across multiple loading conditions. It remains constant when preload or afterload changes (load-independent). EF, SV, and CO all change with loading conditions even if intrinsic inotropy is unchanged.

Q5. A patient with mitral regurgitation has EF 55%. Compared to a normal patient, what does this EF tell us?

✅ C — LV systolic function may already be significantly impaired
In MR, afterload is reduced (blood escapes into the low-pressure LA), which artificially elevates EF. An EF of 55% in MR indicates impaired contractility — equivalent to a much lower EF in a patient with normal valve function. ESC guidelines recommend surgery when EF falls to <60% in MR with symptoms, not waiting for <40%.

Q6. Dobutamine is administered to a patient in cardiogenic shock. How does this affect the ESPVR?

✅ B — ESPVR slope increases (Ees ↑), loop shifts left
Dobutamine is a positive inotrope (β1-agonist). It increases intracellular Ca²⁺ cycling, increasing contractility → steeper ESPVR (↑Ees). At a given preload/afterload, ESV falls and SV increases. The loop narrows and shifts leftward. This is distinct from afterload reduction (which changes Ea, not Ees).

Q7. In cardiac tamponade, what happens to the LV PV loop?

✅ B — Small narrow loop, ↓EDV
Tamponade impairs diastolic filling of all chambers due to elevated intrapericardial pressure. LV EDV falls, loop becomes small and leftward-shifted. SV and CO drop, triggering compensatory tachycardia. Classic pulsus paradoxus (≥10 mmHg inspiratory drop in SBP) reflects respiratory variation in LV filling as RV/LV compete within the non-distensible pericardial space.

Q8. Which cardiomyopathy classically produces a STEEP ESPVR (high Ees) with SMALL EDV and very high EDP?

✅ B — Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM)
HCM produces massive concentric hypertrophy → small LV cavity (↓EDV), hyperdynamic systolic function (↑EF, ↑Ees, steep ESPVR), but severe diastolic dysfunction (↑↑EDP, stiff non-compliant wall). EDPVR shifts steeply leftward. DCM is the opposite (flat Ees, ↑↑EDV). ARVC primarily involves the RV.

Q9. An IABP (intra-aortic balloon pump) is placed in a patient with cardiogenic shock. What is the HEMODYNAMIC EFFECT on the LV PV loop during systole?

✅ C — Decreased ESP → loop narrows, ↓ MVO₂
The IABP deflates just before ventricular systole, creating a vacuum effect in the aorta that reduces the impedance the LV ejects against (↓Ea). This lowers ESP, reduces wall stress, decreases MVO₂, and slightly increases SV. During diastole, the balloon inflates, augmenting diastolic aortic pressure and improving coronary perfusion — this is the 'diastolic augmentation'.

Q10. A patient with HFpEF (EF 62%, E/e' 18, BNP 450 pg/mL) exercises on a bike. What PRIMARILY explains their exertional dyspnea on the PV loop level?

✅ B — No preload reserve; EDP rises steeply
In HFpEF, the EDPVR is steep and shifted left. At rest, EDP may be borderline. During exercise, the normal mechanism of ↑EDV (Frank-Starling) is blocked by the stiff LV wall. Instead, any attempt to increase filling results in a steep rise in EDP along the stiff EDPVR → ↑ pulmonary venous pressure → dyspnea. This is the hallmark of HFpEF: exercise-induced diastolic failure. ESPVR (systolic) may be normal or even enhanced.
