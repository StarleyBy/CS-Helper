Infective endocarditis (IE) – native valve (NVE) vs prosthetic valve (PVE)
Native valve endocarditis (NVE)

Common pathogens: Viridans streptococci (40-60%), Staphylococcus aureus (25-35%), Enterococci, HACEK group (up to 5%).

Empirical regimen (before culture results):
Benzylpenicillin 1.8 g IV q4h + Flucloxacillin 2 g IV q4h + Gentamicin (synergistic dose).

Targeted therapy (guideline-based):

  • Penicillin-susceptible viridans streptococci: Penicillin G or Ceftriaxone for 4 weeks.
  • MRSA / MSSA severe: Nafcillin/oxacillin (or vancomycin if MRSA) + gentamicin for first 3-5 days.
  • Enterococci: Ampicillin + gentamicin or ceftriaxone + ampicillin (for gentamicin-resistant strains).
Duration: 4 weeks for NVE, 6 weeks for PVE and complicated cases.
Prosthetic valve endocarditis (PVE)

Common pathogens (early PVE, <12 months post-op): CoNS (~30-40%), S. aureus, enterococci, Gram-negative rods, fungi (Candida). Late PVE (>12 months) similar to NVE.

Empirical regimen (high MRSA / MDR risk): Vancomycin 30 mg/kg/day IV + Rifampin 900 mg/day + Gentamicin (first 2 weeks).

Targeted therapy:

  • MSSA: Nafcillin or oxacillin 12 g/24h + Rifampin 900 mg/24h + Gentamicin (first 2 weeks).
  • MRSA / CoNS (resistant): Vancomycin + Rifampin + Gentamicin (first 2 weeks).
  • HACEK PVE: Ceftriaxone 2 g/24h IV for 6 weeks. Gentamicin NOT recommended due to nephrotoxicity.
Rifampin resistance warning: CoNS may develop rifampin resistance during therapy; repeat susceptibility testing if relapse occurs.
Post-sternotomy mediastinitis & deep sternal wound infection

Epidemiology: Incidence 0.5-4%; Gram-negative bacteria cause 18-38% of cases, often polymicrobial (20-44%) and earlier onset than Gram-positive mediastinitis. Mortality higher when initial antibiotic therapy is inappropriate.

Common pathogens: S. aureus, CoNS, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Enterobacteriaceae (E. coli, Klebsiella), Acinetobacter baumannii, Mycoplasma hominis (rare but critical).

Culture-directed therapy examples:
  • MSSA: Nafcillin/oxacillin + debridement
  • MRSA / CoNS: Vancomycin, linezolid, or daptomycin
  • Pseudomonas: Piperacillin-tazobactam, meropenem, or ceftazidime
  • ESBL producers: Carbapenems (meropenem, imipenem)
  • Acinetobacter: Colistin + tigecycline or sulbactam combinations
Special pathogens:
  • Mycoplasma hominis: No cell wall → resistant to beta-lactams; treat with moxifloxacin, doxycycline, or clindamycin.
  • Gordonia bronchialis: Prolonged therapy (>=6 weeks); susceptibility test-driven (fluoroquinolones, carbapenems, linezolid).
  • Nocardia farcinica: TMP-SMX, amikacin, or linezolid; surgical debridement essential.
Management principle: Culture-directed antimicrobial therapy + early extensive surgical debridement (immediate or delayed sternal closure) is mandatory. Inappropriate initial antibiotics are associated with significantly higher mortality in Gram-negative mediastinitis.
Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) & MDR pathogens in cardiac ICU

Microbiology of VAP after cardiac surgery: Gram-negative bacilli dominate (approx 90%). Most common isolates: Acinetobacter baumannii (40%), Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Klebsiella pneumoniae (ESBL producers), Enterobacter spp. Multidrug-resistant organisms (MDRO) cause >55% of VAP episodes after cardiac surgery.

Risk factors for MDRO VAP: Pre-op renal impairment, CPB time >=151 min, postoperative AKI, and nasal feeding.

Empirical VAP therapy (early, within 1h)
  • Non-critically ill, low MDR risk: Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g q6h OR Ceftazidime 2 g q8h OR Levofloxacin 750 mg daily.
  • High MDR risk / septic shock: Combination therapy: anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam (meropenem 1 g q8h, piperacillin-tazobactam) + second agent (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, or aminoglycoside). For MRSA risk, add vancomycin or linezolid.
  • Acinetobacter / carbapenem-resistant (XDR): Colistin + tigecycline + meropenem high-dose or sulbactam combination.
De-escalate based on culture & sensitivity; avoid prolonged unnecessary double coverage to prevent resistance.
MDRO & resistance patterns
  • ESBL (Klebsiella, E. coli): Carbapenems (meropenem, imipenem) or ceftazidime-avibactam.
  • Carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter: Colistin (+/- tigecycline, +/- high-dose sulbactam).
  • DTR Pseudomonas: Ceftolozane-tazobactam, ceftazidime-avibactam, or imipenem-relebactam.
Prolonged ICU stay, prior broad-spectrum antibiotics, and re-intubation are strong predictors of MDR VAP. Perform routine surveillance cultures.

Empirical antibiotic therapy recommendations (2025)
Native valve endocarditis (NVE) empirical
  • Community-acquired, no risk factors for MRSA: Ampicillin 2 g IV q4h + Ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily (or Flucloxacillin + gentamicin).
  • Healthcare-associated / MRSA risk: Vancomycin 30 mg/kg/day + Ceftriaxone (or ampicillin-sulbactam).
  • Culture-negative suspicion (HACEK, Brucella, Bartonella): Ceftriaxone + doxycycline (or levofloxacin).
Prosthetic valve endocarditis (PVE) empirical
  • Early PVE (<12 months) / late PVE but high MRSA/MDR risk: Vancomycin + Rifampin + Gentamicin (first 2 weeks).
  • Late PVE (>12 months) with low resistance: Ampicillin + Ceftriaxone (enterococcus + HACEK coverage) ± gentamicin if synergism needed.
  • If fungal PVE suspected (Candida, Aspergillus): Add liposomal amphotericin B or an echinocandin pending cultures.
Post-cardiac surgery sepsis (unknown source)
  • High MDR / ESBL risk (ICU, long hospital stay): Meropenem 1-2 g q8h + Vancomycin (if MRSA risk).
  • Pseudomonas coverage (VAP, sternal wound): Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g q6h OR Cefepime 2 g q8h + ciprofloxacin or tobramycin.
  • Acinetobacter / CRGNB (prior colonisation): Colistin + meropenem + tigecycline.
Monitoring, reversal agents, and special considerations
Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM):
  • Vancomycin: trough 10-15 mg/L (simple infection) or 15-20 (severe/MRSA).
  • Aminoglycosides: peak/trough for synergy or once-daily dosing.
  • Rifampin: not routinely monitored but assess drug interactions (warfarin, immunosuppressants).
  • Linezolid: monitor CBC weekly (thrombocytopenia after 14 days).
Reversal / management of adverse effects:
  • Bleeding on rifampin + anticoagulation: Monitor INR closely; consider temporary VKA reduction.
  • Nephrotoxicity (aminoglycosides, colistin): Hold or substitute (ceftazidime-avibactam, fosfomycin).
  • Clostridioides difficile: Avoid unnecessary broad-spectrum antibiotics; oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin if severe.
  • Anaphylaxis / severe allergy: Desensitisation protocols for beta-lactams in IE.
Key take-home messages for cardiac surgery: - Early and appropriate empirical therapy reduces mortality in Gram-negative mediastinitis and endocarditis. - Biofilm-associated infections (prosthetic valves, sternal wires) require rifampin combination and often surgical intervention. - Multidrug-resistant pathogens (ESBL, CR-Acinetobacter, MRSA) are increasingly common – follow local antibiogram and de-escalate. - In culture-negative cases after cardiac surgery always consider Mycoplasma, Nocardia, Gordonia, and fungi.
