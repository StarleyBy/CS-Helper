🔵 Gram-Positive Organisms

  • Cocci in clusters: Staphylococci (S. aureus, CoNS)
  • Cocci in chains: Streptococci, Enterococci
  • Non-acid-fast rods: Listeria (rare cardiac)
  • Weakly acid-fast rods: Nocardia, Gordonia, Mycobacteria
  • Filamentous branching: Nocardia, Actinomyces
Cell wall: Thick peptidoglycan — targets of β-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems) and glycopeptides (vancomycin, teicoplanin).

🔴 Gram-Negative Organisms

  • Enterobacterales: E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Serratia, Proteus
  • Non-fermenters: Pseudomonas, Acinetobacter, Stenotrophomonas
  • Coccobacilli: Haemophilus, HACEK group, Brucella
  • No cell wall: Mycoplasma (Gram-indeterminate — invisible on Gram stain)
Outer membrane (LPS): Makes Gram-negatives intrinsically resistant to many Gram-positive-targeted drugs. Porins, efflux pumps, and β-lactamases drive acquired resistance.

🍄 Fungal Pathogens in Cardiac Surgery

  • Candida albicans: Most common; biofilm on prosthetic valves; fluconazole susceptible
  • C. parapsilosis: CIED/LVAD biofilm; may have reduced echinocandin susceptibility
  • C. glabrata (Nakaseomyces): Azole-resistant; echinocandin first-line
  • Aspergillus: Rare but fatal PVE; angio-invasive; voriconazole + surgery
  • Risk factors: TPN, broad-spectrum antibiotics, immunosuppression, prolonged ICU/ventilation, renal replacement therapy
Fungal PVE: Mortality >50%. Surgical valve replacement mandatory in most cases combined with antifungal therapy (echinocandin or liposomal AmB). Lifelong secondary prophylaxis often required.

🔪 Surgical Indications in IE (ESC Class I — Emergency/Urgent)

!

Heart failure (HF) — operate within 24h

Acute severe AR or MR from valve destruction → pulmonary edema, cardiogenic shock. Most common indication.

!

Uncontrolled infection

Paravalvular abscess, intracardiac fistula, prosthetic valve dehiscence. PVE not responding to antibiotics >1 week.

↺

Embolism prevention

Mobile vegetation >10 mm (especially anterior mitral leaflet). Recurrent emboli despite antibiotics — operate within 48h.

↺

Fungal / MDR PVE

Candida, Aspergillus PVE: antibiotic-alone cure rate <50%. Surgery + antifungal mandatory.

High-risk features mandating early surgery:
  • 🔴 S. aureus PVE (mortality without surgery ~70%)
  • 🔴 Periannular extension — abscess, pseudoaneurysm, fistula
  • 🟡 New AV block / conduction abnormality (septal extension)
  • 🟡 Persistent bacteremia >5–7 days on appropriate antibiotics
  • 🟡 Vegetation >10 mm after first embolic event
  • 🟠 EuroSCORE II / STS score for operative risk quantification
Endocarditis Team (ESC 2023 Class I): Multidisciplinary team — cardiologist, cardiac surgeon, microbiologist, ID specialist, neurologist, imaging expert — mandatory for all IE cases managed at an endocarditis centre.

📋 Modified Duke Criteria — IE Diagnosis

Sensitivity ~80% for definite IE in native valves. Lower for PVE, CIED-IE, and culture-negative cases. Always combine with clinical judgment and advanced imaging.

🔴 Major Criteria

1. Positive Blood Cultures

  • Typical organism in ≥2 separate cultures: S. aureus, S. viridans, S. gallolyticus, HACEK, Enterococcus (community-acquired, no primary focus)
  • Persistently positive cultures (same organism) ≥12h apart, OR 3 of 3 (or majority of ≥4) BCs drawn >1h apart
  • Single positive culture for Coxiella burnetii OR anti-Phase I IgG titre >1:800

2. Evidence of Endocardial Involvement

  • Echo: Vegetation, abscess, pseudoaneurysm, intracardiac fistula, valve perforation/aneurysm, new prosthetic valve dehiscence
  • New valvular regurgitation (worsening of pre-existing murmur is NOT sufficient)
  • Abnormal ¹⁸F-FDG PET/CT around prosthetic valve implanted >3 months
  • Para-prosthetic leak on cardiac CT
  • Leukocyte SPECT/CT showing abnormal activity (alternative to PET)

🔵 Minor Criteria

  • Predisposition: Underlying cardiac condition (valvulopathy, prosthesis, CHD, previous IE, CIED) OR IV drug use
  • Fever: Temperature >38°C
  • Vascular phenomena: Arterial emboli, septic pulmonary infarcts, mycotic aneurysm, intracranial hemorrhage, conjunctival hemorrhages, Janeway lesions
  • Immunological phenomena: Glomerulonephritis, Osler nodes, Roth spots, rheumatoid factor positive
  • Microbiological evidence: Positive BC not meeting major criterion, or serology of active infection consistent with IE
  • New 2023 additions: ¹⁸F-FDG PET/CT body uptake (non-prosthetic), MRI/CT embolic lesions, peripheral culture positive for IE pathogen

⚕️ Risk Factors & Prevention

Modifiable risk factors:
  • Diabetes mellitus (HbA1c >7%) — doubles mediastinitis risk
  • Obesity (BMI >35) — deadspace, impaired wound healing
  • Bilateral IMA harvest — devascularises sternum
  • Re-sternotomy / redo surgery
  • Prolonged CPB time (>150 min)
  • Postoperative low-output state
  • Emergency surgery, large transfusion volume
Prevention bundle (STS/WHO):
  • Preoperative nasal MRSA decolonization (mupirocin + chlorhexidine bath × 5 days)
  • 2% chlorhexidine-gluconate skin prep (superior to povidone-iodine)
  • Cefazolin within 60 min before incision; re-dose q4h if prolonged; vancomycin if MRSA carrier
  • Glycaemic control — target 140–180 mg/dL intraoperatively and postoperatively
  • Sternal wound dressing protocol; sternal stability assessment at extubation
Prophylaxis duration: Single dose or 24h cefazolin (STS guidelines). Prolonged prophylaxis does NOT reduce DSWI rates and drives MDR organism selection.

🔪 Surgical Management — Key Principles

1

Aggressive debridement

Remove ALL infected/necrotic sternal bone, wires, cartilage. Completeness of debridement is the primary determinant of outcome.

2

NPWT (VAC therapy)

Negative pressure wound therapy — reduces edema, promotes granulation, controls contamination. Bridge to definitive reconstruction.

3

Reconstruction

Pectoralis major flap (most common); omental transposition (excellent vascularity + antibacterial); rectus abdominis flap (large defects). Primary rewiring for limited cases.

Outcome predictors:
  • Time to diagnosis & debridement — every 24h delay worsens outcome
  • Gram-negative / polymicrobial → higher mortality
  • Completeness of debridement
  • Flap viability and choice
  • Minimum 4–6 weeks IV antibiotics post-debridement
Key principle: Inappropriate initial antibiotics (wrong spectrum or delayed initiation) in Gram-negative mediastinitis is independently associated with increased in-hospital mortality. Empirical coverage MUST include anti-pseudomonal and MRSA activity until cultures return.

🛡️ VAP Prevention Bundle

Mandatory bundle (IHI / SHEA):
  • Head-of-bed elevation 30–45° continuously
  • Daily sedation interruption + spontaneous breathing trial
  • Oral decontamination: 0.12% chlorhexidine q6h (especially post-cardiac surgery — reduces VAP ~35%)
  • Subglottic secretion drainage (endotracheal tubes with SSD port)
  • Minimise circuit changes; avoid unnecessary acid suppression
Selective decontamination (SDD/SOD):
  • SDD: oral paste + enteral non-absorbable antibiotics + short systemic course — reduces VAP and mortality in cardiac ICU; NNT ~15
  • SOD (selective oral only): safer alternative in high-MDR settings
  • Controversial: may select resistance in high-MDR-prevalence units
Surveillance cultures: Weekly tracheal aspirates in ventilated cardiac ICU patients detect MDR colonization before clinical infection — enables rapid appropriate empirical coverage.

⚡ CIED & LVAD Infections

CIED infections range from superficial pocket infections to life-threatening lead endocarditis. Complete device extraction is mandatory in most cases — antibiotics alone have <25% cure rate.

📟 CIED Infection Classification

1

Pocket infection (local)

Erythema, swelling, discharge at generator site. No lead involvement. Pathogens: S. aureus, CoNS. Requires complete extraction + reimplantation at new site.

2

CIED-related bacteremia

Positive blood cultures without alternative source + CIED present. S. aureus bacteremia = extraction indicated unless definitive alternative source. TEE + PET to evaluate lead vegetation.

3

CIED lead endocarditis (CDIE)

Vegetation on pacemaker/ICD lead or tricuspid valve. Duke criteria apply. Mortality 10–30%. Complete system extraction mandatory — TLE or surgical if vegetation >25 mm.

4

Erosion / Chronic sinus

Device erosion through skin or chronic sinus tract = always infected. Never treat antibiotics alone. Extraction + prolonged antibiotics required.

🤖 LVAD-Specific Infections (ISHLT Classification)

L1

Driveline infection

Localised infection at exit site — most common LVAD infection. Pathogens: S. aureus (#1), Pseudomonas. Local wound care + targeted systemic antibiotics.

L2

Pump pocket infection

Generator/pump pocket infection. More serious. Often requires device exchange at transplant. Prolonged suppressive antibiotics.

L3

Pump / cannula endocarditis

Vegetation on LVAD pump, cannula, or inflow/outflow conduit. Mortality >50%. Device exchange or urgent transplant.

LVAD infection management principles:
  • Prolonged suppressive antibiotics required in non-transplant candidates (unable to remove device)
  • Anti-biofilm strategy: Rifampin combinations, EDTA driveline flushes
  • Fungal LVAD infection (Candida): Echinocandin + device exchange at transplant
  • High-risk organisms: S. aureus and Pseudomonas — associated with pump thrombosis, stroke, death
  • Weekly driveline wound inspection; blood cultures if febrile
VA-ECMO + infection: ECMO circuits are highly susceptible to biofilm colonization. Candida and CoNS are most common. Weekly surveillance cultures from circuit ports. Persistent bacteremia on ECMO requires source control — all removable catheters and lines should be replaced.

Q1. S. aureus prosthetic valve endocarditis — cultures grow MSSA. Drug of CHOICE (not alternative)?

✅ B — Flucloxacillin + Rifampin + Gentamicin
For confirmed MSSA PVE, anti-staphylococcal penicillins (flucloxacillin/nafcillin) are Class I recommended — superior to vancomycin in randomised trials: lower mortality, faster bacteremia clearance, less nephrotoxicity. Rifampin is added for biofilm penetration on the prosthetic valve. Gentamicin given for first 2 weeks only (synergy, not prolonged). Vancomycin is reserved for penicillin allergy or confirmed MRSA.

Q2. #1 cause of early prosthetic valve endocarditis (<12 months post-implant)?

✅ C — CoNS (predominantly S. epidermidis)
CoNS cause 30–40% of early PVE. They contaminate at implantation and adhere via icaABCD-mediated biofilm (polysaccharide intercellular adhesin — PIA). Over 70% are methicillin-resistant. Late PVE (>12 months) has a pathogen distribution similar to NVE, with streptococci and S. aureus predominating.

Q3. A farmer with cattle contact has culture-negative endocarditis. Priority organism to test for?

✅ C — Coxiella burnetii (Q-fever)
Cattle, sheep, and goats are the primary reservoirs for Coxiella burnetii. It is an obligate intracellular organism — does NOT grow on standard blood cultures. Diagnosis: Phase I IgG titre >1:800 = Major Duke criterion in the 2023 ESC guidelines. Treatment: Doxycycline 200 mg/24h + Hydroxychloroquine 600 mg/24h for ≥18 months (chronic Q-fever IE). Bartonella is linked to cat/flea contact (B. henselae) or louse contact/homelessness (B. quintana).

Q4. Daptomycin is prescribed for MRSA PVE. Which co-medication must be HELD?

✅ B — Statins (Atorvastatin)
Daptomycin causes myopathy/rhabdomyolysis — a risk significantly potentiated by HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors (statins). Hold ALL statins for the entire daptomycin course. Additionally: (1) Daptomycin is inactivated by pulmonary surfactant — never use for pneumonia; (2) Use higher doses for IE (8–12 mg/kg/24h vs 6 mg/kg for bacteremia alone); (3) Monitor CPK weekly — stop if CPK >5× ULN or symptomatic myopathy.

Q5. Post-cardiac surgery mediastinitis — cultures negative on standard agar for 7 days. Organism most likely missed and required action?

✅ B — Mycoplasma hominis
Mycoplasma hominis: (1) lacks a cell wall → not visible on Gram stain; (2) does NOT grow on blood/chocolate/MacConkey agar; (3) intrinsically resistant to all β-lactams (no cell wall target); (4) requires Mycoplasma-specific liquid broth incubated 7–14 days under microaerophilic conditions. Treatment: Moxifloxacin 400 mg/24h IV (best evidence) or Doxycycline 100 mg q12h for 4–6 weeks. Always request specific Mycoplasma culture in culture-negative post-sternotomy mediastinitis.

Q6. Which is a MAJOR Duke criterion per 2023 ESC modified guidelines?

✅ C — ¹⁸F-FDG PET/CT (prosthetic valve)
The 2015 and 2023 ESC guidelines elevated abnormal ¹⁸F-FDG PET/CT activity around a prosthetic valve (implanted >3 months) to a MAJOR Duke criterion. Leukocyte SPECT/CT is also now recognised as a major criterion alternative. This is particularly valuable in PVE where echocardiography is limited by prosthetic acoustic shadowing. Fever, IV drug use, Janeway lesions, Osler nodes, Roth spots, and rheumatoid factor are all MINOR criteria.

Q7. Rifampin added to vancomycin for MRSA PVE. Patient deteriorates at 3 weeks. Most likely explanation?

✅ B — Rifampin resistance emergence
CoNS (and S. aureus) can develop rifampin resistance via rpoB mutations (RNA polymerase β subunit) during therapy — this is precisely why rifampin must NEVER be used as monotherapy. The combination with vancomycin reduces but does not eliminate this risk. On clinical deterioration: repeat susceptibility testing for rifampin resistance. If confirmed: switch to linezolid + daptomycin combination or consult ID for salvage regimen.

Q8. Patient with mechanical valve on warfarin starts rifampin for PVE. What happens to INR?

✅ B — INR decreases (thrombosis risk)
Rifampin is a potent inducer of CYP2C9 (warfarin's primary metabolic enzyme) and P-glycoprotein. This accelerates warfarin clearance, reducing anticoagulant effect — INR falls, increasing thrombosis risk on a mechanical valve. Management: Monitor INR daily when starting rifampin; increase warfarin dose (sometimes 2–5× normal required). Critical warning: when rifampin is STOPPED, CYP2C9 induction fades over 1–2 weeks — INR rises sharply. Monitor INR daily for ≥2 weeks after rifampin discontinuation and reduce warfarin dose proactively.

Q9. Which carbapenem is CONTRAINDICATED for Pseudomonas aeruginosa VAP?

✅ B — Ertapenem
Ertapenem has NO activity against Pseudomonas aeruginosa. This is because: (1) Pseudomonas lacks the specific outer membrane porin (OprD) that ertapenem requires for entry; (2) Pseudomonas possesses AmpC β-lactamase and MexAB-OprM efflux pumps that further exclude ertapenem. Ertapenem should NEVER be used for Pseudomonas infections, VAP, or mediastinitis. Meropenem, imipenem, doripenem, piperacillin-tazobactam, ceftazidime, cefepime, and ciprofloxacin all have anti-pseudomonal activity (subject to local susceptibility).

Q10. Modern vancomycin monitoring target (not trough alone)?

✅ B — AUC₂₄/MIC 400–600 mg·h/L
The 2020 ASHP/IDSA/SIDP joint consensus guideline replaced trough-only monitoring with AUC/MIC-guided dosing. Rationale: (1) trough alone poorly predicts both efficacy AND nephrotoxicity; (2) patients can have "therapeutic" troughs with toxic AUC or sub-therapeutic AUC. Practice: collect 2 blood samples (1–2h post-dose + pre-dose) → use Bayesian software for AUC calculation. Target AUC/MIC 400–600 for S. aureus with MIC 1 mg/L (most MRSA). Trough-only monitoring should be abandoned.

Q11. Patient with MRSA bacteremia has a permanent pacemaker implanted 2 years ago. Correct management?

✅ B — Complete extraction + antibiotics; delayed reimplant
S. aureus bacteremia with a CIED mandates complete system extraction (generator + ALL leads) because: (1) S. aureus seeds pacemaker leads in >50% of cases; (2) antibiotics-alone cure rate <25% due to biofilm; (3) persistent/relapsed bacteremia is life-threatening. Antibiotic duration: ≥2 weeks from first negative blood culture (uncomplicated bacteremia); ≥6 weeks if CDIE confirmed. Reimplantation: ≥14 days after first negative blood culture at a new anatomical site. Use transvenous lead extraction (TLE) if feasible; surgical extraction for vegetations >25 mm or failed TLE.

Q12. Which drug combination is an ABSOLUTE contraindication due to near-complete pharmacokinetic abolition?

✅ B — Voriconazole + Rifampin
Rifampin induces CYP2C19 and CYP3A4 — the primary enzymes metabolising voriconazole — reducing plasma voriconazole levels by >96%, rendering it completely ineffective (undetectable plasma levels). This is an ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION in all prescribing information. If simultaneous antifungal (Aspergillus) + rifampin (biofilm staphylococci) treatment is required: use isavuconazole (less CYP interaction) or liposomal amphotericin B instead of voriconazole. Vancomycin + pip-tazo significantly increases AKI risk but is not absolutely contraindicated; meropenem + colistin is an established combination for XDR GNB.
