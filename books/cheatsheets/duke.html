📖 Important clinical notes

Duke‑ISCVID criteria (2023 update) – Major criteria are now grouped into three logical categories: Microbiological evidence, Imaging evidence, and Pathological evidence. Each category counts as one major criterion regardless of how many sub‑items are fulfilled. This prevents overcounting (e.g., both TTE and PET/CT positive still = 1 major).

Janeway lesions vs Osler nodes: Janeway lesions are painless, non‑tender, erythematous macules on palms/soles – they represent septic microemboli and are characteristic of acute endocarditis (usually S. aureus). Osler nodes are painful, tender, violaceous nodules on finger/toe pulps – they result from immune complex deposition and are typical of subacute endocarditis (e.g., viridans streptococci).

Roth spots: Retinal haemorrhages with a pale centre, seen in IE, anaemia, and connective tissue diseases. Rare but highly suggestive when other criteria are present.

Vascular phenomena: The 2023 update emphasizes that conjunctival haemorrhages and mycotic aneurysms are valid minor criteria. Splinter haemorrhages are not specific but are still considered under vascular phenomena if embolic.

PET/CT: Added as both a major (if clearly positive) and minor (equivocal) criterion. It is especially useful in prosthetic valve endocarditis and cardiac device infections.

Based on: Duke‑ISCVID Criteria (Fowler et al., Clin Infect Dis 2023). Pathological evidence (surgical/autopsy) overrides all and automatically gives Definite IE.
