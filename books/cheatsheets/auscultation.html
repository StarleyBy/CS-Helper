🎤 Auscultation Foundations & Normal Heart Sounds

Mechanism of sound generation, auscultation areas, cardiac cycle correlation, and normal/extra heart sounds.

Electromechanical — Acoustic Correlation

⚡ From ECG Event to Audible Sound

S4 (if present) → pre-S1   |   S3 (if present) → 120–200ms after S2 (early diastole)

Sound generation mechanisms

  • Valve leaflet tension and closure — primary source of S1 and S2
  • Turbulent blood flow — murmurs from stenotic or regurgitant valves, high-flow states
  • Myocardial vibration — contributes to S3 and S4 via ventricular wall oscillations
  • Intracardiac structures — chordae, papillary muscles, prosthetic components
  • Pericardial contact — friction rubs, pericardial knock

Auscultation principles

  • Use diaphragm for high-pitched sounds (S1, S2, AR, MR murmurs)
  • Use bell (light pressure) for low-pitched sounds (S3, S4, MS rumble)
  • Listen in quiet environment; focus on one event at a time
  • Systole = interval between S1 and S2; Diastole = interval between S2 and next S1
  • Time murmurs to carotid pulse (simultaneous with systole)

Patient positions for optimal auscultation:
• Supine: Standard baseline; most murmurs heard
• Left lateral decubitus (LLD): Apex moves closer to chest wall → dramatically enhances S3, S4, MS rumble, MR
• Sitting forward, end-expiration: Heart moves anteriorly → best for AR early diastolic murmur and pericardial friction rub
• Standing vs squatting / Valsalva: See Tab 4 for dynamic manoeuvre effects
S1 — First Heart Sound

🔴 S1

Mechanism

S1 results from closure of the mitral (MV) and tricuspid (TV) valves at the onset of ventricular systole. The MV component (M1) is louder and precedes the TV component (T1) by 20–30 ms. Normally heard as a single sound.

S1 intensity correlates with
PR interval length & leaflet position at onset of systole
S1Causes
Loud S1Short PR interval (pre-excitation), mitral stenosis (thickened leaflets, high excursion), tachycardia, hyperkinetic states, thin chest
Soft S1Long PR (1st-degree AV block — leaflets drift toward closure before systole), severe MR, LBBB, low cardiac output, thick chest, COPD, pericardial effusion
Variable S1Complete AV block (RR varies → PR varies), AF, VT
S2 — Second Heart Sound & Splitting

🔵 S2 & Splitting Patterns

S2 results from closure of the aortic (A2) and pulmonic (P2) valves. A2 precedes P2 and is normally louder. P2 is best heard at the pulmonic area; increased P2 suggests pulmonary hypertension.

PatternMechanismKey associations
Physiological splittingInspiration → ↑venous return → ↑RV stroke volume → delayed P2. Normal split widens on inspiration, disappears on expiration.Normal finding in young adults
Wide splittingDelayed P2 (RBBB, PS, acute PE, RV failure) or early A2 (MR, VSD)RBBB, PE, PS, large ASD, MR
Fixed splittingEqual splitting in inspiration and expiration — RA/RV volume overload eliminates respiratory variationPathognomonic for ASD (ostium secundum)
Paradoxical splittingA2 delayed (closes after P2) → split on expiration, fuses on inspiration. P2 occurs first.LBBB, severe AS, HOCM, RV pacing, aortic stenosis with LV failure
P2 intensity: Normally P2 < A2. Loud P2 (audible at apex) = pulmonary hypertension until proven otherwise. In severe PH, P2 may equal or exceed A2 even at the apex.
A2 intensity: Soft or absent A2 suggests severe AS, aortic regurgitation (leaflet immobility), or low cardiac output. Louder A2 in systemic hypertension.
Extra Heart Sounds: S3 & S4 Gallops

🔷 S3 — Third Heart Sound (Protodiastolic Gallop)

Timing: 120–200 ms after S2, early diastole (rapid ventricular filling phase).

Quality: Low-pitched, dull — use bell at apex in left lateral decubitus position.

Mnemonic: S1–S2–S3 = “Ken–tuc–KY”

Pathological S3 (any age) = ventricular volume overload or impaired compliance:
• Severe MR, severe AR, VSD, DCM, CHF
• High LV filling pressure → rapid deceleration of blood causes myocardial oscillation
• Strongest predictor of elevated LVEDP on clinical exam
Physiological S3: Normal in children, young adults, athletes, and in the 3rd trimester of pregnancy. Benign — reflects rapid, compliant ventricular filling.

🔸 S4 — Fourth Heart Sound (Presystolic Gallop)

Timing: Just before S1, late diastole (atrial contraction → ventricular filling).

Quality: Low-pitched — use bell at apex.

Mnemonic: S4–S1–S2 = “Ten–nes–see”

S4 always pathological (requires a non-compliant, stiff ventricle resisting atrial filling):
• LVH (systemic hypertension, severe AS, HOCM)
• Myocardial ischaemia / acute MI
• Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
• Cannot occur in AF (requires atrial contraction)
Summation gallop: At high heart rates, S3 and S4 fuse into a single loud mid-diastolic sound. Occurs when diastole is short; suggests severely impaired ventricular function.
References: Braunwald’s Heart Disease 12th ed. · Hurst’s The Heart · Physical Examination of the Heart and Circulation (Shaver & Groves) · ESC Guidelines

🔴 Systolic Murmurs

Ejection and regurgitant systolic murmurs with mechanisms, radiation, severity clues, and manoeuvre responses.

Aortic Stenosis (AS)

🔴 Aortic Stenosis

Mechanism & Auscultation

Turbulent high-velocity jet through a narrowed aortic orifice. Harsh, crescendo-decrescendo (diamond-shaped) ejection murmur, best heard at the right 2nd ICS (aortic area), radiating to carotids and neck. Starts after S1, peaks in mid-systole, ends before S2.

Timing

Radiation

  • Classically to neck and carotids
  • Gallavardin phenomenon: High-frequency component radiates to apex — may mimic MR

⚠️ Critical pitfall: In low-flow, low-gradient severe AS (EF <40%), the murmur may be soft Grade 2/6 or nearly inaudible despite critical stenosis. Never use murmur loudness alone to assess AS severity. Echo and Doppler are mandatory.
Hypertrophic Obstructive Cardiomyopathy (HOCM)

🔷 HOCM — Dynamic LVOTO Murmur

Mechanism

Systolic anterior motion (SAM) of the mitral valve leaflet against the hypertrophied septum → dynamic left ventricular outflow tract obstruction. Murmur is crescendo-decrescendo, best at left sternal border, radiating to apex (but NOT to neck). Often accompanied by MR murmur at apex.

Key feature: HOCM murmur is dynamic — changes dramatically with manoeuvres that alter preload and afterload. Distinguishing from fixed AS is critical.

Contrast with AS: AS murmur typically gets louder with squatting (↑preload → ↑stroke volume) and softer with Valsalva — opposite to HOCM. This contrast is the key clinical differentiator.
Mitral Regurgitation (MR)

🔴 Mitral Regurgitation

Mechanism & Auscultation

Pansystolic (holosystolic) murmur at the apex, radiating to the left axilla (posterior MR jet) or to the left sternal border / base (anterior jet, e.g., posterior leaflet prolapse). High-pitched, blowing quality. Starts with S1, continues through S2.

Chronic MR
S1 ⎯⎯⎯⎯ PANSYSTOLIC ⎯⎯⎯⎯S2 (soft) (S3)

Chronic vs Acute MR

  • Chronic: Pansystolic, loud, well-tolerated; LA dilated and compliant → absorbs regurgitant volume
  • Acute MR (papillary muscle rupture, chordal rupture, IE): Soft, decrescendo early systolic murmur or even inaudible. Non-compliant LA → immediate pressure rise → rapid equalisation of LV–LA pressures → gradient disappears in late systole. Acute severe MR may be nearly silent.

Mitral Valve Prolapse (MVP)

Mid-systolic click + late systolic murmur.
Click: from sudden tensing of prolapsing leaflet and chordae.
Murmur: starts after click, extends to S2.

Dynamic changes (preload-dependent):
Standing / Valsalva: click moves earlier in systole (smaller LV)
Squatting: click moves later (larger LV) — may disappear
This movement distinguishes MVP from other causes of systolic click.
Acute severe MR pearl for surgeons: Post-MI papillary muscle rupture (usually posterior PM, RCA territory) or chordal rupture — presents with sudden pulmonary oedema, often without a loud murmur. Echo immediately. Emergency surgery.
Tricuspid Regurgitation, VSD & Innocent Murmurs

🔷 Tricuspid Regurgitation (TR)

Pansystolic murmur at the left lower sternal border (tricuspid area), radiating to the right sternal border.

Carvallo’s Sign

The TR murmur characteristically increases with inspiration (Carvallo’s sign) — inspiration ↑venous return → ↑RV volume → ↑TR jet velocity. This distinguishes TR from MR (which does not increase with inspiration). Sensitivity ~80%.

Severe TR: may be soft or absent despite torrential regurgitation if the low-pressure RV and RA fully equalise (similar to acute MR principle). Look for signs of RV failure, hepatic pulsation, prominent V waves in JVP.

🔹 VSD & Innocent Murmurs

Ventricular Septal Defect

Pansystolic murmur at left 4th–5th ICS (small: loud and harsh; large: paradoxically soft due to pressure equalisation). Thrill often palpable. Associated with left parasternal heave if large.

Innocent / Physiological Murmurs

Still’s murmur (children): Musical, vibratory, Grade 1–2/6, mid-systolic, best at left lower sternal border. Decreases with standing.

Pulmonary flow murmur: Soft ejection murmur at left upper sternal border; common in children, pregnancy, anaemia, fever.

Features of all innocent murmurs: Grade ≤2/6 · Systolic only · No radiation · Normal S1/S2 · No thrill · No extra sounds · Varies with position
References: Braunwald’s Heart Disease · ACC/AHA Valve Guidelines 2021 · Fowler’s Physical Diagnosis of Heart Disease

🔵 Diastolic & Continuous Murmurs

All significant diastolic findings and continuous murmurs require echo confirmation. Diastolic murmur = always pathological.

Clinical rule: Every diastolic murmur is pathological until proven otherwise. No innocent diastolic murmur exists in clinical practice.
Aortic Regurgitation (AR)

🔵 Aortic Regurgitation

Early diastolic murmur

High-pitched, blowing, decrescendo murmur starting at A2, heard best at Erb’s point (3rd ICS left) and left sternal border with patient sitting forward, end-expiration (brings aortic root closer to stethoscope).

Chronic AR
S1 ⎯⎯SYSTOLE⎯⎯ S2 ▶\▶\▶ decrescendo DIASTOLE ⎯⎯ S1

Acute vs Chronic AR

  • Chronic AR: Long-standing, dilated LV accommodates volume; loud murmur; wide pulse pressure; peripheral signs (Corrigan, de Musset, Quincke, Duroziez)
  • Acute AR (IE, aortic dissection): LV small and non-compliant → rapid pressure equalisation → soft, short, early diastolic murmur or absent. S1 may be soft (elevated LVEDP closes MV early). Pulmonary oedema. Emergency surgery.

Austin-Flint murmur

Mid-to-late diastolic rumble at the apex in severe AR. Mechanism: AR jet strikes the anterior MV leaflet → functional MV obstruction → low-pitched rumble mimicking MS.
Distinguishing from MS: Austin-Flint has NO opening snap; loudness decreases with amyl nitrite (which improves AR) whereas MS rumble is unaffected.

Peripheral signs of chronic AR

  • Wide pulse pressure (e.g., 160/60)
  • Corrigan’s pulse: waterhammer, collapsing
  • de Musset’s sign: head nodding
  • Quincke’s sign: nail bed capillary pulsation
  • Duroziez’s sign: systolic/diastolic bruit over femoral with compression
Mitral Stenosis (MS)

🔵 Mitral Stenosis

Mechanism & Auscultation

Low-pitched, mid-diastolic rumble with presystolic accentuation (if sinus rhythm), heard best at apex in left lateral decubitus using the bell with light pressure.

Opening snap

  • High-pitched, snapping sound in early diastole — from abrupt halt of domed MS leaflets
  • A2–OS interval: inversely correlates with severity. Shorter = more severe (higher LA pressure closes gap between A2 and OS)
  • OS disappears in severe calcific MS (immobile leaflets)

MS + AF: Presystolic accentuation disappears in AF (requires atrial contraction). Rumble becomes variable in duration due to variable RR intervals. The diagnosis becomes harder — use long RR intervals to hear the full diastolic rumble.
Other Diastolic Murmurs & Continuous Murmurs

🔵 Pulmonary Regurgitation & Graham-Steell

  • Graham-Steell murmur: High-pitched early diastolic murmur at left 2nd ICS from pulmonary regurgitation secondary to severe pulmonary hypertension. Mimics AR murmur; increases with inspiration (unlike AR). Key clue: loud P2, right ventricular heave, signs of PHT.
  • Primary PR (after Ross procedure, post-surgical): Low-pitched, soft; well tolerated; variable duration depending on PA–RV pressure gradient.

Tricuspid Stenosis

Rare. Mid-diastolic rumble at left lower sternal border; increases with inspiration (like TR); presystolic accentuation in SR. Associated with rheumatic MS and carcinoid syndrome.

Pericardial Sounds

Pericardial Friction Rub

Harsh, scratchy, grating sound with 2–3 components (ventricular systole, early diastole, late diastole / atrial systole). Best heard at left sternal border with patient sitting forward, end-expiration. Variable, transient — may disappear within hours.

Heard in: acute pericarditis, post-cardiac surgery (Dressler syndrome), uraemia, post-MI pericarditis. Increased pressure of stethoscope enhances the rub (distinguishes from pleural rub, which diminishes). Can persist in effusion if pericardial layers still in contact.

Pericardial Knock & Tumour Plop

Pericardial knock

Early diastolic sound (earlier and higher-pitched than S3). Caused by abrupt cessation of ventricular filling when the rigid, thickened pericardium suddenly restricts expansion. Pathognomonic of constrictive pericarditis. Best at left sternal border with the patient leaning forward.

Tumour plop

Left atrial myxoma: Low-pitched mid-diastolic “plop” as the prolapsing tumour falls through the MV orifice. Position-dependent (loudest in lateral decubitus). May mimic MS opening snap. Always consider when clinical presentation is atypical.

❤️ Cardiac Surgery & Prosthetic Valves

Prosthetic valve sounds, dysfunction, post-surgical auscultation findings, and cardiac surgery-specific clinical pearls.

Mechanical Prosthetic Valves

⚙️ Mechanical Valve Sounds — Normal vs Pathological

Normal mechanical valve sounds

Bileaflet tilting disc (St. Jude, On-X): Crisp, distinct metallic click at closure (equivalent to S1 for MVR; S2 for AVR). Opening click is softer and may be subtle. Both clicks are high-pitched and metallic.

Single tilting disc (Bjork-Shiley, Medtronic-Hall): Similar crisp click; slightly different pitch. Characteristic ‘clunk’ quality in some models.

Ball-in-cage (Starr-Edwards, historical): Two sounds per cycle — opening click and closing click, both prominent.

What to listen for at each visit

  • Intensity of closing click — should be consistent
  • Any new murmur (was previously absent)
  • Change in character of existing click
  • Muffling or dampening of the click
FindingLikely causeAction
Soft / muffled closing clickProsthetic thrombosis, pannus ingrowth, stuck leaflet — decreased leaflet excursionUrgent echo (TOE preferred), cinefluoroscopy. Emergency if haemodynamically compromised.
New systolic murmur (AVR)Prosthetic stenosis (thrombosis/pannus), paravalvular leak (PVL) with MR equivalent, patient–prosthesis mismatchEcho with Doppler gradients; compare with baseline
New early diastolic murmur (AVR)Paravalvular aortic regurgitation, prosthetic valve endocarditis (PVE)TOE; blood cultures; CT if endocarditis suspected

⚠️ Prosthetic valve thrombosis (PVT): Medical emergency. Clinical presentation: muffled or absent closing click, new systolic murmur (if AVR), haemodynamic deterioration. Confirm with TOE or cinefluoroscopy. Treatment: UFH anticoagulation (small non-obstructive thrombus), thrombolysis, or emergency redo surgery based on thrombus size, haemodynamics, and surgical risk.
Bioprostheses & TAVI

🌿 Bioprosthetic Valves

Stented bioprostheses (Edwards PERIMOUNT, Medtronic Mosaic, Hancock II) produce no metallic click — auscultatory profile similar to a native valve but with:

  • Soft ejection murmur at base (normal — from mild turbulence through stent struts)
  • No opening click
  • Grade 1–2/6 murmur may be present physiologically
Structural valve deterioration (SVD): Progressive restenosis or regurgitation. New or increasing murmur on follow-up. Compare with baseline echo. Stentless valves (Freestyle, Prima Plus) have the most “native-like” auscultation.

Prosthetic valve endocarditis (PVE)

New or changing murmur in a patient with a prosthetic valve + fever = PVE until proven otherwise. New regurgitant murmur = possible leaflet destruction or dehiscence. TOE is mandatory (TTE often insufficient).

🅾 TAVI (Transcatheter Aortic Valve Implantation)

Post-TAVI auscultation differs from surgical AVR:

  • No metallic click (leaflets are biological)
  • Soft systolic flow murmur common (physiological within stent frame)
  • Paravalvular aortic regurgitation: Very common, especially with self-expanding valves. Low-pitched early diastolic murmur at left sternal border. Moderate or severe PVL is clinically significant.
  • Pacemaker-induced LBBB murmur variation: RV apical pacing causes altered septal motion → may change murmur character
TAVI + new severe AR murmur: Urgent echo. May indicate valve embolism, leaflet tear, or severe PVL. Transcatheter valve-in-valve or surgical explantation may be required.

⚠️ Critical Pearls for Cardiac Surgeons & Intensivists

  • IABP counterpulsation: Creates audible diastolic augmentation — may confuse auscultation. Always note IABP timing during exam.
  • Post-sternotomy: Subcutaneous air or sternal wires may transmit clicks. Sternal click (not cardiac) is reproducible with palpation of sternum.
  • Early post-op tamponade: Muffled heart sounds, pulsus paradoxus, tachycardia. Beck’s triad (hypotension, JVD, muffled sounds) is specific but late. Echo immediately if suspected.
  • Acute AR post-TAVI or post-AVR: Presents with haemodynamic collapse and soft/absent new early diastolic murmur — do not delay echo for clinical diagnosis.
  • Post-cardiac surgery pericarditis (Dressler): Pleuritic chest pain + friction rub + fever, days 2–6 post-op. Differentiate from surgical site pain and pneumonia. NSAIDs/colchicine if haemodynamically stable.
  • Haemolysis from PVL: Chronic intravascular haemolysis (elevated LDH, low haptoglobin, anaemia) — even without a loud murmur, significant haemolysis warrants re-evaluation.

🔎 Dynamic Manoeuvres & Diagnostic Algorithm

Bedside manoeuvres that alter cardiac physiology and change murmur intensity, plus a stepwise diagnostic algorithm for unidentified murmurs.

Physiological Basis of Dynamic Manoeuvres
Principle: Murmur intensity changes when preload (ventricular filling), afterload (systemic resistance), or heart rate changes.
• ↑Preload (squatting, PLR, expiration, handgrip for LV): ↑ventricular volume → ↑fixed-orifice murmurs; ↓dynamic obstruction (HOCM)
• ↓Preload (standing, Valsalva strain): ↓ventricular volume → ↑HOCM; ↓fixed-orifice murmurs
• ↑Afterload (handgrip): ↑LV pressure → ↑MR, AR; ↓HOCM, AS
• Inspiration: ↑venous return → ↑right-sided murmurs (TR, PR, PS, TS)

In fixed AS the pulse pressure increases post-PVC — this can differentiate the two at the bedside.

Clinical Pitfalls & Interpretation Errors

⚠️ Critical Errors to Avoid

  • Murmur loudness ≠ severity: Soft AS may be critical (low-flow). Silent MR may be acute and life-threatening. Never grade severity from murmur intensity alone.
  • Silent severe AR: Acute AR (aortic dissection, IE) — murmur may be brief and faint. The early diastolic murmur may be missed in a tachycardic patient.
  • Treating the murmur, not the patient: Innocent murmurs require no treatment. Murmur grading (Levine 1–6/6) reflects loudness, not haemodynamic significance.
  • Missing concurrent murmurs: AR + Austin-Flint can mimic MS. TR + MR can occur together (rheumatic). Always listen systematically at each area.
  • Ignoring dynamic changes: A murmur heard at rest may change significantly with mild exercise or position. HOCM is easily missed in a supine resting patient.
