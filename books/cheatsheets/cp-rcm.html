🩺 Definitions, Epidemiology & Clinical Presentation

Two diseases producing identical clinical syndromes of diastolic heart failure — distinguished only by careful haemodynamic and imaging analysis.

CP · Constrictive Pericarditis

What is it?

Chronic fibrous thickening and calcification of the pericardium that encases the heart in a rigid shell, preventing diastolic filling. The pericardium — normally 1–2 mm thick — becomes a 5–20+ mm rigid constraint on all four cardiac chambers.

definition
RCM · Restrictive Cardiomyopathy

What is it?

Intrinsic myocardial disease causing markedly abnormal diastolic filling due to increased myocardial stiffness. The pericardium is entirely normal. Ventricles are non-dilated with preserved EF but severely impaired relaxation and compliance.

Aetiology
Causes of CP
  • Post-cardiac surgery (25–30%): #1 identifiable cause in the West. Any cardiac surgery, most common after CABG/valve. Onset 6 months–15 years post-op.
  • Idiopathic/viral (40–50%): Post-pericarditis, often enteroviral
  • Post-radiation (10–20%): Mediastinal XRT for lymphoma, breast cancer
  • Tuberculosis: Predominant in Asia/Africa (up to 80% of CP in endemic regions); calcification common
  • Other: CTD (RA, SLE), uremia, bacterial/purulent, trauma, malignancy, asbestos
Key clinical insight: Post-cardiac surgery CP is often missed — dyspnoea is attributed to "post-op state." Any post-CABG/valve patient with elevated JVP and progressive dyspnoea must be evaluated for CP.
causes
Causes of RCM
  • Cardiac amyloidosis (most common in West): AL (plasma cell dyscrasia) or TTR (transthyretin — wild-type in elderly men, hereditary)
  • Cardiac sarcoidosis: Non-caseating granuloma infiltration; often presents as AV block or VT
  • Haemochromatosis: Iron deposition; responds to phlebotomy/chelation if early
  • Löffler's endocarditis / HES: Eosinophil infiltration → endocardial fibrosis → apical obliteration
  • Endomyocardial fibrosis (EMF): Tropical; sub-Saharan Africa predominant
  • Idiopathic: Sarcomeric gene mutations (MYH7, TNNI3, TNNT2); familial
  • Other: Fabry, glycogen storage, carcinoid, radiation myopathy
Clinical Presentation

🩺 Shared Symptoms — The Identical Syndrome

Right-sided HF symptoms (both):
  • Progressive exertional dyspnoea — universal presenting complaint
  • Peripheral oedema — bilateral, ascending
  • Ascites — often disproportionate (especially CP)
  • Hepatomegaly / congestive hepatopathy / jaundice
  • Fatigue from ↓ CO
Physical signs (both):
  • Elevated JVP — nearly universal; may be extreme
  • Kussmaul's sign — ↑ JVP with inspiration (classic in CP, seen in RCM too)
  • Prominent x and y descents (especially y in CP)
  • Hepatojugular reflux — positive
  • AF — common late complication
CP — Distinguishing Signs
  • Pericardial knock: High-pitched early diastolic sound, earlier than S₃, at LLSB/apex. Represents abrupt cessation of ventricular filling at the pericardial volume limit. Present in approximately one-third to one-half of patients with CP.
  • Pulsus paradoxus: Mild (~10 mmHg) in 20–30% of CP. Larger (>25 mmHg) suggests effusive-constrictive physiology.
  • Absence of pulmonary oedema: Both LV and RV diastolic pressures constrained equally — Frank pulmonary oedema is less common than in RCM, despite elevated filling pressures.
  • Pericardial calcification on CXR/CT: visible in 25–30%.
exam
RCM — Distinguishing Signs
  • S₃ gallop: Lower-pitched, slightly later than pericardial knock. Reflects impaired filling into non-compliant ventricle.
  • Loud S₄: Atrial gallop — atrial kick into stiff ventricle (sinus rhythm required).
  • Pulmonary oedema more likely: LVEDP often very high in amyloid → pulmonary venous hypertension.
  • Biatrial dilation on exam/CXR — massive LA/RA enlargement.
  • Systemic signs of underlying disease: Macroglossia, periorbital purpura (AL amyloid), lymphadenopathy (sarcoid), bronzing (haemochromatosis).
  • No pericardial knock.

🫀 Pathophysiology

Understanding the haemodynamic mechanisms is essential to correctly interpreting every diagnostic test.

Constrictive Pericarditis — The Rigid Shell

🔩 CP: Fixed Total Cardiac Volume — Four Key Consequences

1. Enhanced ventricular interdependence:
Fixed total cardiac volume → when one ventricle fills more, the other fills less. Inspiration → ↑ RV filling → septum shifts left → ↓ LV filling. Produces the septal bounce on echo and discordant LV/RV pressures at catheterization.
2. Dissociation of intrathoracic from intracardiac pressures:
Rigid pericardium insulates the heart from respiratory pressure changes. Inspiration ↓ intrathoracic P → ↓ pulmonary vein P, but LV diastolic P unchanged → ↓ pulmonary-to-LV pressure gradient → ↓ LV filling → ↓ mitral E velocity with inspiration (the basis of echo respiratory variation).
3. Early diastolic filling then abrupt stop:
Normal myocardial relaxation → rapid early filling → filling ABRUPTLY STOPS at pericardial volume limit → square root sign (dip-plateau) on pressure tracings; high E with short deceleration time on echo.
4. Equalization of diastolic pressures:
Pericardium constrains all chambers equally → LVEDP ≈ RVEDP ≈ PCWP ≈ RAP (within 5 mmHg). The haemodynamic hallmark at cardiac catheterization.
Ventricular Interdependence in CP — Fixed Pericardial Volume
Critical insight: The myocardium is NORMAL in CP. Pericardiectomy removes the constraint → haemodynamics normalize → potentially curative.
Restrictive Cardiomyopathy — The Stiff Myocardium

🧱 RCM: Intrinsic Myocardial Stiffness — Four Key Consequences

1. Elevated LVEDP with LVEDP >> RVEDP:
Infiltrated myocardium (amyloid, fibrosis, iron) → steep EDPVR → any ↑ volume → large ↑ pressure. LV typically stiffer than RV → LVEDP > RVEDP by >5 mmHg. Unlike CP where they equalize.
2. Normal ventricular interdependence:
Normal pericardium → not a fixed-volume container → NO enhanced ventricular interdependence → NO respiratory variation in Doppler flows → mitral E constant throughout respiration.
3. Impaired myocardial relaxation (↓ e'):
Diseased myocardium → impaired active relaxation → markedly reduced annular velocities on TDI (e' <5 cm/s). The myocardium cannot generate the suction that drives early diastolic filling normally. This is the basis of the low e' in RCM.
4. Biatrial dilation from chronically elevated filling pressures:
High LVEDP → LA hypertension → LA dilation. High RVEDP → RA hypertension → RA dilation. Both atria massively dilated — the anatomical hallmark of RCM. BNP markedly elevated reflecting myocardial stress.
Critical insight: The myocardium is diseased in RCM. Pericardiectomy does NOT help. Treatment targets the underlying cause; transplant for end-stage disease.

🔬 Echocardiography — First-Line Diagnostic

Systematic echo with TDI provides >85% diagnostic accuracy. Multiple modalities must be applied together.

Systematic echo protocol: (1) Pericardial thickness/calcification · (2) Ventricular size, wall thickness, EF · (3) Atrial size · (4) Septal motion with respiration · (5) Mitral inflow Doppler + respiratory variation · (6) TDI medial and lateral e' — the key discriminator · (7) Tricuspid inflow variation · (8) Hepatic vein Doppler · (9) IVC · (10) Speckle tracking strain
2D Findings
CP — 2D Echo
  • Pericardial thickening: Bright echogenic pericardium >4 mm; calcification = intensely bright with posterior acoustic shadowing
  • Normal LV systolic function: EF ≥55%; normal wall motion
  • "Tubular" LV shape: Constrained, may appear narrow in short axis
  • Mild LA enlargement; RA enlarged but less than in RCM
  • IVC dilated, <50% collapse with sniff (↑ RAP)
  • Flattened diastolic posterior wall on M-mode — abrupt stop of posterior motion in mid-diastole
2D
RCM — 2D Echo
  • Normal pericardium: <2 mm — important negative finding
  • Biatrial massive dilation (hallmark): Both LA and RA markedly enlarged; LA/LV volume ratio >1 is a clue
  • Wall thickness: ↑↑ in amyloid (>12–25 mm); "sparkling"/granular texture; normal in idiopathic/sarcoid
  • Normal or mildly ↓ EF (45–60%)
  • Apical obliteration (Löffler's/EMF)
  • Valvular involvement: Thickened valves in amyloid; EMF causes MR/TR
Important caveat on pericardial thickness: A normal pericardial thickness on echo (<4 mm) does not exclude constrictive pericarditis. Approximately 20% of surgically confirmed CP patients have pericardial thickness within normal limits, particularly in radiation-induced and post-surgical cases. Haemodynamic features (TDI e′, respiratory variation) and CMR are more reliable than thickness alone.
Septal Bounce — The CP-Specific 2D Sign

↔️ Septal Bounce / Ventricular Septal Shift

Mechanism: Inspiration → ↑ RV filling → RV expands → but total cardiac volume fixed → IVS shifts LEFT → abrupt leftward motion of septum at inspiration onset. On echo: characteristic "bounce" or "shudder" of IVS visible in real-time 2D and M-mode.
M-mode appearance: Abnormal posterior then abrupt anterior motion of IVS in early diastole = "W" shape of IVS. This combined with respiratory variation is highly specific for CP.
Absent in RCM: Normal pericardium → no volume constraint → no enhanced ventricular interdependence → no septal bounce.

Mitral Inflow Doppler — Respiratory Variation

🌊 The Most Important Doppler Finding

Measure peak mitral E velocity at end-expiration and beginning of inspiration. This is the most diagnostically powerful Doppler measurement.

CP
E variation ≥25%

Expiration: E high (e.g. 1.2 m/s) → Inspiration: E falls markedly (e.g. 0.85 m/s) = 29% variation

% variation = (E_exp − E_insp) / E_exp × 100 ≥ 25%

Pericardium insulates heart from thoracic pressure → ↓ pulmonary vein-to-LA gradient with inspiration → ↓ LV filling → ↓ E wave

RCM
E variation <10%

E velocity nearly constant throughout respiration (e.g. 1.4 m/s throughout)

% variation < 10% → no significant respiratory effect

Normal pericardium → normal pressure coupling → pulmonary vein and LV pressures change concordantly → filling gradient unchanged → E constant

Sensitivity/Specificity: >25% variation: Sn 84%, Sp 91% for CP vs RCM. Pitfalls: Rapid breathing underestimates variation. Volume overload can mask CP variation — repeat after diuresis. AF makes assessment unreliable (use >10-beat average). Significant MS causes spurious elevation.
Tissue Doppler Imaging — The Single Most Discriminating Test

⚡ TDI Mitral Annular e' — The Most Powerful Single Echo Feature

TDI e' measures myocardial velocity independently of loading conditions, reflecting intrinsic myocardial relaxation. This is the most powerful single measurement for CP vs RCM distinction.

CP — TDI
e' ≥ 8 cm/s (often ≥9)

Normal or supernormal (annulus paradoxus/reversus). Most studies use medial e' ≥8 cm/s; contemporary literature increasingly uses ≥9 cm/s as the threshold.

In CP, intrinsic myocardial relaxation is preserved. Because the myocardium relaxes normally, medial annular velocities remain relatively preserved despite impaired ventricular filling volume — TDI reflects myocardial velocity, not filling volume.

Annulus Reversus (highly suggestive of CP): Normal: lateral e' > medial e'. In CP: medial e' ≥ lateral e'. The lateral wall is tethered by the pericardium → its motion is relatively limited compared to the medial wall. This reversal of normal annular velocity ratio is highly suggestive of constrictive physiology in the appropriate clinical context.
E/e' ratio: normal–mildly ↑ (8–14)
TDI
RCM — TDI
e' < 5 cm/s

Markedly reduced at both lateral and medial annulus.

The myocardium is intrinsically diseased → impaired relaxation → very low annular velocities despite preserved EF. Amyloid infiltration, fibrosis, and iron deposits all reduce myocardial velocity.

E/e' ratio dramatically elevated: High transmitral E (high filling pressure) / very low e' (impaired relaxation) = very high E/e' ratio. E/e' >14 = significant diastolic dysfunction. In amyloid: E/e' may reach 20–40+.
E/e' ratio: ↑↑↑ (often >15–20, sometimes >30 in amyloid)
Clinical impact: Medial e' ≥8–9 cm/s → favours CP → consider pericardiectomy. Medial e' <5 cm/s → favours RCM → pursue RCM workup (amyloid screen, biopsy, transplant evaluation). This single number redirects the entire management pathway.
Other Doppler & Advanced Echo Findings

CP — Tricuspid & Hepatic Vein

  • Tricuspid E increase with inspiration (>40%): Opposite to mitral — inspiration ↑ RV filling → tricuspid E ↑. Discordant respiratory response (mitral ↓, tricuspid ↑) is specific for CP.
  • Hepatic vein expiratory diastolic reversal: Flow reversal during expiration (in RCM it's inspiratory).
CP Doppler constellation: Inspiration → ↓ mitral E + ↑ tricuspid E + expiratory hepatic vein reversal. This pattern together is essentially diagnostic of CP.

RCM — Tricuspid & Hepatic Vein

  • Minimal respiratory variation in both: Mitral and tricuspid E change <10% — concordant pattern.
  • Hepatic vein inspiratory reversal: Flow reversal occurs with inspiration in RCM.

Speckle Tracking — GLS & Strain

CP: LV-GLS normal (−18 to −22%). Myocardial mechanics fundamentally intact. RV free wall strain may be mildly reduced from pericardial tethering — can recover after pericardiectomy.
RCM — Amyloid "Apical Sparing": Amyloid densest at base → base/mid segments severely impaired in longitudinal strain → but apex relatively spared → "bull's-eye" map shows preserved apical strain with impaired basal/mid strain. This pattern is strongly suggestive of cardiac amyloidosis (Sn ~80%, Sp ~90%), but not pathognomonic — also observed in hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, chronic kidney disease, and hypertensive heart disease. Integrate with other features.

📈 Cardiac Cardiac Catheterization — Reference Invasive Haemodynamic Test

Used when non-invasive imaging (echo, CMR, CT) is inconclusive. In 2025, catheterization follows — it does not replace — comprehensive non-invasive evaluation.

When non-invasive imaging (echo, CMR, CT) is inconclusive, simultaneous right and left heart catheterization with respiratory pressure recordings provides definitive haemodynamic differentiation. In 2025, cath follows — not replaces — comprehensive non-invasive evaluation.

Indications: Echo inconclusive · Before pericardiectomy (confirm + exclude CAD) · Discordant echo findings · Effusive-constrictive physiology suspected · Pulmonary hypertension assessment
Criterion 1 — Diastolic Pressure Equalization

CP — The "5 mmHg Rule"

Classic CP signature:
RVEDP ≈ LVEDP ≈ PCWP ≈ RAP — all within 5 mmHg
Typical: RAP ~15 · RVEDP ~15 · PCWP ~16 · LVEDP ~16 mmHg
Simultaneous RV & LV pressure — CP (equalized EDP)
Square Root Sign (Dip-and-Plateau):

Diastolic RV (and LV) pressure shows: early rapid drop (dip) → filling abruptly stops at pericardial limit → pressure plateaus at elevated EDP = √ sign. Also seen in RCM — NOT specific for CP alone.

Square root sign — diastolic pressure (both CP & RCM)

RCM — Diastolic Pressures

RCM haemodynamic signature:
LVEDP is often modestly higher than RVEDP (difference variable, can be 2–10+ mmHg), and the close equalization seen in CP is typically absent. PCWP generally exceeds RAP by ≥5 mmHg. Note: in advanced amyloidosis, pressures may approach equalization, making differentiation harder.
Typical: RAP ~15 · RVEDP ~12 · PCWP ~24 · LVEDP ~25 mmHg

LV typically stiffer than RV in infiltrative disease → pressures diverge. LVEDP − RVEDP >5 mmHg is the most reliable catheterization criterion for RCM over CP.

⚠️ Effusive-Constrictive Pericarditis

A combined syndrome where pericardial effusion AND constriction coexist. After pericardiocentesis, effusion drains but haemodynamics do NOT normalize — the thickened visceral pericardium still constrains the heart.

Diagnostic criteria: RAP ≥10 mmHg before drainage → after complete pericardiocentesis: RAP remains ≥10 mmHg AND fails to fall >50%. Persisting dip-plateau after drainage.
Causes:
  • Malignancy (most common)
  • Idiopathic/viral
  • Post-radiation · Tuberculosis · Post-cardiac surgery
Treatment: Anti-inflammatory if active inflammation; pericardiectomy of the visceral pericardium if persistent constriction.

💊 Advanced Imaging, Diagnosis & Treatment

Multimodality imaging beyond echo, biomarkers, biopsy, and definitive management strategies for both conditions.

Advanced Imaging Modalities

🧲 Cardiac MRI (CMR) — The Anatomical Gold Standard for CP

  • Pericardial thickness: CMR accurately measures pericardial thickness (>4 mm = abnormal; >6 mm = strongly supports CP). Superior to echo and CT for thin pericardium detection. Normal pericardium ≤2 mm on CMR.
  • Late gadolinium enhancement (LGE) of pericardium: Pericardial LGE = active inflammation → predicts response to anti-inflammatory therapy. Non-enhancing CP → fibrotic, less likely to respond to medical therapy → proceed to surgery.
  • Real-time cine CMR (respiratory gating off): Visualizes septal bounce and ventricular interdependence in real time during free breathing — even more sensitive than echo for this finding.
  • Tagging sequences: Demonstrate pericardial-myocardial adhesion (myocardium moves WITH pericardium rather than sliding freely) — pathognomonic for CP when present.
  • T1/T2 mapping: Detects myocardial inflammation/infiltration; elevated T1 in amyloid; elevated T2 in myocarditis/sarcoid.
  • Myocardial LGE patterns in RCM: Amyloid: diffuse subendocardial/transmural LGE with abnormal nulling; Sarcoid: patchy mid-myocardial or subepicardial LGE (RV insertion points); Haemochromatosis: ↓T2* (iron); Idiopathic RCM: often no LGE.

📡 CT — Best for Pericardial Calcification

  • Pericardial calcification: CT is the definitive modality for detecting and quantifying pericardial calcification — far superior to echo and CMR. Presence of significant calcification in appropriate clinical context = CP confirmed (essentially pathognomonic when circumferential).
  • Pericardial thickness: Accurately measured; >4 mm supports CP.
  • Coronary anatomy: CT coronary angiography or formal coronary angiography (pre-pericardiectomy) to assess for CAD — very common comorbidity.
  • Mediastinal anatomy: Surgical planning — extent of calcification, relationship to coronary arteries and great vessels (particularly important when calcification encases the coronary arteries).
  • Note for RCM: CT shows normal pericardium, possible biatrial dilation, may show myocardial calcification in haemochromatosis.
CT vs CMR: Use CT when calcification is suspected (pre-surgical planning); use CMR for soft tissue characterization, myocardial involvement, and when pericardial thickness is near-normal (CMR more sensitive for thin but pathological pericardium).

🚩 Red Flags for Cardiac Amyloidosis — Screen These Patients

Systemic clues:
  • Bilateral carpal tunnel syndrome — especially if bilateral/recurrent in an older man
  • Lumbar spinal stenosis (ATTR deposits in ligamentum flavum)
  • Biceps tendon rupture ("Popeye sign") — spontaneous, atraumatic
  • Macroglossia — pathognomonic for AL amyloidosis
  • Periorbital purpura ("raccoon eyes") — AL amyloid in periorbital capillaries
  • Peripheral / autonomic neuropathy — especially in hereditary ATTR
  • Nephrotic-range proteinuria — AL amyloid renal involvement
Cardiac clues:
  • Low-voltage ECG with thick LV walls — voltage/mass discordance; ~70% sensitive for amyloid
  • Pseudo-infarct Q waves without coronary disease (inferior/lateral leads)
  • Apical sparing on GLS — strongly suggestive (not pathognomonic)
  • Unexplained HFpEF in patients >65 with thick walls — ATTR until proven otherwise
  • Thickened valves + granular sparkling myocardium on echo
  • Rapidly progressive aortic stenosis in elderly — ATTR amyloid co-exists with AS in ~15%
  • Intolerance to ACEi/ARBs/CCBs/digoxin — amyloid heart is exquisitely sensitive
⁹⁹ᵐTc-PYP rule: Any patient ≥60 with unexplained thick LV walls (≥12 mm) + HF symptoms → PYP scan. Grade 2–3 uptake without monoclonal protein = ATTR confirmed without biopsy. This is a game-changing test.

💎 Cardiac Amyloidosis — The Most Important RCM in 2025

Cardiac amyloidosis has emerged as the most prevalent and clinically important form of RCM. Two main types with very different prognosis and treatment:

AL (light-chain) Amyloidosis:
  • Plasma cell dyscrasia produces misfolded immunoglobulin light chains → amyloid fibrils deposited in heart, kidney, nerves, liver
  • Prognosis: Median survival 6–12 months untreated; 5 years with chemotherapy
  • Treatment: Chemotherapy targeting the plasma cell clone (daratumumab + bortezomib + dexamethasone ± cyclophosphamide); stem cell transplant in selected patients; heart transplant rarely (requires preceding BMT to eliminate the amyloid source)
  • Diagnosis: Fat pad biopsy (70% sensitivity); bone marrow biopsy; cardiac biopsy; serum free light chains
Red flags for AL amyloid: Macroglossia, periorbital purpura ("raccoon eyes"), carpal tunnel syndrome, nephrotic-range proteinuria + cardiac RCM = AL amyloidosis until proven otherwise.
ATTR (transthyretin) Amyloidosis:
  • Misfolded transthyretin (TTR) protein, either wild-type (age-related, predominantly men >70 years) or hereditary (Val122Ile most common in African Americans)
  • Prognosis: Better than AL; median survival 2–6 years untreated; significantly improved with tafamidis
  • Treatment: Tafamidis (Vyndaqel) — TTR stabilizer; significantly reduces mortality and hospitalization (ATTR-ACT trial, 30% relative mortality reduction); Patisiran/inotersen — RNA interference silencers for hereditary ATTR; Acoramidis — newer TTR stabilizer
  • Clue: ⁹⁹ᵐTc-PYP scintigraphy (bone scan) — Grade 2–3 cardiac uptake without monoclonal protein = ATTR diagnosis without biopsy
Tafamidis clinical impact: First disease-modifying therapy for any RCM. All patients >60 with unexplained LV wall thickening (≥12 mm), preserved EF, and characteristic echo/CMR findings should be screened for ATTR before attributing findings to hypertension.

🔬 Endomyocardial Biopsy (EMB) — When and Why

Indications in RCM:
  • Confirm amyloidosis (Congo red stain → apple-green birefringence; electron microscopy → fibril pattern; mass spectrometry → typing AL vs ATTR)
  • Confirm sarcoidosis (non-caseating granulomas) when other sites inaccessible
  • Confirm haemochromatosis (Prussian blue iron stain)
  • Classify idiopathic RCM (fibrosis pattern)
  • Exclude myocarditis mimicking RCM
Yield and limitations:
  • Amyloid: 90–100% sensitivity (typical sampling 5 pieces from RV septum)
  • Sarcoid: 20–40% sensitivity (patchy distribution) — PET/CT-guided biopsy ↑ yield
  • Haemochromatosis: High (iron deposits diffuse)
  • Complication rate: 0.5–1% major (cardiac perforation, tamponade, AV block, fistula)
  • Not indicated in CP — diagnosis is pericardial, not myocardial
Fat pad biopsy first: In suspected AL amyloidosis, abdominal subcutaneous fat pad biopsy is positive in 70% and avoids cardiac biopsy risk. If negative + high suspicion → EMB.
Diagnostic Algorithm — Step-by-Step Approach

📐 Practical Diagnostic Pathway: CP vs RCM

1
Clinical history and examination
History of cardiac surgery, radiation, TB, CTD → raises CP pre-test probability. Macroglossia, periorbital purpura, neuropathy → AL amyloid. Elderly man, aortic stenosis, bilateral CTS → ATTR amyloid. Family history of CMP → idiopathic/hereditary RCM. Calculate pre-test probability before imaging.
2
Biomarkers: BNP, troponin, SPEP, free light chains, ferritin
Markedly elevated BNP (>1000 pg/mL) strongly favours RCM. Monoclonal protein → AL amyloid workup. Very high ferritin + ↑ transferrin sat. → haemochromatosis. Normal/mildly elevated BNP compatible with CP.
3
Comprehensive echocardiography with Doppler and TDI
Septal bounce + medial e' ≥8 + mitral E variation ≥25% + pericardial thickening → CP likely. Biatrial dilation + e' <5 + E/e' >15 + wall thickening ("sparkling") + no septal bounce → RCM likely. Assess all parameters systematically. Strain analysis: apical sparing → amyloid.
4
Cardiac CT and/or CMR
CT: pericardial calcification (virtually diagnostic of CP when circumferential). CMR: pericardial thickness + LGE pattern (pericardial vs myocardial) + tagging (pericardio-myocardial adhesion) + T1/T2 mapping (infiltration). ⁹⁹ᵐTc-PYP scan: Grade 2–3 + no monoclonal protein = ATTR amyloidosis confirmed without biopsy. FDG-PET: active sarcoidosis.
5
Cardiac catheterization (if still uncertain)
Simultaneous LV/RV pressures during respiration: discordant (LV↓ when RV↑) = CP. Diastolic pressure equalization (LVEDP − RVEDP <5 mmHg) = CP. Concordant + LVEDP >> RVEDP = RCM. Volume loading if resting haemodynamics inconclusive. Coronary angiography pre-pericardiectomy.
6
Endomyocardial biopsy (if RCM confirmed but cause unclear)
Perform EMB to type RCM if non-invasive workup inconclusive. Fat pad biopsy first in AL amyloid. EMB for sarcoid (PET-guided), haemochromatosis, and idiopathic RCM classification. Not needed if ATTR confirmed by PYP scintigraphy.
Treatment
CP — Treatment

🔪 Pericardiectomy — The Only Cure

  • Complete (radical) pericardiectomy: The definitive treatment. Remove as much of the visceral and parietal pericardium as safely possible — from phrenic to phrenic nerve. Incomplete resection = inadequate — residual constriction is common if <75% removed.
  • Surgical approach: Median sternotomy (preferred — allows complete resection) or lateral thoracotomy. CPB standby — may be needed if pericardium adherent to epicardium or myocardium.
  • Mortality: 5–10% (experienced centres: 3–5%). Higher in: radiation-induced CP (myocardial damage), advanced disease (CI <1.5), severe calcification encasing coronaries.
  • Long-term outcomes: 70–90% of patients significantly improve. Haemodynamic improvement may be delayed 3–6 months post-op (myocardial adaptation needed). 10-year survival ~50–70%.
  • Medical therapy (pre-operative / inflammatory CP):
    • NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600 mg tid) + Colchicine 0.5 mg bid × 3 months (reduces recurrence/progression)
    • Active pericardial LGE on CMR → anti-inflammatory before surgery
    • Corticosteroids if NSAIDs fail or autoimmune/TB cause
    • TB: anti-TB therapy + prednisolone for tuberculous CP
  • Avoid: Diuretics in excess (preload-dependent; over-diuresis → ↓ CO). Negative inotropes, vasodilators (further ↓ CO).
Pericardiectomy timing: Earlier is better — severe calcification, long-standing disease, and radiation-induced CP have worse outcomes. Operate before severe myocardial atrophy or hepatic fibrosis develops. NYHA III–IV with confirmed CP = prompt surgical referral.
Rx
RCM — Treatment

💊 Disease-Specific + Supportive Therapy

  • Diuretics (mainstay of symptomatic relief): Loop diuretics (furosemide) ± aldosterone antagonist. Careful titration — over-diuresis ↓ preload → ↓ CO (preload-dependent diastolic function). Aim: euvolaemia, not aggressively dry.
  • Heart rate control: Target HR 60–70 bpm — longer diastole → better filling. β-blockers or ivabradine cautiously. Avoid tachycardia (shortens diastolic filling time).
  • Anticoagulation: Warfarin/DOAC — for AF (high prevalence), LA thrombus (Löffler's, EMF), or intracardiac thrombus.
  • Amyloid-specific:
    • AL: Daratumumab + Bortezomib + Dexamethasone (D-VD) — first-line; stem cell transplant if eligible
    • ATTR: Tafamidis 61 mg/day (wild-type and hereditary Val30Met); Patisiran or Inotersen (hereditary ATTR with polyneuropathy); Acoramidis (emerging)
    • Avoid CCBs (contraindicated in amyloid — worsen hypotension and heart block)
    • Avoid digoxin (binds amyloid fibrils → toxicity at low levels)
    • Avoid ACEi/ARBs in advanced AL (hypotension)
  • Sarcoidosis: Prednisolone 40 mg/day → taper over 6–12 months. ICD for VT/AV block (>3° or type 2° Mobitz II). CRT if LBBB + low EF.
  • Haemochromatosis: Phlebotomy (weekly until ferritin <50 ng/mL) or deferasirox/deferoxamine chelation. Can partially reverse cardiomyopathy if detected early.
  • Löffler's/HES: Hydroxyurea, imatinib (FIP1L1-PDGFRA+ cases), corticosteroids; anticoagulation for apical thrombus.
  • Heart transplantation: For end-stage RCM unresponsive to medical therapy. AL amyloid requires preceding plasma cell clone eradication (BMT) to prevent amyloid recurrence in donor heart. ATTR transplant outcomes good (but limited by age/comorbidities). LVAD bridge to transplant problematic in RCM (small cavity, suction events).
Critical Pitfalls & Teaching Points

⚠️ Common Diagnostic Pitfall Scenarios

Clinical Situation How it complicates diagnosis What to do
Severe tricuspid regurgitation Prominent v wave and rapid y descent on JVP mimics CP; dilated IVC; right-sided congestion Echo: evaluate TR severity and RV function. CP and severe TR can co-exist. Cath differentiates.
Atrial fibrillation Masks respiratory Doppler variation (mitral E varies with RR interval, not respiration). Cannot use standard variation criteria. Use >10 beat average; assess septal bounce, TDI e′, and cath haemodynamics instead of respiratory Doppler.
Mechanical ventilation Positive pressure ventilation reverses normal respiratory physiology — all respiratory Doppler indices unreliable Perform echo during brief spontaneous breathing trial; or defer to cath during spontaneous breathing.
COPD / severe obstructive lung disease Exaggerated intrathoracic pressure swings → large respiratory Doppler variation even without CP; may produce apparent "septal bounce" Context: elevated JVP? Pericardial thickness on CMR? TDI e′? Cath discordance (not respiratory variation alone).
Prior cardiac surgery (without CP) Pericardial scarring and adhesions may produce annulus reversus (medial e′ ≥ lateral e′) without true constrictive physiology; pericardial thickening without haemodynamic compromise Require haemodynamic evidence (respiratory variation AND cath equalization) before diagnosing CP post-surgery — anatomy alone is insufficient.
Radiation heart disease Can produce simultaneous constrictive pericarditis AND myocardial restriction — truly mixed physiology. Both e′ and respiratory variation may be intermediate. CMR: distinguish pericardial vs myocardial LGE. Cath: volume loading. Low threshold for surgical exploration + biopsy if uncertain.
Mitral annular calcification (MAC) Annulus calcification restricts annular motion → reduces lateral e′ selectively → may mimic annulus reversus pattern without CP Evaluate full clinical context. Significant MAC on echo; CMR pericardium; respiratory variation; cath if needed.
Hypovolaemia masking CP Under-filled patients may not show respiratory variation — pressure equalization at low filling volumes Reassess after adequate volume loading; cath with volume challenge (500–1000 mL NS) unmasks CP.
Advanced amyloidosis with near-equalized pressures In end-stage cardiac amyloidosis, LVEDP and RVEDP can approach equalization, mimicking CP haemodynamics TDI e′ (markedly low in amyloid); CMR LGE pattern; PYP scan; EMB. Clinical context (amyloid features, BNP markedly elevated).

⚠️ Common Diagnostic Mistakes (Continued)

  • Attributing post-surgical dyspnoea to "cardiac surgery" without considering CP: CP develops 6 months–15 years post-op. Any post-CABG/valve patient with elevated JVP and dyspnoea needs echo ± CMR to exclude CP.
  • Relying on pericardial thickness alone: 20% of CP has normal pericardial thickness (<2 mm) on echo. Haemodynamics and TDI are more reliable than thickness measurements alone.
  • Accepting low BNP as evidence against RCM: BNP can be relatively low in early/compensated RCM. It is the very high BNP that argues against CP, not a normal BNP that excludes RCM.
  • Missing amyloidosis as cause of "HFpEF": ATTR amyloidosis mimics HFpEF. In any patient >60 with HFpEF, thick walls, and low-voltage ECG → screen with ⁹⁹ᵐTc-PYP. Treat with tafamidis if ATTR confirmed.
  • Operating on RCM patient (misdiagnosed as CP): Pericardiectomy on an RCM patient does not improve haemodynamics and carries surgical mortality without benefit. Pre-operative EMB or very thorough non-invasive workup is essential in all borderline cases.
  • Using CCBs or digoxin in cardiac amyloidosis: Both are potentially dangerous — CCBs bind amyloid fibrils → negative inotrope effect amplified; digoxin binds amyloid → toxicity at low levels.

🎯 High-Yield Teaching Points

  • TDI e' is the single most useful echo test: e' ≥8 = CP (normal myocardium); e' <5 = RCM (diseased myocardium). This one number often determines the entire management pathway.
  • Discordant LV/RV pressure response at catheterization is the gold standard: LV↓ when RV↑ with inspiration = CP. This pattern reflects enhanced ventricular interdependence and is the most specific single haemodynamic feature.
  • "Annulus reversus" in CP: Medial e' ≥ lateral e' = highly suggestive of constrictive physiology (in appropriate clinical context — also possible post-cardiac surgery, mitral annular calcification). Normal physiology and RCM both show lateral > medial e'.
  • Diastolic pressure equalization ≠ square root sign: The square root sign is seen in BOTH conditions. Only equalization (LVEDP − RVEDP <5 mmHg) is specific for CP.
  • ⁹⁹ᵐTc-PYP scintigraphy = ATTR without biopsy: Grade 2–3 uptake + absence of monoclonal protein = ATTR diagnosis with >99% specificity. Game-changing for elderly patients with suspected ATTR amyloid.
  • Early pericardiectomy = better outcomes: Once severe calcification encases the coronary arteries, pericardiectomy becomes technically treacherous. Operate before the window closes.
  • Complete pericardiectomy, not partial: Incomplete removal is a major cause of suboptimal outcomes. Phrenic-to-phrenic resection is the standard. CPB improves completeness in difficult cases.
  • Post-pericardiectomy haemodynamic improvement is delayed: The myocardium needs 3–6 months to remodel and recover from chronic constraint. Patients need reassurance and continued medical support in this period.
ECG Findings
CP — ECG
  • Non-specific: No pathognomonic ECG finding
  • Common: Low-voltage QRS (pericardial fluid / fibrous tissue attenuates signal)
  • Atrial fibrillation: Common late finding (both CP and RCM)
  • P-wave abnormalities: LA enlargement pattern (broad, notched P in II; deep negative terminal deflection in V1)
  • T-wave changes: Non-specific T-wave inversions
  • Electrical alternans: Only if concurrent large effusion
ECG
RCM — ECG (disease-specific)
  • Amyloid hallmark — Low voltage + thick walls: QRS voltage paradoxically LOW despite increased wall thickness on echo. This "voltage-mass discordance" is ~70% sensitive for cardiac amyloid.
  • Pseudo-infarct pattern (amyloid): Pathological Q waves (especially inferior/lateral leads) without coronary disease — amyloid deposits cause electrical "dead zones"
  • Conduction disease (sarcoid): Complete AV block, LBBB, RBBB — may be presenting manifestation of cardiac sarcoidosis; ICD warranted
  • VT (sarcoid, haemochromatosis): Sustained VT as presenting arrhythmia — ICD required
  • Atrial fibrillation: Very common in all forms of RCM (massive atrial dilation)
  • Short PR, delta wave: Rare; Fabry disease can mimic pre-excitation

The three questions that guide every CP vs RCM evaluation:
1. Is the pericardium thickened/calcified? (CT/CMR/echo)
2. Is the myocardium moving normally? (TDI e' ≥8 = CP; e' <5 = RCM)
3. Is there respiratory dissociation between intrathoracic and intracardiac pressures? (Doppler variation >25% or cath discordance = CP)
