Trauma arrival algorithm
Immediately life-threatening — the six killers

⚡ Tension pneumothorax

Shift of mediastinum; obstructive shock. Needle decompression immediately — do not wait for CXR.

⚡ Open pneumothorax

Sucking chest wound; immediate occlusive dressing with flutter valve or 3-sided seal.

⚡ Massive haemothorax

>1500 mL blood; immediate chest tube; consider thoracotomy if >200 mL/h continues.

⚡ Flail chest

≥3 consecutive ribs fractured in ≥2 places; paradoxical motion; intubation if respiratory failure.

⚡ Cardiac tamponade

Beck's triad; obstructive shock; eFAST confirms. Pericardiocentesis or ED thoracotomy.

⚡ Airway obstruction

Assess immediately; jaw thrust / chin lift; suction; definitive airway if GCS ≤8 or obstruction.

Epidemiology & mechanism

🚗 Blunt trauma

  • Most common in high-income countries — MVA, fall from height, crush
  • Aortic injury (isthmus, decelerating mechanism)
  • Blunt cardiac injury (sternal impact)
  • Rib fractures, flail chest, pulmonary contusion
  • Diaphragmatic rupture (left > right)

🔫 Penetrating trauma

  • Gunshot wounds (GSW): High-velocity; tract injury unpredictable
  • Stab wounds: Lower velocity; more predictable tract; cardiac injury common
  • 80% of penetrating cardiac trauma: RV (anterior exposure)
  • Pericardial tamponade more likely with stab than GSW

💨 Airway Management (A)

Airway assessment, difficult airway, surgical airway, and cardiothoracic-specific considerations.

Airway assessment

🔍 Initial airway evaluation

Signs of airway compromise

  • Agitation / altered consciousness — hypoxia until proven otherwise
  • Stridor, gurgling, hoarseness
  • Use of accessory muscles; paradoxical breathing
  • Facial / mandibular fractures; blood in oropharynx
  • Tracheal deviation (late sign — tension PTX / massive haematoma)
  • Subcutaneous emphysema (tracheobronchial injury)

Indications for definitive airway

  • GCS ≤8
  • Inability to maintain airway patency
  • Risk of aspiration (blood, secretions)
  • Impending airway compromise (expanding haematoma, angioedema)
  • Respiratory failure (RR >30, SpO₂ <90% on O₂)
  • Planned general anaesthesia / operative intervention

Surgical airway
Cricothyrotomy
  • Indication: Cannot intubate / cannot oxygenate
  • Landmark: cricothyroid membrane (between thyroid and cricoid cartilage)
  • Technique: Horizontal stab incision → dilate → insert 6.0 cuffed ETT or dedicated cric tube
  • Confirm with waveform capnography
  • Convert to surgical tracheostomy within 24–72 h (subglottic stenosis risk)
Contraindications: Direct laryngeal / tracheal injury; age <8 years (use needle cric as bridge).
airway
Cardiothoracic Considerations
  • Mediastinal haematoma: Expanding after great vessel injury may compress airway; intubation can precipitate rupture — plan ahead
  • Tracheobronchial injury: Maintain spontaneous ventilation if possible; bronchoscopy-guided intubation; single-lung ventilation of unaffected side
  • SVC syndrome: Head / neck venous distension; airway oedema; intubate early before deterioration
  • Post-sternotomy: Re-entry via existing wound may be needed for resuscitative purposes
Tracheobronchial injury

Tracheobronchial Injury

IMMEDIATE
Mechanism
  • Blunt: sudden deceleration; trachea and mainstem bronchi most vulnerable within 2 cm of carina
  • Penetrating: GSW or stab to neck / chest
Clinical findings
  • Haemoptysis
  • Subcutaneous emphysema (massive)
  • Tension pneumothorax not resolving with chest tube
  • Persistent large air leak
Imaging
  • CXR: pneumomediastinum, persistent PTX, "fallen lung" sign
  • CT: tracheal defect, mediastinal air
  • Bronchoscopy: definitive diagnosis and guides intubation
Management
  • Single-lung ventilation past the injury
  • Thoracic surgery: primary repair; resection if large defect
  • Right posterolateral thoracotomy for right-sided injuries
  • Median sternotomy for tracheal/carinal injuries

🫁 Breathing & Ventilation (B)

Life-threatening thoracic injuries — diagnosis, immediate management, surgical decision.

Tension Pneumothorax

IMMEDIATE — DO NOT DELAY
Pathophysiology

Air enters pleural space and cannot exit → progressive accumulation → mediastinal shift → compression of contralateral lung and great veins → ↓ VR → obstructive shock → cardiac arrest

Clinical diagnosis
  • Respiratory distress
  • Absent breath sounds (ipsilateral)
  • Hypotension / obstructive shock
  • Tracheal deviation (late and unreliable)
  • JVD (may be absent with hypovolaemia)
Ultrasound (eFAST)
  • Absent lung sliding
  • Barcode sign on M-mode (absent seashore sign)
  • Lung point — diagnostic (interface of PTX and adherent lung)
  • No B-lines
Management
  • Needle decompression: 2nd ICS mid-clavicular line (or 4th/5th ICS anterior axillary line if obese)
  • Immediate chest tube (28–32 Fr) after needle
  • In cardiac arrest: bilateral finger thoracostomies first
  • Do not wait for CXR

Massive Haemothorax

IMMEDIATE
Definition

>1500 mL blood in pleural cavity (or >200 mL/h for 2–4 h after chest tube). Sources: intercostal/mammary vessels (most common), pulmonary laceration, major vessel injury.

Diagnosis
  • Dullness to percussion (ipsilateral)
  • Absent breath sounds
  • Haemorrhagic shock
  • CXR: opacification of hemithorax
  • eFAST: anechoic fluid in dependent costophrenic angle
Immediate management
  • Large-bore IV × 2; activate MTP
  • Chest tube (36 Fr): 5th ICS anterior axillary line — autotransfusion if available
  • Record initial drainage and hourly output
  • Measure drainage before removing tube
Surgical indications
  • Initial drainage >1500 mL
  • >200 mL/h for 2–4 hours
  • Haemodynamic instability despite resuscitation
  • Clotted haemothorax (VATS or thoracotomy)
  • Approach: posterolateral thoracotomy

Open Pneumothorax (Sucking Chest Wound)

IMMEDIATE
Pathophysiology

If wound >⅔ tracheal diameter, air preferentially enters through wound (lower resistance). Impaired ventilation → hypoxia.

Clinical findings
  • Visible chest wound with audible air movement ("sucking")
  • Respiratory distress
  • May rapidly develop tension PTX
Management
  • 3-sided occlusive dressing (flutter valve effect) — or commercial chest seal
  • Chest tube at separate site
  • Never fully seal without chest tube — risk of tension PTX
  • Definitive: OR closure of defect
Notes

Penetrating wounds ≥3 cm require formal surgical closure. Large defects may require myoplasty or mesh reconstruction.

Flail Chest

URGENT
Definition

≥3 consecutive ribs fractured in ≥2 places → free-floating segment moving paradoxically with respiration. Underlying pulmonary contusion is the main driver of hypoxia.

Clinical findings
  • Paradoxical chest wall movement
  • Splinting, tachypnoea
  • Crepitus / bony instability on palpation
  • CT: most sensitive for rib fractures and contusion extent
Management
  • O₂; multimodal analgesia (intercostal nerve block, epidural)
  • Intubation + PEEP if RR >30, SpO₂ <90%, fatigue
  • Aggressive physiotherapy; secretion clearance
  • Restrictive fluid strategy (avoid pulmonary oedema)
Surgical rib fixation (ORIF)
  • Indications: ventilator dependence; severe pain; operative chest exploration for other reasons
  • Titanium rib plates / bars
  • ↓ ventilator days, ↓ pneumonia, ↓ ICU stay

Massive transfusion & haemostatic resuscitation
MTP Activation & Blood Products
  • Activation criteria: Shock Index >1.4; anticipated >10 units pRBC in 24 h; ABC score ≥2
  • Ratio: pRBC : FFP : Platelets = 1 : 1 : 1
  • Tranexamic acid: 1 g IV over 10 min (within 3 h of injury; CRASH-2 / CRASH-3); repeat 1 g over 8 h
  • Calcium: 1 g CaCl₂ per 4 units pRBC (citrate chelation)
  • Fibrinogen: Target >1.5 g/L; Cryoprecipitate or fibrinogen concentrate
  • Permissive hypotension: Target SBP 80–90 mmHg until surgical haemostasis (avoid in TBI)
MTP
Viscoelastic Testing (ROTEM / TEG)
  • EXTEM CT: Clotting time — ↑ = factor deficiency (FFP)
  • FIBTEM A5/A10: Fibrin clot strength — ↓ = fibrinogen deficiency (cryoprecipitate)
  • EXTEM A10/A15: Early amplitude — ↓ = platelet/fibrinogen deficiency
  • EXTEM ML: Lysis — ↑ = hyperfibrinolysis → tranexamic acid
  • Goal-directed therapy: superior to empirical ratio-based transfusion for coagulopathy correction
ROTEM-guided: Reduces FFP use, ↓ transfusion-associated complications, enables targeted factor replacement.

🫀 Cardiac Trauma

Blunt cardiac injury, penetrating cardiac trauma, tamponade, myocardial and pericardial rupture.

Cardiac Tamponade

IMMEDIATE
Beck's triad
  • Hypotension
  • Muffled heart sounds
  • Distended neck veins (JVD)
  • Complete triad present in only 10–40% of trauma cases
eFAST / Echo
  • Pericardial effusion (anechoic rim)
  • RV diastolic collapse (most sensitive sign)
  • RA systolic collapse
  • IVC plethora (>2.1 cm, no respiratory collapse)
  • Mitral E variation >25% with respiration
ECG
  • Sinus tachycardia
  • Electrical alternans (QRS amplitude varies beat-to-beat — pathognomonic)
  • Low-voltage QRS
  • PR depression; ST changes
Management
  • Pericardiocentesis: Subxiphoid approach; echo-guided if time allows; needle/sheath → remove 30–50 mL → significant haemodynamic improvement
  • ED thoracotomy: Haemodynamic arrest or <5 min CPR
  • OR sternotomy: Stable enough for controlled repair

Penetrating cardiac trauma

🔫 Penetrating cardiac trauma — anatomical priorities

Chambers by frequency

  • Right ventricle (40–45%): Most anterior — stab wounds most common cause; often tamponade; best prognosis
  • Left ventricle (35%): GSW more common; thicker wall may tamponade tract; worse prognosis
  • Right atrium (10%): Low-pressure; may bleed massively
  • Left atrium (5%): Posterior — difficult access; usually fatal

Surgical approach by haemodynamics

  • Haemodynamic arrest / <5 min CPR: ED resuscitative thoracotomy (left anterolateral)
  • Haemodynamically unstable: Immediate OR — median sternotomy
  • Haemodynamically stable + positive eFAST: Expedited OR — sternotomy; pericardial window first if uncertain
  • Coronary injury: Ligation acceptable for distal vessels; CABG for proximal LAD
Prehospital: Penetrating cardiac trauma + CPR for <15 min → survivable with ED thoracotomy. Blunt cardiac arrest → <5 min. Beyond these limits: resuscitation is futile.

Aortic isthmus: 90% of blunt aortic injuries occur at the aortic isthmus (ligamentum arteriosum) — at the junction of the mobile aortic arch and the fixed descending thoracic aorta.
TEVAR vs open repair
TEVAR (preferred for most Grade III–IV)
  • Advantages: Avoids thoracotomy; ↓ spinal cord ischaemia risk; ↓ blood loss; suitable for polytrauma
  • Landing zone: ≥2 cm proximal and distal seal
  • Zone 2 coverage: May require left subclavian revascularisation (carotid-subclavian bypass if dominant vertebral or LIMA graft)
  • Contraindications: Unsuitable anatomy (small aorta, tortuous access); active infection; long-term durability concern in young patients
Timing: Delayed repair (24–72 h) acceptable if medically manageable; allows resuscitation of other injuries. Emergency repair if haemodynamically unstable.
repair
Open Repair (selected cases)
  • Unsuitable anatomy for TEVAR (hypoplastic aorta, young patient)
  • Concomitant cardiac injury requiring sternotomy/bypass
  • Left posterolateral thoracotomy (4th ICS)
  • Left heart bypass (LHB) or CPB to protect spinal cord and viscera
  • Clamp-and-sew: acceptable if <30 min cross-clamp time
  • Spinal cord monitoring: MEP/SSEP; CSF drainage if paraplegia risk
Spinal cord protection: CSF drain; MAP >90 mmHg; hypothermia; minimise cross-clamp time; LHB preferred over clamp-and-sew for complex repairs.
Great vessel injuries

🫀 SVC & Innominate vein

  • Penetrating > blunt; often associated with mediastinal haematoma
  • SVC: Tamponade-like presentation; JVD; facial oedema
  • Surgical access: median sternotomy ± right anterolateral thoracotomy
  • Repair: primary repair; patch venoplasty; SVC reconstruction (PTFE graft)
  • Temporary balloon control if major haemorrhage

🫁 Pulmonary artery & veins

  • Usually penetrating; often immediately fatal
  • Pulmonary artery control: temporary hilar twist (rotate lung 180°) → controls PA and PV simultaneously
  • Pneumonectomy: if hilar destruction; carry high mortality (>50%)
  • Pulmonary vein injury: massive haemothorax; arterial bleeding on chest tube
  • Lung-sparing repair preferred when anatomically feasible

🔬 Imaging & eFAST

Extended FAST exam, chest X-ray, CT, and TEE in thoracic trauma.

eFAST examination — windows and findings

🔊 eFAST protocol — 6 views

VIEW 1 · RUQ (Morrison's pouch)
  • Hepatorenal interface
  • Anechoic fluid = haemoperitoneum
  • Most sensitive abdominal window
VIEW 2 · LUQ (Koller's pouch)
  • Splenorenal interface
  • Left pleural space (above diaphragm)
  • Anechoic = haemothorax or haemoperitoneum
VIEW 3 · Pelvic (Douglas's pouch)
  • Most dependent abdominal space
  • Fluid posterior to bladder / uterus
VIEW 4 · Pericardial (subxiphoid)
  • Anechoic rim around heart = effusion
  • RV diastolic collapse = tamponade
  • Parasternal long-axis as alternative
VIEW 5 · Right pleura (2nd ICS)
  • Lung sliding: Normal = rules out PTX at that point
  • Absent sliding: PTX or pleural adhesion
  • Barcode sign (M-mode) = PTX
  • Lung point = diagnostic for PTX
VIEW 6 · Left pleura (2nd ICS)
  • Mirror of right side
  • B-lines: indicate pulmonary oedema or contusion
  • PLAPS (posterolateral): haemothorax
Sensitivity / specificity: eFAST for haemoperitoneum: 78–96% / 95–100%. For haemothorax: 92% / 99.7%. For PTX: 86–98% / 97–100% (superior to supine CXR for PTX). For pericardial effusion: 96–100% / 97–100%.

CT & TEE in thoracic trauma
CT — Indications & Protocol
  • Haemodynamically stable only — CT is not for unstable patients
  • Pan-scan (head + C-spine + chest + abdomen/pelvis): Standard for significant blunt trauma in stable patients
  • CT aortography: Widened mediastinum; deceleration mechanism; aortic injury suspected
  • Delayed venous phase: mesenteric / solid organ injury
  • Pericardial window by CT: >50 mL effusion = haemopericardium until proven otherwise
imaging
TEE in Trauma — Cardiothoracic Perspective
  • Intraoperative: Gold standard for cardiac function during thoracic trauma repair
  • Aortic injury characterisation: Intimal flap, pseudoaneurysm, aortic regurgitation — superior to TTE in intubated patients
  • Valvular injuries: Acute MR (papillary rupture), AR (aortic dissection), TR after blunt trauma
  • Ventricular function: Real-time monitoring during TEVAR; post-resuscitative thoracotomy assessment
  • Haemodynamic instability in ICU: TEE > TTE (better windows, no chest wall interference)
TEE is underutilised in trauma. For cardiothoracic surgeons managing complex thoracic trauma, TEE provides irreplaceable real-time information that changes management in >30% of cases.

🏥 Surgery, Resuscitative Thoracotomy & Damage Control

RT indications and technique, chest tube, damage control thoracic surgery, ECMO, ICU.

Resuscitative thoracotomy (RT)

🔴 Resuscitative thoracotomy — indications & survival

Indications (EAST / WTA guidelines)

  • Penetrating chest / cardiac: CPR <15 min; OR signs of life (pupil response, spontaneous movement, cardiac electrical activity)
  • Penetrating non-thoracic: CPR <5 min
  • Blunt trauma: CPR <5 min + treatable cause (tamponade, tension PTX)
  • Witnessed cardiac arrest in trauma bay
Survival rates: Penetrating cardiac 19–35% · Penetrating non-cardiac 15% · Blunt trauma 1.4%. Perform only when survival is realistically achievable.

Technique — left anterolateral thoracotomy

  1. Left 4th/5th ICS; quick incision through all layers
  2. Rib spreader (Finochietto) — aggressive retraction
  3. Open pericardium anterior to phrenic nerve (T-incision)
  4. Evacuate haemopericardium; identify cardiac wound
  5. Direct cardiac repair: finger occlusion → Foley balloon → suture (2-0 Prolene horizontal mattress)
  6. Internal cardiac massage: two-handed compression at 100/min
  7. Descending aortic cross-clamp: augments coronary and cerebral perfusion
  8. Internal defibrillation: 20 J biphasic (paddles directly on myocardium)
Chest tube — insertion technique & management
Insertion Technique
  1. Position: supine or 30° head-up; ipsilateral arm raised
  2. Site: 5th ICS anterior axillary line (safe triangle)
  3. Local anaesthesia: infiltrate generously including rib periosteum
  4. 2–3 cm incision; blunt dissect over superior rib margin (avoid NVB)
  5. Finger sweep to confirm pleural entry and exclude adhesions
  6. Insert tube (28–32 Fr for haemothorax; 24–28 Fr for PTX); direct posteriorly/superiorly
  7. Connect to underwater seal; confirm with CXR + fogging
chest tube
Troubleshooting & Complications
  • Persistent air leak: Large → tracheobronchial injury; confirm with bronchoscopy; consider double-lumen intubation + VATS
  • No drainage despite effusion on imaging: Tube malposition (intraparenchymal, intraabdominal); confirm position on CXR/CT
  • Clotted haemothorax: t-PA instillation (if >3 days) or VATS decortication
  • Empyema: Remove early; antibiotics; VATS or open decortication
  • Subcutaneous position: Tube outside pleura — immediate repositioning
Remove when: Output <150–200 mL/24 h AND no air leak AND lung fully expanded on CXR. Remove on expiration or Valsalva.
Damage control thoracic surgery & ECMO

🔧 Damage control principles

  • Abbreviated surgery: Control haemorrhage + contamination only; pack and leave
  • Cardiac packing: Pericardial packs + temporary chest closure for irreversible bleeding; return to OR at 24–48 h
  • Pulmonary tractotomy: Through-and-through lung wounds; linear stapler along wound tract → haemostasis without lobectomy
  • Pneumonectomy: Last resort for hilar destruction; mortality >50%; avoid if possible
  • Temporary intravascular shunt: For major vessel injury; allows delayed repair after resuscitation
  • Open chest management: Bogota bag or Ioban dressing; return in 24–48 h

💉 ECMO in thoracic trauma

  • VA-ECMO: Cardiac arrest / refractory cardiogenic shock (blunt cardiac injury); bridge to recovery or transplant
  • VV-ECMO: Severe pulmonary contusion / ARDS refractory to conventional ventilation; bridge to recovery
  • ECPR: ECMO-facilitated CPR for traumatic cardiac arrest — emerging evidence; requires no active uncontrollable haemorrhage
  • Perioperative: TEVAR with complex spinal cord protection; rewarming in severe hypothermic arrest
Contraindications: Uncontrolled haemorrhage; multi-organ failure; irreversible neurological injury; advanced malignancy.
Post-operative ICU management

📊 Haemodynamic monitoring

  • Arterial line (radial or femoral); CVP; consider PAC or PiCCO for complex cases
  • Target MAP 65–75 mmHg; SVR; CO/CI monitoring
  • Daily echo for tamponade surveillance (haemopericardium risk post-repair)

💨 Ventilation strategy

  • Lung-protective: TV 6 mL/kg IBW; PEEP 5–8 cmH₂O; plateau pressure <30
  • Pulmonary contusion: higher PEEP; prone if P/F <150
  • After pneumonectomy: contralateral lung — very restrictive strategy

🩸 Bleeding & coagulopathy

  • ROTEM-guided transfusion; maintain Hb >80; fibrinogen >1.5; platelets >50
  • Surgical re-exploration if chest tube >200 mL/h sustained
  • Heparin reversal after TEVAR — only if major bleeding

AVPU & Herniation Recognition
  • Cushing's triad (late sign of ICP ↑): Hypertension + Bradycardia + Irregular respirations → impending herniation
  • Rapid neurological deterioration in trauma: exclude haemorrhagic shock first (hypoperfusion mimics TBI)
  • Spinal cord injury: Assess motor and sensory level; document pre-intervention
  • ASIA grading: A (complete) → E (normal) — document for medicolegal and prognostic purposes
Cardiothoracic relevance: Spinal cord ischaemia after aortic repair presents as ASIA-A paraplegia — must be distinguished from pre-existing traumatic cord injury before TEVAR/open repair.
Cervical spine — immobilisation and clearance

🦴 C-spine management in thoracic trauma

Immobilisation

  • Maintain inline stabilisation throughout primary survey
  • Hard collar — apply as soon as possible; ensure airway is maintained
  • Do not delay airway management for C-spine immobilisation — airway takes priority
  • Log-roll with 4-person technique for any repositioning
  • Backboard: remove as soon as possible (pressure ulcer, pain, respiratory splinting)

Clinical clearance criteria (NEXUS)

  • No midline C-spine tenderness
  • No focal neurological deficit
  • Normal level of alertness (GCS 15)
  • No intoxication
  • No painful distracting injury
If ANY NEXUS criterion fails: CT C-spine required. Plain X-ray alone insufficient for clearance in trauma.
E — Exposure & environment control

🌡️ Complete exposure and temperature management

Complete exposure protocol

  • Remove all clothing — scissors to cut; do not remove manually if injury suspected
  • Log-roll: inspect posterior thorax, flanks, buttocks (commonly missed wounds)
  • Back of the thorax: Posterior thoracotomy wounds, exit wounds, spinal deformity
  • Perineal / rectal exam if pelvic fracture or penetrating trauma near pelvis
  • Document all wounds with photographs (medicolegal)

Hypothermia prevention — the lethal triad

  • Hypothermia + Acidosis + Coagulopathy = lethal triad of trauma → mortality >90% if all three present
  • Cover patient immediately after exposure; warm blankets
  • Warm IV fluids and blood products (38°C)
  • Warm OR / trauma bay environment (>26°C)
  • Active external rewarming (Bair Hugger)
  • Severe hypothermia (<30°C): Consider ECMO rewarming; cardiac arrest may be reversible
Hypothermia in cardiac surgery trauma patients: Therapeutic hypothermia (32–35°C) may be considered after ROSC from traumatic cardiac arrest, but requires exclusion of ongoing haemorrhage first. Unintentional hypothermia is uniformly harmful.
Secondary survey — head-to-toe examination

📋 Systematic secondary survey

Head & face

  • Scalp lacerations (haemorrhage control)
  • Skull palpation (deformity, crepitus)
  • Eyes: pupil, hyphema, globe rupture
  • Ear: haemotympanum (basilar skull #)
  • Battle's sign, raccoon eyes (delayed)
  • Oropharynx: blood, teeth, foreign bodies

Neck & chest

  • Tracheal position; SQ emphysema
  • Carotid pulsations; vascular bruits
  • Penetrating wounds: do not remove impaled objects
  • Chest wall: crepitus, paradoxical motion
  • Auscultation anterior + lateral + posterior
  • Sternal palpation (fracture = BCI screen)

Abdomen, pelvis, extremities

  • Abdominal palpation (peritonism)
  • Pelvic compression / distraction (if stable — once only)
  • Femoral pulses bilaterally
  • Long bone injuries: deformity, shortening
  • Vascular injury: absent pulses, expanding haematoma
  • Compartment syndrome: tense compartments, pain on passive stretch

Traumatic cardiac arrest — algorithm

👥 Special Populations

Pregnancy, paediatrics, elderly, anticoagulated patients, LVAD patients, and heart transplant recipients.

Pregnancy — trauma management

🤰 Pregnant Trauma Patient

HIGH COMPLEXITY
Physiological differences
  • Blood volume ↑ 40–50% → haemorrhagic shock may appear late
  • HR ↑ 10–15 bpm (physiological tachycardia)
  • BP ↓ 10–15 mmHg (↓ SVR)
  • Left uterine displacement from 20 weeks (IVC compression)
  • Diaphragm elevated → lung bases compressed
  • Gastric emptying ↓ → aspiration risk ↑
Primary survey modifications
  • Left lateral tilt 15° or manual uterine displacement from 20 weeks
  • RSI with cricoid pressure (aspiration risk)
  • Chest tube: insert 1–2 interspaces higher than usual (diaphragm elevated)
  • Radiation: necessary imaging should not be withheld; shield abdomen when feasible
  • Resuscitate mother first — fetal outcome depends on maternal stability
Obstetric assessment
  • Fetal heart rate monitoring (>20 weeks): CTG for ≥4 h (minimum)
  • Placental abruption: most common cause of fetal loss; may be silent
  • Uterine rupture: abdominal tenderness, fetal parts palpable, fetal distress
  • Kleihauer-Betke test: quantify feto-maternal haemorrhage; guide Rh prophylaxis
  • Anti-D immunoglobulin: Rh-negative mothers after any significant trauma
Perimortem caesarean section
  • Perform within 4 min of maternal cardiac arrest if ≥24 weeks gestation
  • Do not wait for fetal viability assessment — improves maternal cardiac output
  • Midline incision; classical uterine incision; deliver fetus and placenta
  • Continue maternal CPR / RT throughout
Paediatric thoracic trauma

👶 Paediatric considerations

Physiological differences

  • Compliant chest wall: Rib fractures uncommon; significant intrathoracic injury may occur without rib fractures
  • Higher respiratory rate (normal: infant 40–60, child 20–30 bpm)
  • Higher HR (neonate 120–160; infant 100–140; child 70–120 bpm)
  • SBP lower: neonate ~60, child 80 + (2 × age in years)
  • Smaller airways → obstruction more rapidly critical
  • Larger occiput → cervical flexion in supine position → pad under shoulders for neutral alignment

Management pearls

  • Broselow tape or weight-based formula (weight ≈ 2 × [age+4] kg) for drug doses
  • Uncuffed ETT in <8 years (or cuffed with careful pressure monitoring)
  • IO access if IV fails after 2 attempts
  • Chest tube size: (age/4 + 4) Fr approximation
  • Non-accidental injury (NAI): posterior rib fractures, multiple stages of healing; mandatory reporting
  • ECMO: broader indications in paediatric trauma (better recovery potential)
Rib fractures in children are uncommon and usually indicate significant force. Their presence should raise suspicion of pulmonary contusion, aortic injury, and non-accidental trauma. Do not dismiss as minor.
Elderly patients — geriatric trauma

👴 Geriatric trauma considerations

Physiological vulnerabilities

  • Chronotropic incompetence: Cannot mount adequate tachycardia response to hypovolaemia → shock appears normotensive
  • Beta-blocker use: masks tachycardia (Shock Index less reliable)
  • Baseline hypertension: SBP 120 may represent hypotension relative to their normal
  • Reduced pulmonary reserve: rib fractures → disproportionate respiratory compromise
  • Rib Fracture Score (RFS): 1–4 ribs = low risk; 5+ ribs = high mortality; bilateral fractures = ICU
  • Reduced bone density → rib fractures with lower-energy mechanisms

Anticoagulation considerations

  • Warfarin: INR target reversal with 4F-PCC (25–50 units/kg) + vitamin K 10 mg IV
  • DOACs (apixaban/rivaroxaban): Andexanet alfa (if available) or 4F-PCC
  • Dabigatran: Idarucizumab 5 g IV (specific reversal)
  • Dual antiplatelet: Platelet transfusion + desmopressin 0.3 mcg/kg if life-threatening bleeding
  • Always check medication list on arrival; alert haematology for complex reversal
LVAD patients — unique management challenges

⚙️ Left ventricular assist device (LVAD) in trauma

Assessment

  • LVAD patients may have no palpable pulse (continuous flow device) — assess by Doppler; MAP by arterial line
  • Target MAP 70–90 mmHg for most LVAD patients
  • Device alarm: low flow = obstructed inflow cannula, hypovolaemia, or tamponade
  • Suction event (device suck-down): hypovolaemia → RV overload → septum shift → further ↓ LV filling
  • LVAD controller: do not disconnect; carry the controller

Trauma management

  • Cardiac arrest with LVAD: Standard CPR — compressions safe with continuous-flow LVADs (HeartMate 3, HVAD)
  • Defibrillation: standard energy levels; LVAD is not damaged by external shock
  • Anticoagulation: LVAD patients on warfarin (INR target 2–3); reverse for haemorrhage with 4F-PCC
  • Contact LVAD coordinator immediately (institution-specific protocols)
  • Haemothorax: chest tube as standard; LVAD driveline location must be known before any chest incision
Pericardial tamponade in LVAD patients may present atypically — the device may partially maintain output. High suspicion required; eFAST and urgent echo are essential in any LVAD patient with haemodynamic deterioration after thoracic trauma.
Heart transplant recipients

💉 Transplant heart in trauma

  • Denervated heart: No vagal response to pain or haemorrhage → atropine is ineffective for bradycardia; use isoprenaline or direct chronotropes (adrenaline)
  • Tachycardia is absent as early warning sign of haemorrhage — rely on SBP, lactate, and eFAST for haemorrhage assessment
  • Rejection medications: immunosuppression (tacrolimus, mycophenolate, prednisolone) — do not discontinue even in trauma; stress-dose steroids for major surgery (hydrocortisone 100 mg q8h)
  • Opportunistic infections more likely: pneumonia may complicate rib fractures rapidly
  • Cardiac allograft vasculopathy (CAV): ECG may not show classic STEMI pattern with coronary injury (denervated — painless ischaemia)
  • Contact transplant team immediately; coordinate immunosuppression management
Sternotomy wound: Prior sternotomy scar is present. Re-entry carries risk of injury to adherent structures. If re-sternotomy required for trauma, oscillating saw with great care; femoral cannulation should be established before sternotomy if time allows.

Step-by-step operative procedures — indications, access, technique, and critical steps for thoracic trauma surgery.

Median sternotomy — cardiac & great vessel access

Median Sternotomy

PRIMARY APPROACH — CARDIAC TRAUMA
Indications
  • Penetrating cardiac trauma (haemodynamically unstable)
  • Cardiac tamponade (controlled setting)
  • Great vessel injury (ascending aorta, SVC, innominate)
  • Combined cardiac + pulmonary injury requiring CPB
  • Conversion from resuscitative thoracotomy
Technique
  1. Supine; arms tucked; pad between shoulders for sternal elevation
  2. Midline incision: sternal notch → xiphoid
  3. Diathermy to linea alba; clear suprasternal space bluntly
  4. Sternal saw from xiphoid upward (or top-down in emergency)
  5. Bone wax / electrocautery to marrow bleeding
  6. Sternal retractor; pericardium opened T-shaped / longitudinal
Critical steps
  • Cardiac control: Digital occlusion of wound → Foley balloon tamponade → horizontal mattress suture (2-0 Prolene) with pledgets
  • Coronary proximity: Always inspect suture line does not incorporate coronary artery
  • CPB cannulation: Ascending aorta + right atrium if needed; femoral as backup
  • Haemostasis: Argon beam, bone wax, topical haemostats at closure
Complications
  • Sternal wound infection / dehiscence
  • Mediastinitis (rare, high mortality)
  • Air embolism during cardiac repair
  • Injury to RV / PA on re-entry
  • Phrenic nerve injury
Left anterolateral thoracotomy / resuscitative thoracotomy

Left Anterolateral Thoracotomy (RT)

EMERGENCY — TRAUMA BAY
Position & landmarks
  • Supine; left arm extended overhead
  • 4th or 5th ICS (nipple level in males, inframammary fold in females)
  • Incision from sternum to posterior axillary line
  • Extend to clamshell (bilateral) if right-sided injury or poor exposure
Step-by-step
  1. Skin incision with scalpel — bold, decisive; no time to waste
  2. Cut intercostal muscles with Mayo scissors over superior rib margin
  3. Rib spreader (Finochietto) — open aggressively
  4. Pericardium: grasp, incise anterior to phrenic nerve (T-shape or longitudinal)
  5. Evacuate haemopericardium; identify cardiac wound
  6. Aortic cross-clamp: descending thoracic aorta anterior to spine
Cardiac repair in the field
  • Finger occlusion: Immediate control
  • Foley catheter balloon: Insert into wound, inflate 30 mL, gentle traction
  • Suture repair: 2-0 Prolene horizontal mattress with pledgets; avoid coronaries
  • Atrial wounds: Satinsky clamp; oversew with 3-0 Prolene running
  • Internal defibrillation: 20 J biphasic; paddles directly on myocardium
Internal cardiac massage
  • Two-handed biventricular compression preferred
  • Rate 100/min; avoid laceration of epicardium
  • One-handed: left hand cups heart from below; right thumb compresses apex toward base
  • Assess for tension PTX (right side) before commencing massage
Posterolateral thoracotomy — lung & descending aorta

Posterolateral Thoracotomy

DEFINITIVE — HAEMOTHORAX / AORTIC REPAIR
Indications
  • Massive haemothorax not controlled by chest tube
  • Persistent pulmonary air leak (tracheobronchial injury)
  • Oesophageal injury
  • Descending thoracic aorta (open repair, if TEVAR not available)
  • Empyema or clotted haemothorax (if VATS not possible)
Position & technique
  • Lateral decubitus: operative side up; bean bag or sandbags
  • Left: 4th ICS (aorta), 5th–6th ICS (lung)
  • Right: 4th–5th ICS (oesophagus, right lung, right PA)
  • Serratus and latissimus usually divided in emergency
  • Double-lumen ETT or bronchial blocker — single lung ventilation
Pulmonary laceration repair
  • Tractotomy: Linear stapler along wound tract for through-and-through wounds
  • Suture repair: 2-0 Vicryl mattress; absorbable
  • Lobectomy: Anatomical resection for hilar injury or destroyed lobe
  • Pneumonectomy: Last resort; mortality >50%; stapled or hilar clamp-and-divide
  • Hilar twist (Mattox manoeuvre): Rotate lung 180° to control hilar haemorrhage
Aortic repair steps
  • Identify aortic isthmus (posterior to left PA, distal to left subclavian)
  • Proximal control: aorta between left CCA and subclavian
  • Distal control: descending aorta distal to injury
  • Left heart bypass (LHB): left pulmonary vein → LHB pump → descending aorta or femoral
  • Repair: patch aortoplasty or interposition graft (Dacron)
Pericardiocentesis & pericardial window
Emergency Pericardiocentesis
  1. Position: supine, head up 30–45° (pools blood inferiorly)
  2. Site: subxiphoid angle (between xiphoid and left costal margin)
  3. 18G spinal needle with 20 mL syringe; ECG lead attached to needle hub
  4. Advance at 45° angle directed toward left shoulder; aspirate continuously
  5. ST elevation or PVC on ECG → withdraw 0.5 cm (myocardial contact)
  6. Aspirate blood (non-clotting = pericardial); if dark red clot = ventricular puncture
  7. Insert guidewire (Seldinger) → dilate → pigtail catheter for continued drainage
Even 30–50 mL aspirated from tense haemopericardium can restore haemodynamics sufficiently to reach OR. Do not delay for large-volume drainage.
peri
Surgical Pericardial Window
  • Indication: Equivocal eFAST; haemodynamically stable; penetrating mechanism near cardiac box; recurrent tamponade
  • Subxiphoid window (Gold standard): 5 cm midline incision below xiphoid; retract xiphoid cranially; dissect onto pericardium; excise 2 cm window; if blood → proceed to sternotomy
  • VATS pericardial window: Left-sided; camera in 5th ICS; grasper + scissors; 2 cm window; effective for recurrent effusions
  • Interpretation: Clear serous fluid = benign; haemoserous = investigation; frank blood = operative repair
If blood is found at pericardial window: Convert immediately to sternotomy. Do not close. Have OR team scrubbed and ready before window creation in penetrating trauma.
