⚖️ The Foundational Rule: Predicted Body Weight
PBW FORMULA
Male: 50 + 2.3 × (heightin − 60)
Female: 45.5 + 2.3 × (heightin − 60)
Based on height, not weight. 1 inch = 2.54 cm.
WHY IT MATTERS
Using actual body weight in obese patients causes severe volutrauma. A 140 kg patient still receives 6 mL/kg based on their predicted lean body mass — not 840 mL per breath.

Dual-control, Intelligent & Special Modes

🤖 INTELLiVENT-ASV — Closed-loop

Integrates IBW, minute ventilation target, and real-time SpO&sub2; / EtCO&sub2; feedback to auto-adjust RR, PS, PEEP, and FiO&sub2; breath-to-breath. Hamilton C1’s flagship intelligent mode.

Targets: EtCO&sub2; 35–45 mmHg · SpO&sub2; 88–96% · Safety limits: Pmax 40, PEEP max 20, RR max 40/min
Caution: Avoid in severe metabolic acidosis with compensatory tachypnea — algorithm may under-ventilate by misinterpreting high RR as demand already met.

⏱️ DuoLevel / APRV

Two pressure levels (P_high / P_low) with unrestricted spontaneous breathing at both levels. Sustained recruitment via prolonged P_high; CO&sub2; clearance via brief T_low release.

Typical settings: P_high 20–30 cmH&sub2;O · T_high 4–6 sec · P_low 0–5 · T_low 0.2–0.8 sec (set so expiratory flow at end ≈75% of peak)
Contraindicated: Severe obstructive disease (auto-PEEP risk) · Large air leak
Elevated ICP: use with caution — high mean airway pressure may impair cerebral venous drainage; not an absolute contraindication.

🌬️ NIV & VS

  • NIV: Non-invasive ventilation with turbine-driven leak compensation up to 40 L/min. COPD exacerbation, APO. Contraindicated in facial trauma, upper airway obstruction, inability to protect airway.
  • VS (Volume Support): Spontaneous breaths with pressure-servo targeting set Vt. No mandatory breaths. Used for weaning and SBT.
  • PRVC: Pressure-limited, volume-targeted dual-control. Proprietary names vary by manufacturer (AutoFlow—Dräger, VC+—Puritan Bennett, PRVC—Maquet/Servo).

Advanced Hamilton-specific Parameters

📡 P0.1 — Respiratory Drive Index

Airway occlusion pressure in the first 100 ms of inspiratory effort. Reflects central respiratory drive without the confound of Vt or mode settings.

P0.1 (cmH&sub2;O)Interpretation
<1Overassistance / over-sedation
1.5–3.5Adequate drive — target range
>4–6High respiratory drive; risk of P-SILI and fatigue

📐 RCexp, Pmax & INTELLiVENT Targets

  • RCexp (expiratory time constant): Qualitative guide to expiratory time. No universal threshold — prolonged RCexp in COPD/asthma signals need for longer Te. Monitor trends, not absolute values.
  • Pmax / High-pressure limit: Individualize per patient — set above operating Ppeak but low enough to detect dangerous rises. No single universally safe fixed value.
  • INTELLiVENT-ASV targets: Set EtCO&sub2; 35–45, SpO&sub2; 88–96%, %MinVol. Closed-loop auto-adjusts — avoid in severe metabolic acidosis with compensatory tachypnea.

🛡️ Protective Lung Ventilation & ARDS

Minimizing ventilator-induced lung injury (VILI) through volume, pressure, and driving-force limitation. Berlin classification and ARDSnet protocol.

The Four Mechanisms of VILI
VOLUTRAUMA
Excessive tidal volume overstretches alveoli → membrane disruption
BAROTRAUMA
Excessive alveolar pressure (Pplat) → pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum
ATELECTRAUMA
Repeated collapse and reopening of alveoli → shear stress injury
BIOTRAUMA
Mechanical injury triggers inflammatory mediator release → systemic MODS
Protective Targets
Targets — Aim For

✅ Protective ventilation parameters

  • Tidal Volume: 6 mL/kg PBW (ARDS) · 6–8 mL/kg PBW (others)
  • Plateau Pressure (Pplat): ≤30 cmH&sub2;O
  • Driving Pressure (ΔP = Pplat − PEEP): ≤15 cmH&sub2;O (ideally ≤13)
  • PEEP: Sufficient to prevent derecruitment — titrate by compliance or FiO&sub2;/PEEP table
  • FiO&sub2;: Lowest to maintain SpO&sub2; 92–96%; avoid >0.6 long-term
  • PaCO&sub2;: Permissive hypercapnia acceptable (pH ≥7.20) to limit pressures
  • Prone position: P/F <150 after 12–24 h optimised settings → ≥16 h/day
  • Conservative fluids: Post-resuscitation phase — avoid fluid overload
vs
Avoid — Injurious

🚫 VILI risk factors

  • Vt >8 mL/kg PBW in ARDS or at-risk lungs
  • Pplat >30 cmH&sub2;O
  • Driving pressure >15 cmH&sub2;O
  • FiO&sub2; >0.6 without escalating PEEP or recruitment
  • Derecruitment: unnecessary PEEP reductions, circuit disconnections
  • Hyperoxia (PaO&sub2; >100 mmHg without indication)
  • High mechanical power (RR, Vt, driving pressure all contribute)
  • Uncorrected patient–ventilator asynchrony
Measuring Driving Pressure

🔢 Step-by-step: ΔP measurement on Hamilton C1

  1. Switch to VC-CMV or VSIMV (patient must be passive — no spontaneous effort)
  2. Apply inspiratory hold for 0.5–3 seconds
  3. Read Pplat from the displayed plateau pressure
  4. Calculate: ΔP = Pplat − PEEP
  5. If ΔP >15: reduce Vt by 1 mL/kg PBW steps or increase PEEP to improve compliance
  6. Reassess after every change
Why ΔP matters more than Vt alone: A patient with low respiratory system compliance (stiff, consolidated lungs) will have a high ΔP at the same Vt as a healthy lung. Driving pressure reveals the actual mechanical stress delivered to the functional lung — it adapts to the patient’s lung mechanics automatically.
Clinical pearl: If ΔP >15 despite Vt already at 6 mL/kg PBW → reduce to 4 mL/kg PBW and check. Also consider whether PEEP is optimised — recruiting collapsed alveoli increases compliance and reduces ΔP without changing Vt.

ARDSnet Low-Vt Protocol Summary

📋 Lung-protective ventilation protocol (ARDSnet)

  • Vt = 6 mL/kg PBW — reduce to 4 if Pplat >30 or ΔP >15
  • Pplat ≤30 cmH&sub2;O
  • Driving pressure ≤15 cmH&sub2;O
  • RR up to 35/min to maintain pH ≥7.20
  • PEEP/FiO&sub2; titration per low- or high-PEEP table
  • Target SpO&sub2; 92–96% or PaO&sub2; 55–80 mmHg
  • Cisatracurium (NMB) 48 h if P/F <150 with significant dyssynchrony
  • Conservative fluid management post-resuscitation
  • Prone ≥16 h/day if P/F <150 after 12–24 h on optimised settings
  • Consider APRV or INTELLiVENT-ASV for refractory hypoxemia
  • ECMO evaluation if P/F persistently <80 despite optimised care

⚡ Patient–Ventilator Asynchrony & Auto-PEEP

Identify and correct the mismatch between patient neural drive and ventilator delivery. Asynchrony prolongs ventilation and worsens outcomes.

Key principle: Always identify the asynchrony pattern from waveforms before escalating sedation. Sedation suppresses the symptom without fixing the cause — and may worsen P-SILI.

Auto-PEEP — Intrinsic PEEP & Dynamic Hyperinflation

💨 Pathophysiology & Consequences

Auto-PEEP (iPEEP) occurs when expiratory flow fails to reach zero before the next breath — air traps and raises end-expiratory alveolar pressure above set PEEP.

Causes: COPD · Asthma · High RR · Short expiratory time · High Vt · Small ETT · Secretions · Dynamic airway collapse
Consequences:
• ↑ Intrathoracic pressure → ↓ venous return → hypotension / obstructive shock
• Ineffective triggering (patient must overcome iPEEP to trigger)
• Dynamic hyperinflation → pneumothorax risk
• ↑ Work of breathing
Bedside test: Unexplained hypotension in COPD/asthma patient on ventilator → briefly disconnect ETT from circuit. If haemodynamics improve and trapped air audibly released → auto-PEEP confirmed.

📏 Measurement & Management

Measurement: Apply expiratory hold (0.5–2 sec) → read total PEEP from display.
auto-PEEP = total PEEP − set PEEP

Management ladder

  • ↓ RR → ↑ expiratory time
  • ↓ Vt (less volume to exhale per breath)
  • ↑ I:E ratio to 1:3–1:4 in obstructive disease
  • ↑ Peak inspiratory flow (shortens Ti → longer Te)
  • Bronchodilators + aggressive secretion clearance
  • Apply external PEEP ≈ 70–85% of measured iPEEP to stent airways and reduce trigger threshold
Cardiac surgery: Auto-PEEP severely impairs RV filling in a preload-dependent post-operative heart. Monitor proactively in any COPD or bronchospasm patient post-sternotomy.

Permissive Hypercapnia

♾️ Concept & rationale

Permissive hypercapnia = intentional tolerance of elevated PaCO&sub2; to achieve lower Vt and driving pressures, prioritising lung protection over normocapnia.

Acceptable parameters:
• PaCO&sub2; up to 60–80 mmHg if haemodynamically tolerated
• Minimum acceptable pH: ≥7.20–7.25
• Avoid rapid CO&sub2; changes (arrhythmia risk)
• NaHCO&sub3; may temporise if pH <7.20 (controversial; tidal correction only)
Lung-protective benefit: Modest hypercapnic acidosis may have direct anti-inflammatory, lung-protective effects. Generally well tolerated in ARDS with adequate haemodynamics.

🚫 Contraindications & cautions

  • Intracranial hypertension (TBI, post-neurosurgery): CO&sub2; is a potent cerebral vasodilator; hypercapnia worsens ICP. Avoid permissive hypercapnia.
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Hypercapnia and acidosis ↑ PVR → worsens RV failure.
  • RV failure post-cardiac surgery: Maintain normocapnia; hypercapnia ↑ RV afterload.
  • Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.20): additional respiratory acidosis is dangerous.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias attributable to acidosis.
Mechanical Power — The Energy Dimension of VILI

⚙️ Unified VILI metric: energy delivered per minute

Mechanical Power (MP) integrates volume, pressure, flow, and rate — capturing the cumulative injurious energy delivered to the respiratory system. It overcomes the limitation of single-parameter assessment.

Simplified formula (VC-CMV)
MP (J/min) = 0.098 × RR × Vt(L) × (Ppeak − ΔP/2)

Pressures in cmH&sub2;O · Vt in litres

Main contributors to high MP:
• High Vt (dominant driver)
• High respiratory rate
• High driving pressure
• High PEEP (static component)
• High airway resistance (↑ Ppeak)
Hamilton C1: INTELLiVENT-ASV can display estimated MP. Reducing RR and Vt together synergistically lowers MP — even when each individual value appears acceptable in isolation.
Evidence status: Emerging metric — not yet embedded in formal guidelines (2025). Provides a valuable unified lens especially when standard parameters conflict (e.g. low Vt but very high RR + PEEP). (Gattinoni et al., Intensive Care Med 2016)
Mechanical Ventilation after Cardiac Surgery

🏥 Post-operative goals & fast-track extubation

  • Early extubation (fast-track): Target 4–8 h post-CABG / valve repair in uncomplicated cases. Reduces VAP, ICU stay, and cost.
  • Protective ventilation even for short post-op periods: Vt 6–8 mL/kg PBW
  • SpO&sub2; 94–98% (liberal target acceptable with adequate cardiac output)
  • EtCO&sub2; 35–42 mmHg; avoid hypercapnia if elevated PVR suspected
  • PEEP 5–8 cmH&sub2;O to prevent post-sternotomy / CPB-related atelectasis
  • Propofol or dexmedetomidine preferred over benzodiazepines for fast-track
Phrenic nerve palsy (post-IMA harvest, hypothermia): assess by diaphragm ultrasound. Unilateral palsy may still permit extubation. Bilateral palsy requires continued ventilatory support.

⚠️ Haemodynamic interactions with PPV

  • High PEEP → ↓ RV preload: In RV failure (post-tricuspid, PH, RV MI) — use lowest effective PEEP; avoid driving preload-dependence into haemodynamic crisis.
  • Hypercapnia & hypoxia → ↑ PVR → ↑ RV afterload: Maintain normocapnia and normoxia in RV failure. Avoid permissive hypercapnia in this setting.
  • Pulse pressure variation (PPV) for fluid responsiveness: Valid only in fully controlled, arrhythmia-free ventilation with Vt ≥8 mL/kg — a direct conflict with lung-protective ventilation in ARDS patients.
  • Post-pneumonectomy: PEEP 3–5 cmH&sub2;O on remaining lung; minimise FiO&sub2;; avoid high mean airway pressures.

✅ Extubation readiness criteria

  • Awake, follows simple commands
  • Spontaneous Vt >5 mL/kg PBW · RR <30/min
  • SpO&sub2; >92% on PS ≤8 cmH&sub2;O + PEEP 5
  • FiO&sub2; ≤0.40 · PEEP ≤8 cmH&sub2;O
  • No vasopressor escalation
  • Intact cough / secretion clearance
  • P0.1 <4–6 cmH&sub2;O (adequate drive; no excessive demand)
Post-extubation in high-risk patients: HFNO prophylactically to reduce re-intubation risk. NIV for cardiogenic pulmonary oedema.

🚨 Delay extubation if:

  • Haemodynamic instability / ongoing vasopressor escalation
  • Significant bleeding requiring re-exploration
  • Uncontrolled arrhythmia (rapid AF, VT)
  • Persistent severe hypoxemia (FiO&sub2; >0.5, PEEP >8)
  • Neurological deficit, prolonged obtundation
  • Severe metabolic acidosis / hypothermia
  • Bilateral phrenic palsy or diaphragmatic injury

Troubleshooting — Hamilton C1 Errors & Warnings

🚫 Device errors

  • “High PEEP leak” — circuit leak or exhalation valve malfunction → check expiratory filter, reconnect, run SST
  • “Check patient circuit” — disconnection or occlusion → inspect circuit, replace wet filter
  • “Occlusion / high pressure” — secretions, ETT kink, biting → suction, check ETT position
  • “Power failure” — battery depleted → connect external power; C1 has 4-hour hot-swappable battery
  • “Sensor error” — contaminated flow/pressure sensor → run leak test, replace sensor
Ventilator inoperative: Disconnect and manually bag immediately. Always have backup device at bedside.

⚠️ Clinical warnings

  • “Low compliance” — dynamic compliance <30 mL/cmH&sub2;O: atelectasis, ARDS, pneumothorax → recruit, CXR
  • “Auto-PEEP high” — COPD/asthma → ↓ RR, ↑ Te, bronchodilators, apply external PEEP ~70–85% iPEEP
  • “Asynchrony detected” — double-triggering or ineffective efforts → review waveforms, adjust trigger or mode
  • “High FiO&sub2; demand” — FiO&sub2; >0.6 prolonged → optimise PEEP, recruitment, prone, treat underlying cause
Evidence-based Weaning

🏁 SAT + SBT protocol (ACCCM/ERS 2017)

Daily Spontaneous Awakening Trials (SAT) + Spontaneous Breathing Trials (SBT) are the recommended strategy — superior to gradual SIMV rate reduction (Girard 2008; ACCCM/ERS 2017).

SAT: Interrupt sedation daily until awake/arousable. Reassess sedation strategy — target lightest effective sedation.
SBT: PSV 5–8 cmH&sub2;O + PEEP 5 cmH&sub2;O or CPAP/T-piece for 30–120 min.
Pass criteria: RR <35, SpO&sub2; >92%, spontaneous Vt >5 mL/kg PBW, no distress, haemodynamically stable.
Pass → extubate. Fail → full support; reassess daily.
SIMV rate reduction is NOT recommended as a primary weaning strategy — it is slower and associated with worse outcomes compared to daily SBT. Use SIMV for partial support in pre-SBT patients, not as a stepwise weaning ladder.

Readiness checklist

  • Underlying cause of RF treated / improving
  • FiO&sub2; ≤0.40 · PEEP ≤8 cmH&sub2;O
  • Haemodynamically stable (minimal/no vasopressors)
  • Intact cough / secretion clearance
  • Awake, follows simple commands
  • P0.1 <4–6 cmH&sub2;O
Advanced Mode Deep Dive

🤖 INTELLiVENT-ASV — Closed-loop ventilation

INTELLiVENT-ASV uses ideal body weight, %MinVol target, and real-time SpO&sub2; / EtCO&sub2; to continuously auto-adjust RR, PS, PEEP, and FiO&sub2;. No manual breath-to-breath adjustments needed during stable phases.

Best for: Stable ARDS weaning · post-op cardiac surgery · neurological patients · overnight monitoring
Avoid in: Severe metabolic acidosis with compensatory tachypnea (algorithm interprets high RR as met demand, may under-ventilate) · Acute haemodynamic instability with rapid lung mechanics changes
