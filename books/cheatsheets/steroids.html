💊 Pharmacology & Reference

Glucocorticoid receptor pharmacology, equivalence table, individual drug profiles, and classification.

Mechanism of Action

🧬 Glucocorticoid Receptor Signalling

  • Genomic (slow, hours–days): GC binds cytoplasmic GR → nuclear translocation → GRE binding → transactivation (anti-inflammatory proteins: lipocortin-1, IL-10) or transrepression (AP-1, NF-κB → ↓ IL-1, IL-6, TNF, COX-2, iNOS)
  • Non-genomic (fast, minutes): Membrane-associated GR → rapid suppression of arachidonic acid release; explains immediate haemodynamic effects of high-dose/pulse therapy
  • HPA-axis feedback: Cortisol (endogenous) / exogenous GCs suppress CRH and ACTH → adrenal cortex atrophy with prolonged use

Individual Drug Profiles

Hydrocortisone (Cortisol)

  • Routes: PO, IV, IM, topical
  • Key uses: Adrenal insufficiency replacement, adrenal crisis, stress dosing, septic shock, anaphylaxis
  • Advantage: Has mineralocorticoid activity → preferred for adrenal replacement. Short biologic half-life allows physiological pulsatile dosing.
  • Renal failure: No adjustment; prefer over dexamethasone in AI replacement
  • Liver failure: Safe (unlike prednisone which requires hepatic activation)
  • Pregnancy: Drug of choice for adrenal insufficiency (converted to inactive cortisone by placental 11β-HSD2)

Prednisolone / Prednisone

  • Routes: PO (prednisone prodrug), IV/PO (prednisolone)
  • Key uses: Most chronic inflammatory diseases (RA, SLE, PMR, GCA, IBD, asthma, MS relapse), organ transplant maintenance
  • Advantage: Long clinical experience, flexible dosing, once-daily AM dosing for HPA preservation
  • Liver failure: Use prednisolone (not prednisone) — prednisone requires hepatic conversion
  • Pregnancy: Acceptable; low placental transfer (11β-HSD2 inactivates ~88%)

Methylprednisolone (MP)

  • Routes: PO, IV, IM; pulse IV (500–1000 mg)
  • Key uses: MS relapse, SLE pulse, transplant rejection, severe autoimmune disease, post-cardiac surgery
  • Advantage: Minimal mineralocorticoid effect (preferred in fluid-restricted/cardiac patients). Good CNS penetration.
  • Pulse therapy: 500–1000 mg IV over 30–60 min daily × 3 days. Monitor: BP, glucose, ECG (QT).
  • Cardiac surgery: Used perioperatively for CPB-related SIRS, vasoplegic syndrome (see Protocols)

Dexamethasone

  • Routes: PO, IV, IM
  • Key uses: Cerebral oedema, croup, PONV, COVID-19 (RECOVERY trial), chemotherapy anti-emesis, foetal lung maturity, dexamethasone suppression test, HPA assessment, spinal cord compression
  • Advantage: Highest GC potency. Zero mineralocorticoid activity. Long biologic half-life (36–72 h) → once daily dosing.
  • Disadvantage: Most HPA-suppressive per mg. Poorest choice for long-term replacement (no MC activity).
  • Crosses placenta fully (NOT inactivated by 11β-HSD2) → use intentionally for foetal lung maturation or foetal heart block treatment

Fludrocortisone

  • Route: PO only
  • Key uses: Mineralocorticoid replacement in primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison’s disease), congenital adrenal hyperplasia, orthostatic hypotension
  • Dose: 0.05–0.2 mg/day. Adjust to supine/standing BP, sodium, potassium, renin level.
  • Important: Do NOT increase during illness (MC replacement dose remains constant). Only hydrocortisone (GC) dose is doubled in illness.

Pulse Therapy

  • Definition: Very high-dose IV GC over short period (typically 3 consecutive days)
  • Standard doses: MP 250 mg / 500 mg / 1000 mg IV over 30–60 min daily × 3 days
  • Indications: MS relapse, SLE nephritis, severe vasculitis, transplant rejection, ADEM, optic neuritis, rapidly progressive GN
  • Monitoring: BP, glucose hourly, ECG (arrhythmia with rapid IV), electrolytes, infection screen
Absolute contraindications to pulse therapy: Active untreated infection (including TB, fungal) · Active GI bleeding · Severe uncontrolled DM · Psychosis (active) · Heart failure decompensation (relative)

📋 Clinical Protocols by Indication

Evidence-based dosing regimens for specific conditions with guideline source and monitoring recommendations.

Pulmonary

Asthma Exacerbation

Prednisone 40–60 mg PO daily × 5–7 days
Methylprednisolone 40–80 mg IV q12–24h (if unable to take PO)
⬛ Level A GINA 2024 · No taper needed for courses <3 weeks

COPD Exacerbation

Prednisone 40 mg PO daily × 5 days
⬛ Level A GOLD 2024 · Longer courses not superior (REDUCE trial) · No taper required

COVID-19 — Severe (requiring O₂ / ICU)

Dexamethasone 6 mg IV or PO daily × 10 days (or until discharge)
⬛ Level A RECOVERY Trial · NNT ~8 for mortality in O₂-requiring patients · Avoid in mild disease (no O₂ requirement)

Organizing Pneumonia (OP / COP)

Prednisone 0.75–1 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg) × 4–8 weeks
Then taper: 40 mg × 4 wk → 20 mg × 4 wk → 10 mg × 4 wk → 5 mg × 4 wk → stop
⬜ Level B ATS/ERS ILD Guidelines · Total duration: 6–12 months · Monitor for relapse

Severe Pneumonitis (drug-induced / ICI toxicity)

Grade 2: Prednisone 1–2 mg/kg/day
Grade 3–4: Methylprednisolone 1–2 mg/kg IV daily; if no improvement in 48h: add IVIG or infliximab
⬜ Level B SITC & ESMO ICI Toxicity Guidelines 2023

ARDS (refractory, >7 days)

Methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg/day IV × 14 days, then taper over 4 weeks
⬜ Level B ARDS Network · Avoid if >14 days (ACCP CPG). Dexamethasone 6 mg (RECOVERY dosing) for COVID-ARDS.
Rheumatology

Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA) / Temporal Arteritis

Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg) × 4 weeks, then slow taper over 12–18 months
If visual symptoms / imminent blindness: MP 500–1000 mg IV × 3 days FIRST
⬛ Level A ACR/EULAR 2021 · Tocilizumab allows faster taper · PJP prophylaxis if >20 mg prednisone

Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

Prednisone 15–25 mg/day initially → taper by 2.5 mg every 2–4 weeks to 10 mg
Then taper by 1 mg every 4–8 weeks (total duration 12–18 months)
⬛ Level A EULAR 2015 / ACR 2021 · Monitor CRP/ESR monthly · Relapse common with rapid taper

Severe SLE Flare

Moderate: Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day
Severe: MP pulse 500–1000 mg IV × 3 days → Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day
⬜ Level B EULAR SLE 2023 · Add hydroxychloroquine · HBV screen before pulse

Vasculitis (ANCA-associated — induction)

MP 500–1000 mg IV × 3 days (if severe / organ-threatening) → Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day
Taper to <5 mg at 3 months (PEXIVAS protocol)
⬛ Level A PEXIVAS trial · ACR/EULAR ANCA Guidelines 2022

Inflammatory Myositis (DM/PM)

Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) × 4–6 weeks
Then taper slowly over 2 years; add steroid-sparing agent early (azathioprine, MTX)
⬜ Level B EULAR/ACR Myositis 2019

RA (bridge therapy)

Prednisone 5–10 mg/day as bridge to DMARD therapy
Target: <5 mg/day or discontinue as DMARD takes effect
⬛ Level A EULAR RA 2022 · Use lowest effective dose; not monotherapy
Neurology

Multiple Sclerosis Relapse

Methylprednisolone 1000 mg IV daily × 3–5 days (no taper required)
⬛ Level A Milanese C et al. · Speeds relapse recovery; does not alter long-term disability. PO MP 625 mg/day non-inferior to IV in COPOUSEP trial.

Autoimmune Encephalitis / ADEM

MP 1000 mg IV daily × 5 days → Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day × 4–6 weeks → taper over 3–6 months
⬜ Level B · Add IVIG and/or plasma exchange if incomplete response after 5 days
Gastroenterology

Acute Severe Ulcerative Colitis (ASUC)

Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV q6h × 3–5 days (Truelove & Witts criteria)
If non-response at day 3: add ciclosporin 2–4 mg/kg or infliximab 5 mg/kg
⬛ Level A ECCO IBD 2023 · Surgical colectomy if failure at day 7

Crohn’s Disease (active flare)

Prednisone 40–60 mg/day × 4–8 weeks, then taper by 5 mg/week
Budesonide 9 mg/day (ileal/right colon disease) — lower systemic AE
⬛ Level A ECCO 2023 · Steroids are not maintenance therapy in Crohn’s
ICU & Cardiac Surgery

Septic Shock (refractory to vasopressors)

Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day IV (50 mg q6h or 200 mg continuous infusion)
± Fludrocortisone 50 mcg PO daily (APROCCHSS trial — survival benefit)
⬛ Level A Surviving Sepsis 2021 (Class 2B — if NE >0.25 mcg/kg/min) · ADRENAL & APROCCHSS trials · Taper when vasopressors weaned

Vasoplegic Syndrome Post-CPB

Hydrocortisone 200–300 mg/day IV (as for septic shock) — first-line after methylene blue
Methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg IV as alternative (less mineralocorticoid effect)
⬜ Expert consensus · Bojar 6th ed. · Intraoperative MP (10–15 mg/kg at CPB prime) may reduce post-CPB SIRS

Adrenal Crisis

Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV bolus IMMEDIATELY → 50–100 mg IV q6–8h OR 200 mg/24h continuous
IV fluid resuscitation: 0.9% NaCl 1 L over 30–60 min (correct hyponatraemia and hypotension)
⬛ Level A Endocrine Society AI CPG 2016 · Do NOT delay treatment for investigation · Transition to oral HC when stable

Cardiac Transplant Rejection (acute cellular)

Grade 2R/3R rejection: Methylprednisolone 500–1000 mg IV daily × 3 days
Then oral prednisone 1 mg/kg → taper to maintenance over 4–6 weeks
⬜ Level B ISHLT Guidelines 2010 · Reassess with biopsy at 2 weeks · Consider OKT3 / ATG for haemodynamic compromise

Anaphylaxis (adjunct)

Hydrocortisone 200 mg IV or MP 125 mg IV or Prednisone 50 mg PO
Epinephrine remains FIRST-LINE. GC may prevent biphasic reactions (evidence limited).
⬜ Level B / 2C WAO Anaphylaxis Guidelines 2020

Perioperative CPB — Anti-inflammatory

Methylprednisolone 10–15 mg/kg IV at CPB prime (prophylactic SIRS suppression)
OR Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg single dose (DECS trial — reduced postoperative AF)
⬜ Level B DECS Trial · Benefit on AF; no mortality benefit. Increases glucose. Centre-specific protocols vary.

⚠️ Adrenal Insufficiency Symptoms on Tapering

Suspect HPA insufficiency during taper if:

  • Fatigue, weakness, myalgia disproportionate to disease
  • Nausea, anorexia, weight loss
  • Orthostatic hypotension, tachycardia
  • Hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, hypoglycaemia
  • Eosinophilia
Diagnosis: 08:00 serum cortisol <100 nmol/L (<3.6 μg/dL) = adrenal insufficiency likely. Cortisol >500 nmol/L = normal. 100–500 nmol/L = borderline → perform ACTH stimulation test (Synacthen 250 mcg IV; peak cortisol >500 nmol/L at 30–60 min = adequate response).

GCA — Very slow taper (12–18 months)

PMR — Slow taper (18–24 months)

20 mg × 4 wk → 17.5 mg × 4 wk → 15 mg × 4 wk → 12.5 mg × 4 wk → 10 mg × 6 wk → 9 mg × 4 wk → 8 mg → 7 mg → 6 mg → 5 mg (each 4 wk) → 4 mg → 3 mg → 2 mg → 1 mg (each 8 wk) → stop
Monitor ESR/CRP monthly · Relapse in ~50% — increase to last effective dose

20–40 mg: ↓ 5 mg every 1–2 weeks
10–20 mg: ↓ 2.5 mg every 2–4 weeks
5–10 mg: ↓ 1 mg every 4 weeks

⚠️ Safety, Adverse Effects & Monitoring

Systematic review of corticosteroid adverse effects, prevention strategies, monitoring schedule, and special populations.

Adverse Effects by System

⚕️ Metabolic & Endocrine

  • Hyperglycaemia: Universal at high doses. Screen all patients. Postprandial peaks. Manage with sliding scale insulin; may need long-acting insulin if chronic.
  • Weight gain / central obesity: Cushingoid redistribution (buffalo hump, moon face, truncal obesity). Dose- and duration-dependent.
  • Dyslipidaemia: ↑LDL, ↑triglycerides, ↓HDL. Statin therapy if indicated.
  • HPA axis suppression: See HPA calculator. Risk of adrenal crisis under physiological stress.
  • Hypogonadism: Chronic use → ↓testosterone, ↓oestrogen, amenorrhoea, impotence.

🦴 Cardiovascular

  • Hypertension: Both GC (Na/H₂O retention) and MC (fludrocortisone). Monitor BP at each visit. ACE inhibitor / ARB preferred if anti-hypertensive needed.
  • Fluid retention / oedema: Especially with agents with significant MC activity (hydrocortisone, prednisone).
  • Arrhythmia (pulse therapy): Rapid IV MP can cause bradycardia and VT. Administer over 30–60 min; ECG monitoring during pulse.
  • Accelerated atherosclerosis: Via dyslipidaemia, hypertension, hyperglycaemia. Long-term use associated with ↑CV risk.

🦵 Osteoporosis & Musculoskeletal

  • GIOP (GC-induced osteoporosis): Most common iatrogenic cause of osteoporosis. Greatest bone loss in first 3–6 months. Affects trabecular > cortical bone.
  • Prevention: Calcium 1000–1200 mg/day + Vitamin D 800–1000 IU/day for ALL patients on >5 mg prednisone-equiv for >3 months
  • Bisphosphonates: Alendronate or zoledronic acid — if ≥5 mg/day for ≥3 months (ACR GIOP 2022 guideline)
  • Steroid myopathy: Proximal weakness (hip flexors, shoulder abductors). Dose-dependent. Fluorinated GCs (dexamethasone, triamcinolone) cause more myopathy. Non-fluorinated preferred for long-term use.
  • Avascular necrosis (AVN): Especially femoral head. Risk with high-dose pulse therapy. Present with hip/groin pain.

🧠 Psychiatric & Neurological

  • Mood and cognition: Insomnia, euphoria, irritability (early high dose); depression (chronic). Rarely: frank psychosis or mania.
  • Pseudotumour cerebri: Rare; on tapering. Presents with headache, papilloedema.
  • Posterior subcapsular cataracts: After months to years of use. Annual ophthalmology review.
  • Glaucoma: ↑ intraocular pressure. Screen at baseline and annually.

🦚 Infectious Risk

  • Bacterial infections: Risk proportional to dose and duration. Opportunistic infections with >20 mg/day.
  • PJP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia): Prophylaxis with cotrimoxazole 480 mg/day if >20 mg prednisone-equiv for >4 weeks. Alternatively dapsone or atovaquone.
  • HBV reactivation: Screen HBsAg and HBcAb before starting. HBsAg-positive: antiviral prophylaxis (tenofovir/entecavir). Inactive carrier: prophylaxis if >10 mg for >4 weeks.
  • TB: Screen with IGRA or TST before starting long-term GC. Treat latent TB before high-dose therapy.
  • Live vaccines contraindicated on >20 mg prednisone-equiv (immunosuppressed).

🪨 GI & Dermatological

  • Peptic ulcer disease: Risk ↑ with concurrent NSAIDs. PPI prophylaxis if: high-dose GC + NSAID, or history of PUD, or anticoagulant co-use.
  • Skin: Skin atrophy, striae, easy bruising, impaired wound healing, acne, hirsutism, hyperpigmentation.
  • Growth retardation: In children on supraphysiological doses; use alternate-day dosing when possible.
Adrenal Crisis — Recognition & Emergency Management

🚨 Clinical Presentation

Adrenal crisis is a life-threatening emergency. Consider in any patient on chronic steroids who develops haemodynamic instability under physiological stress (illness, surgery, vomiting preventing oral doses).

Symptoms

  • Profound hypotension (hallmark) — vasoplegic, catecholamine-resistant
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (acute abdomen mimicry)
  • Fever or hypothermia
  • Severe fatigue, confusion, altered consciousness
  • Hypoglycaemia (especially children)

Biochemistry

  • Hyponatraemia (Na <130 mEq/L)
  • Hyperkalaemia (K >5.5 mEq/L)
  • Hypoglycaemia (<3.5 mmol/L)
  • Eosinophilia (clue to adrenal insufficiency)
  • Pre-renal azotaemia
  • Random cortisol <100 nmol/L = adrenal insufficiency until proven otherwise

⚡ Emergency Treatment Protocol

DO NOT delay treatment to await cortisol result. Draw blood for cortisol + ACTH simultaneously, then treat immediately without waiting for results.
  1. Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV bolus immediately
  2. 0.9% NaCl 1 L IV over 30–60 min (correct hypovolaemia + hyponatraemia)
  3. 50% glucose 50 mL IV if hypoglycaemic (BGL <3.5 mmol/L)
  4. Hydrocortisone 50 mg IV q6h OR 200 mg/24h continuous infusion
  5. Identify and treat precipitating cause (infection, surgery, missed doses)
  6. Monitor BGL hourly; electrolytes q4–6h; continuous BP
  7. Transition to oral HC (20 mg AM / 10 mg PM) when stable, then taper to maintenance
  8. Fludrocortisone not needed during crisis — HC at >50 mg/day provides sufficient MC activity
Prevention: All patients on chronic steroids (≥5 mg/day for ≥1 month) must carry a Steroid Emergency Card and medical alert ID. Instruct to: double their dose during febrile illness, injury, or vomiting; seek emergency care if unable to take oral medication; never miss a dose without medical advice.
Glucocorticoid-Induced Hyperglycaemia

📐 Pattern & Monitoring

GC cause predominant postprandial hyperglycaemia by inducing hepatic gluconeogenesis and peripheral insulin resistance. Fasting glucose may be near-normal while postprandial peaks are severely elevated. HbA1c misses acute GC-induced hyperglycaemia.

💊 Insulin Management

Morning prednisolone / prednisone (once daily)

  • Hyperglycaemia peaks 6–12 h after morning dose
  • Isophane (NPH) insulin at lunchtime matches the pharmacokinetic peak effectively
  • Alternative: short-acting insulin with main meal + sliding scale

Divided doses or continuous IV GC

  • Sustained hyperglycaemia throughout 24 h
  • Basal-bolus insulin regimen preferred; insulin infusion for IV doses >200 mg HC/day

Tapering GC

  • Reduce insulin proportionally as GC tapered — hypoglycaemia risk if insulin not adjusted
  • After short burst: glucose normalises rapidly — discontinue insulin simultaneously with GC
  • Pre-existing DM: may need permanent insulin regimen intensification if GC >3 months
Pulse MP 500–1000 mg: Severe hyperglycaemia (BGL >20 mmol/L) common. Monitor 4-hourly. Insulin infusion or aggressive subcutaneous regimen required. Risk of HHS in elderly Type 2 DM.

Special Populations

Special Considerations

PopulationKey considerations
PregnancyHydrocortisone / prednisolone preferred (high placental 11β-HSD2 inactivation). Avoid dexamethasone/betamethasone (full placental transfer) unless intentional (foetal lung maturity — 12 mg IM × 2 doses). Monitor for gestational DM. Adrenal insufficiency in mother: stress dose during labour (HC 100 mg IV at delivery).
Elderly (>70)Highest risk for GIOP (osteoporosis already present), cognitive effects, hyperglycaemia, and infection. Use lowest effective dose. Bisphosphonate prophylaxis from day 1 if ≥5 mg for ≥3 months. Falls risk assessment.
ChildrenGrowth retardation with supraphysiological chronic doses. Use weight-based dosing. Alternate-day regimens when possible. Bone density monitoring. Adhere to physiological replacement doses (hydrocortisone 8–10 mg/m²/day in 3 divided doses for AI).
Hepatic failureAvoid prednisone (requires hepatic conversion). Use prednisolone directly. Dexamethasone, hydrocortisone, and methylprednisolone are safe alternatives.
Renal failureHydrocortisone and methylprednisolone preferred. Accumulation of some metabolites in severe CKD. Fluid retention risk higher with MC-active agents. Dialysis not efficiently removes GCs.
Diabetes mellitusGC dramatically worsens postprandial glycaemia. Intermediate-acting insulin (NPH) timed with GC peaks may be needed. Monitoring QID or continuous. Risk of HHS with high-dose IV GC.
