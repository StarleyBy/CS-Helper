1. Electrophysiological Foundations: Waves, Intervals, Segments, and Norms

Technical calibration configurations, physiological parameters, and morphological metrics.

⚙️ Technical Specifications & Calibration

  • Paper Speed Calibration:
    • 25 mm/s: 1 mm small square = 40 ms; 5 mm large square = 200 ms.
    • 50 mm/s: 1 mm small square = 20 ms; 5 mm large square = 100 ms.
  • Voltage Gain: Standard configuration is 10 mm/mV. Half-gain (5 mm/mV) or double-gain (20 mm/mV) settings alter voltages drastically. Check calibration spikes before diagnosing low or high voltage syndromes.
  • Filter Configurations: Diagnostic monitoring mode (0.05–150 Hz) is mandatory for ST/T evaluation. Monitoring filter mode (0.5–40 Hz) artificially alters and introduces false ST segment shifts and suppresses fine components like pacemaker spikes or Epsilon waves.

⏱️ The QT & QTc Interval Dynamics

Normal upper boundary limits: ♂ ≤ 450 ms and ♀ ≤ 460 ms. Value crossing > 500 ms marks critical high-vulnerability threshold for inducing automaticity and triggered *Torsades de Pointes* (TdP).

⚠️ Mathematical Formula Operational Boundaries:
• Bazett's Formula: QTc = QT / √RR. Clinically unstable at extreme parameters; severly overcorrects at rapid heart rates and undercorrects during significant bradycardia.
• Fridericia's Formula: QTc = QT / ∛RR. Highly stable and statistically preferred formula for tachycardic or bradycardic boundaries.

3. Chamber Enlargement, Hypertrophy, and Strain Dynamics

Voltage metrics, tracking equations, and secondary repolarization phenotypes.

🫀 Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH) Criteria

  • Sokolow-Lyon Index: S(V1) + R(V5 or V6) ≥ 35 mm or R(aVL) ≥ 11 mm.
  • Cornell Voltage Criteria: R(aVL) + S(V3) > 28 mm in males or > 20 mm in females. Highly specific framework.
  • Peguero-Lo Presti Index (Superior Sensitivity): Deepest S wave in any single lead (S_SD) + S(V4) ≥ 4 mm (males) / ≥ 3 mm (females).
  • Romhilt-Estes Scoring System: Point-based matrix scoring voltage, LAE, left axis deviation, QRS duration, and delayed intrinsicoid deflection.
  • LVH Strain Pattern: Asymmetric, smoothly downsloping ST segment depressions coupled with deep asymmetrical T-wave inversions in the lateral tracking leads (I, aVL, V5, V6). Represent structural remodeling, not acute primary ischemia.

RV & Atrial Enlargement Criteria

  • Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH):
    • Sokolow-Lyon RV Index: R(V1) + S(V5/V6) > 10.5 mm.
    • Dominant R wave in V1 with an R/S ratio > 1.0; right axis deviation present.
    • RV Strain Pattern: ST depression and T-wave inversion across V1–V3 and inferior leads.
  • Left Atrial Enlargement (P-mitrale): Notched P wave in limb leads with an inter-peak notch interval > 40 ms; total P duration > 120 ms. Biphasic P wave in V1 with terminal negative component ≥ 1 mm deep and ≥ 40 ms wide.
  • Right Atrial Enlargement (P-pulmonale): Tall, sharp, peaked P wave voltage amplitude > 2.5 mm in inferior anchors (II, III, aVF) or > 1.5 mm inside V1/V2 leads.

4. Sinoatrial Node Dysfunction and Atrial Arrhythmias

Exit blocks, macro-reentrant macrocircuits, and automated focus mapping.

⏳ Sinoatrial (SA) Exit Blocks Matrix

  • First-Degree SA Block: Conduction elongation from pacemaker cells to the surrounding atrium occurs but remains unviewable on surface standard ECG grids.
  • Second-Degree SA Block Type I (Wenckebach): Grouped beating intervals with progressive PP interval shortening sequence until a P-QRS complex drops completely. The resulting pause duration is mathematically less than double the preceding PP cycle.
  • Second-Degree SA Block Type II: Intermittent abrupt dropped P wave complexes without prior interval changes. The pause duration is an exact integer multiple of the baseline PP cycle.
  • Third-Degree SA Block / Sinus Arrest: Total absence of sinus tracking P waves. Relies on junctional or ventricular escape focuses.

🦅 Macro-Reentrant Atrial Circuits

Atrial Flutter:

  • Typical (Cavo-Tricuspid Isthmus Dependent): Counter-clockwise re-entry yields deep negative "sawtooth" configurations in leads II, III, aVF, and upright positive waves in V1. Clockwise variants flip these directional polarities.
  • Conduction Ratios: Usually locked at fixed ratios (2:1, 3:1, 4:1). A critical danger occurs during 1:1 rapid conduction, mimicking hyper-unstable wide Ventricular Tachycardia if aberrant pathways co-exist.

Atrial Fibrillation (AF): Completely chaotic, multiple micro-reentrant waves. Irregularly irregular ventricular response pattern. Coarse variants imply structural trial enlargement metrics.

⚡ Focal & Chaotic Ectopic Arrhythmias

  • Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia (MAT): Heart tracking rate exceeding >100 bpm featuring ≥ 3 morphologically distinct P waves with completely varying, changing PP, PR, and RR intervals. Strongly linked with acute COPD exacerbation, severe hypoxemia, and acute hypomagnesemia.
  • The Ashman Phenomenon: Occurs when a long RR interval is immediately followed by a short RR cycle. The premature impulse tracks into the refractory period of one of the bundle branches (typically the Right Bundle branch), inducing an aberrant QRS complex tracking pattern. Mimics single or ventricular ectopy salvos.

Nodal vs. Infranodal block topology mapping and fascicular disease configurations.

🔱 Monofascicular Intraventricular Delay

  • Complete LBBB: QRS duration ≥ 120 ms. Broad, notched or slurred monophasic R waves in lateral hosts (I, aVL, V5, V6). Complete loss of standard septal q waves in lateral leads. Deep S waves in V1–V3.
  • Complete RBBB: QRS duration ≥ 120 ms. Triphasic rsr', rsR', or rSR' complexes in V1–V2 ("M-shaped" complex). Broad, wide, slurred S waves in lateral anchors (I, aVL, V5, V6).
  • Left Anterior Fascicular Block (LAFB): Strong Left Axis Deviation (-30° to -90°). Small q with tall R (qR complexes) in I and aVL; small r coupled with deep S (rS complexes) in II, III, aVF.
  • Left Posterior Fascicular Block (LPFB): Strong Right Axis Deviation (+90° to +180°) in the absolute absence of secondary drivers (like RVH or lateral wall loss). rS complexes in I and aVL; qR complexes in II, III, aVF.

⚠️ Bifascicular, Trifascicular, & Masquerading Blends

  • Bifascicular Syndrome: Combination of RBBB + LAFB (common) or RBBB + LPFB (highly unstable indicator of widespread disease).
  • Trifascicular Block Phenotypes:
    • Incomplete: Bifascicular presentation coupled with a prolonged 1st-degree AV block pattern.
    • Alternating Bundle Branch Block: RBBB and LBBB alternating on successive traces. Signifies critical, near-total failure of the entire conduction apparatus.
  • Masquerading Bundle Branch Block: Precordial leads exhibit clear RBBB, but limb leads exhibit an LBBB morphology. Indicates extensive bilateral structural destruction.

6. Pre-excitation Syndromes and Accessory Pathways

Delta physics, accessory pathway tracking, and pre-excited tachyarrhythmia traps.

⚡ Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Structural Matrix

The standard presentation displays a triad: shortened PR interval (<120 ms), slurred initial upstroke of the QRS complex (Delta Wave), and a widened QRS complex (>110 ms) with secondary repolarization changes tracking discordant to the primary vector.

/\ / \ <-- Normal QRS / \ _/\ / \ <-- WPW Pre-excitation Delta Wave slurring initial vector / \

Pathway Localization (Arruda & Gallagher Principles): Map the Delta wave and QRS polarity in V1: a dominant upright vector (Positive/Type A) confirms a left-sided pathway tracking vector; a downsloping negative vector in V1 (Negative/Type B) tracks a right-sided pathway alignment across the tricuspid valve.

🚨 Pre-Excited Atrial Fibrillation & FBI Syndrome

When AF occurs down a manifest accessory pathway, it bypasses the rate-limiting filtering effects of the AV node. It presents as the lethal FBI Syndrome: Fast, Broad, Irregular.

  • Ventricular response rates often exceed 250 to 300+ bpm due to the brief refractory periods of the bypass pathway.
  • Morphologies change continuously from beat to beat based on the degree of bypass pathway utilization vs. AV node tracking.
  • Absolute Contraindication Warning: NEVER administer AV nodal blocking drugs (Adenosine, Beta-blockers, Verapamil, Diltiazem, Digoxin). Blocking the AV node removes competitive tracking and accelerates conduction down the accessory pathway, causing degeneration into fatal Ventricular Fibrillation (VF). Use Procainamide or immediate synchronized electrical cardioversion.

7. Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardias (SVT)

Dual AV nodal tracks, macro-reentry dynamics, and RP relationship paradigms.

🔄 AV Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia (AVNRT)

  • Typical (Slow-Fast / Common): Uses dual pathways inside the AV node. Anterograde conduction tracks down the slow pathway; retrograde conduction speeds up the fast pathway. Retrograde P waves are hidden within the QRS or present as a pseudo-R' wave in V1 or a pseudo-S wave in inferior leads.
  • Atypical (Fast-Slow): Reverses tracking. Anterograde conduction uses the fast track; retrograde tracks up the slow pathway. This yields a long-RP tachycardia pattern with inverted retrograde P waves preceding the QRS complex.

⏱️ The RP Interval Diagnostic Paradigm

Differentiate narrow complex regular tachycardias by calculating the position of the retrograde P wave relative to the preceding R wave:

  • Short-RP Tachycardias (RP < PR or RP < 90 ms): Typical AVNRT (P wave near simultaneous with QRS), Orthodromic AVRT (P wave tracking clearly visible within the ST segment frame).
  • Long-RP Tachycardias (RP > PR): Atypical AVNRT, Focal Atrial Tachycardia (FAT), Permanent Junctional Reciprocating Tachycardia (PJRT).

8. Wide QRS Complex Tachycardias: VT vs. SVT with Aberrancy

Stepwise algorithmic differentiation criteria for regular wide-complex rhythms.

⚖️ The Core Diagnostic Algorithms

The absolute foundational clinical rule: All regular wide complex tachycardias are Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) until proven otherwise.

1. The Brugada Framework Cascade:

  1. Absence of an RS complex across all precordial leads ($V_1$–$V_6$)? → VT
  2. RS interval length exceeding >100 ms in any single precordial lead? → VT
  3. Atrioventricular (AV) dissociation identified? → VT
  4. Specific morphology criteria matched in $V_1$–$V_2$ and $V_6$? → VT

2. The Vereckei Engine (Lead aVR Analysis):

  1. Presence of an initial dominant R wave in $aVR$? → VT
  2. Initial r or q wave duration width exceeding >40 ms? → VT
  3. Notching present on the initial downward limb of a negative QRS? → VT
  4. Ventricular activation velocity ratio ($V_i/V_t \le 1$)? → VT

🕵️‍♂️ Morphological Clues & Eponymous Signs

  • AV Dissociation Indicators: Look closely for independent P waves, Capture beats (briefly conducted normal narrow complexes), and Fusion beats (hybrid intermediate shapes showing combined activation).
  • Josephson's Sign: Fine notching or fragmentation located near the lowest point (nadir) of the deep S wave vector. Signifies VT tracking through myocardial scar borders.
  • Cabrera's Sign: Notching or stepping on the ascending (upward) limb of the deep S wave vector across precordial leads V1–V4. Confirms extensive history of prior myocardial infarction.
  • Precordial Concordance: Completely positive or completely negative vectors across all precordial leads V1 to V6. Confirms a ventricular focal origin.

9. Ventricular Arrhythmias and Topographic Mapping

Ectopic mapping, pathway tracking, and idiopathic channel focuses.

🗺️ Outflow Tract Topographic Mapping

  • Right Ventricular Outflow Tract (RVOT) focus: Displays a complete Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB) morphology coupled with a strongly vertical or inferior axis vector (strongly positive tracking in leads II, III, and aVF). Driven by intracellular calcium overload triggers. Response can be terminated with Adenosine.
  • Left Ventricular Outflow Tract (LVOT) focus: Exhibits LBBB tracking with early precordial transition zone shifts ($V_2$ or $V_3$), or complete Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB) architecture with a sharp inferior axis vector.
  • Belhassen Idiopathic Fascicular VT: Re-entry circuit localized within the left posterior fascicle network. Morphological signature presents an RBBB pattern coupled with extreme Left Axis Deviation. Highly responsive to Verapamil therapy. Do not mistake for ischemic VT.

🌀 Polymorphic Variants & Bidirectional Traps

  • Bidirectional VT: The frontal plane QRS axis alternates beat-to-beat (e.g., flipping between extreme LAD and extreme RAD). Strongly indicates acute Digitalis Toxicity or underlying Catecholaminergic Polymorphic Ventricular Tachycardia (CPVT).
  • Torsades de Pointes (TdP): Polymorphic VT displaying a continuous twisting of the main QRS vector around the isoelectric baseline. Must occur in the setting of a prolonged baseline QT/QTc interval. Typically initiated by a characteristic "short-long-short" sequence of RR cycle intervals.

⚠️ Lethal OMI Equivalents (No Classic STEMI Elements)

  • Wellens Syndrome (Critical LAD Stenosis): Captured exclusively during *pain-free* periods.
    • Type A: Biphasic T waves in V2 and V3.
    • Type B: Deep, highly symmetrical inverted T waves in V2 and V3.
    • Stress testing is strictly contraindicated due to the imminent risk of an extensive anterior wall occlusion. Inform the cath lab.
  • de Winter Sign (Hyperacute LAD Occlusion): Displays sharp, upsloping horizontal ST-segment depressions ($>1 \text{ mm}$ at the J-point) across precordial leads V1–V6, transitioning into tall, broad, symmetric hyperacute T waves, coupled with small ST elevations in lead aVR.
  • Aslanger Pattern: ST-segment elevation isolated *only* to lead III, with widespread ST depression across I, aVL, V4–V6 leads, and an elevated aVR. Indicates acute inferior OMI masked by multi-vessel disease or severe right ventricular ischemia.
  • South African Flag Sign: Localized ST elevation in leads I, aVL, and V2, paired with ST-segment depression in lead III. Confirms occlusion of a first diagonal branch of the LAD.

🛡️ Ischemia with Underlying Conduction Distortions

The Modified Smith-Sgarbossa Criteria (LBBB & Paced Rhythms): Validates acute ischemia despite widened conduction. Any single component matching triggers OMI alert status:

  1. Concordant ST Elevation: ST elevation $\ge 1$ mm in any single lead tracking a concordantly positive QRS vector. (Extremely specific).
  2. Concordant ST Depression: ST depression $\ge 1$ mm inside leads V1, V2, or V3.
  3. Modified Smith Ratio Criteria: Excessive discordant ST-segment elevation measured at the J-point, where the ratio of ST elevation relative to the preceding terminal negative S wave is ST/S ≤ -0.25 (ST elevation amplitude represents $\ge 25\%$ of the total S wave depth).

11. Myocardial Scars, Chronic Infarction, and Non-Ischemic Q-Waves

Tissue necrosis metrics, vector drops, and non-ischemic mimics.

🪦 Pathological Q-Wave Tracking Matrix

Confirms localized transmural tissue loss and scar formation. Criteria require:

  • Any visible Q wave vector in right precordial leads $V_1$ through $V_3$.
  • Q-wave duration width ≥ 30 ms (0.03 s) coupled with a depth amplitude ≥ 1 mm or representing ≥ 25% of the subsequent R-wave vector inside leads I, II, aVL, aVF, or V4–V6. Must track across at least two anatomically contiguous leads.
  • Equale's Sign: Stable pathological T-wave flattening or inversion adjacent to Q-wave territories without active ST-segment displacement. Confirms a mature, chronic scar tracking vector.
🔍 Non-Ischemic Pseudo-Infarction Mimics: Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (deep septal Q waves in lateral leads), pre-excitation syndromes (negative delta waves mimicking path Q vectors in inferior leads), and deep QS vectors in V1-V3 due to complete LBBB layout.

12. Channelopathies, Cardiomyopathies, and Sudden Cardiac Death (SCD) Syndromes

Genetic ion-channel failures and structural mechanical mutations.

🇧 Brugada Syndrome Diagnostics

  • Type 1 (Diagnostic Phenotype): Widespread coved (convex) ST elevation ≥ 2 mm in at least one right precordial lead ($V_1$ or $V_2$) positioned in the standard or high (2nd/3rd intercostal space) baseline grid, leading into a symmetric, inverted T wave.
  • Type 2 (Suspicious Phenotype): "Saddle-back" ST morphology with elevation $\ge 2$ mm dropping into a positive or broad biphasic T wave structure.
  • Unmasked by high fevers or administration of Class Ic sodium blockers. Avoid list database at brugadadrugs.org.

🧬 Congenital Long & Short QT Syndromes

  • LQT1 Phenotype (60%): *KCNQ1* mutation. Arrhythmia triggered by exercise or swimming stress. Displays broad, prolonged, early-onset T waves.
  • LQT2 Phenotype (35%): *KCNH2* mutation. Arrhythmia triggered by sudden acoustic stimuli (alarms). Displays low-amplitude, notched or bifid T waves.
  • LQT3 Phenotype (5%): *SCN5A* mutation. Driven by bradycardia; events occur during sleep. Displays late-onset T waves with a long, flat ST segment.
  • Short QT Syndrome (SQTS): Confirmed when QTc ≤ 340 ms. Characterized by near-absent ST segments and tall, narrow, symmetrical T waves. High rate of spontaneous early AF/VF.

🫀 Cardiomyopathic Sudden Death Triggers

  • Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM): Displays huge precordial voltages. Features deep, sharp, narrow "dagger-like" Q waves inside lateral and inferior leads (septal hypertrophy vectors). Apical HCM (Yamaguchi Syndrome) displays giant, deeply inverted precordial T waves ($> -10 \text{ mm}$) tracking from V3 through V6.
  • Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy (ARVC):
    • The Epsilon Wave: A distinct, low-amplitude electrical notch or post-excitation deflection between the end of the QRS complex and the start of the ST segment inside right precordial leads V1–V3.
    • T-wave inversions across leads V1–V4 in individuals over 14 years old (in the absence of complete RBBB).

14. Systemic, Pulmonary, and Extra-Cardiac Conditions

Mechanical shifts, macro-vascular blocks, and central neurological tracking loops.

🫁 Pulmonary Embolism & Acute Cor Pulmonale

  • Sinus Tachycardia: The most common, non-specific presentation.
  • Simultaneous T-Wave Inversions (Highly Specific): Concurrent, structural T-wave inversions across *inferior leads* (II, III, aVF) AND *right precordial leads* (V1–V4). Confirms severe, acute right ventricular strain.
  • The S1Q3T3 Pattern (McGinn-White Sign): Deep S wave in lead I, pathological Q wave vector in lead III, and an inverted T wave in lead III. Present in only 15–20% of acute massive PE presentations.
  • Acute new-onset complete or incomplete RBBB, right axis deviation shifts, and clockwise rotation of the precordial transition zone out to V5–V6.

🕊️ Pericardial Syndromes & Tamponade

  • Acute Pericarditis Stage I: Widespread, diffuse concave upsloping ST elevations across almost all leads (except aVR and V1), accompanied by widespread PR-segment depression. Lead aVR exhibits reciprocal ST depression and PR segment elevation.
  • Spodick's Sign: Downward-sloping line of the TP segment baseline, seen clearly in up to 80% of acute pericarditis cases.
  • Massive Tamponade / Effusion: Low QRS voltage across all leads, coupled with Electrical Alternans (the amplitude, axis, and polarity of the QRS complexes alternate beat-to-beat as the heart swings within the fluid-filled pericardial sac).

🧠 Central Nervous System Intracranial Catastrophes

The Neurogenic T-Wave Pattern: Classically unmasked during acute subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH), massive ischemic strokes, or severe traumatic brain injuries. Features massive, widely broad-based, deeply inverted "cerebral" T waves across precordial leads, along with substantial QTc prolongation. Mimics ischemia due to central autonomic dysfunction.

15. Cardiac Implantable Electronic Devices (CIED): Pacing and Malfunctions

Hardware synchronization tracking, sensing failures, and endless loop tracking.

🩻 Pacing Architectures & Vectors

  • RV Apical Pacing Vector: Features a pacing spike followed by a widened QRS complex that mimics a complete LBBB morphology with a strong Left Axis Deviation.
  • Biventricular Pacing / CRT: Displays a dominant, upright R wave vector in lead $V_1$ (R-wave dominant, mimicking an RBBB pattern). This confirms electrical activation coming from the epicardial lead placed over the left ventricular lateral wall.

⚠️ Hardware Function Failures

  • Failure to Pace: Total absence of pacing spike outputs despite the patient's intrinsic tracking heart rate falling well below the programmed lower rate limits.
  • Failure to Capture: Pacing spikes fire normally at regular intervals, but they fail to trigger a subsequent myocardial depolarization wave (no P wave or QRS complex follows the spike).
  • Undersensing Malfunction: The device fails to see intrinsic baseline cardiac signals. It fires asynchronously, introducing a critical danger of triggering VF if a pacing spike lands on an intrinsic T wave (Spike-on-T Phenomenon).
  • Pacemaker-Mediated Tachycardia (PMT): An endless-loop tracking rhythm where the device senses retrograde P waves and fires ventricular pacing spikes at the upper tracking limit. Can be terminated by applying a magnet to switch the device into VOO/DOO mode.

16. Advanced Pediatric Electrocardiography

Evolutionary baseline developments and neonatal structural markers.

👶 Age-Dependent Evolution Matrix

  • Neonatal Dominance: Right ventricular dominance is physiological in neonates and infants due to fetal circulation tracking. It presents as normal Right Axis Deviation ($>110^{\circ}$) and a dominant, tall R wave inside lead $V_1$. This gradually shifts leftward over the first years of development.
  • The Juvenile T-Wave Pattern: Inverted T waves extending from lead $V_1$ through $V_3$ are entirely normal findings in children up to early adolescence. They should not be mistaken for ischemia or strain patterns.
  • Katz-Wachtel Phenomenon: Massive, equiphasic (biphasic) QRS complexes with huge combined voltage amplitudes tracking across the mid-precordial leads ($V_2$–$V_4$). This finding is pathognomonic for congenital heart defects causing severe biventricular hypertrophy, such as a large Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD) or an Atrioventricular Septal Defect (AVSD).
