🧭 Cardiac axis (I + aVF)

  • I↑ aVF↑ → normal (-30° to +90°)
  • I↑ aVF↓ → left axis deviation (LAFB, LVH, inferior MI)
  • I↓ aVF↑ → right axis deviation (RVH, PE, COPD)
  • I↓ aVF↓ → northwest axis → VT until proven (90% spec!)
💡 Tip: Isoelectric lead method – the axis is perpendicular to the most equiphasic QRS. Useful when quadrant ambiguous.

🕰️ Bradyarrhythmias & AV block

🔻 Second‑degree AV block

Mobitz I (Wenckebach) Progressive PR prolongation → dropped QRS. Usually benign, AV node level.

Mobitz II Constant PR with intermittent dropped QRS. Infranodal, high risk of complete block → pacemaker.

2:1 block – can’t classify from single strip; look at QRS width (wide favors Mobitz II).

🛑 Complete heart block

Complete AV dissociation, atrial rate > ventricular rate.

• Junctional escape (narrow QRS, 40‑60) – block at AV node
• Ventricular escape (wide QRS, 20‑40) – infranodal block, worse prognosis

💡 Narrow complex complete HB often responds to atropine (AV nodal ischaemia in inferior MI). Wide complex → atropine ineffective, needs pacing.

⚡ Wide complex tachycardia (QRS ≥120 ms)

🚨 GOLDEN RULE

All regular wide complex tachycardias are VT until proven otherwise.

Brugada algorithm (stepwise):
1) Absence of RS in all precordial leads → VT
2) RS interval >100 ms in any lead → VT
3) AV dissociation → VT
4) Morphology criteria (V1/V6) → VT

▸ Morphology clues: taller left rabbit ear in V1 (VT) vs taller right (RBBB aberrancy)

🧩 Specific VT entities

🔸 RVOT VT – LBBB pattern, inferior axis, adenosine‑sensitive (often benign).
🔸 Bidirectional VT – alternating axis → digoxin toxicity until proven.
🔸 Torsade de Pointes – polymorphic with QRS twisting around baseline, always preceded by long QT. Treat with IV magnesium (2g), NOT amiodarone.

💡 Wellens’ syndrome – biphasic or deep T inversion in V2‑V3 during pain‑free period = critical LAD stenosis. Do not stress test; call cath lab.

⚠️ Patterns you must not miss

  • De Winter – upsloping STD + tall symmetric T waves in precordial leads (LAD occlusion, no STE!)
  • Left main / triple vessel – diffuse STD with STE in aVR > V1
  • Sgarbossa criteria (LBBB/ paced) – concordant STE ≥1 mm (5 pts), STD V1‑V3 (3 pts) → score ≥3 specific for MI
  • New RBBB + LAFB in chest pain → proximal LAD occlusion until disproven

🧪 Electrolytes, toxins & drugs – nuanced signs

⚡ Hyperkalaemia (progressive)

Peaked T → PR prolongation/P loss → wide QRS → sine wave → VF/asystole.
Calcium first! (membrane stabilization), then insulin+glucose, albuterol, kayexalate/dialysis.

T /‾\ → [P flattens] → ~~~~ (sine)

💧 Hypokalaemia

ST depression, prominent U wave (U > 1 mm), T‑U fusion → pseudo‑QT prolongation. Increases Torsade risk.

💊 Digoxin effect vs toxicity

Effect: downsloping “Salvador Dali” STD, short QT.
Toxicity: any arrhythmia – most common = ventricular bigeminy; highly specific = bidirectional VT or atrial tachycardia with block.

🧪 TCA / Na‑channel blocker toxicity

Wide QRS (>100 ms), terminal R wave >3 mm in aVR. Give IV sodium bicarbonate (target pH 7.50‑7.55).

💡 Hypocalcaemia – prolongs ST segment (QTc prolonged) but T wave normal.
Hypercalcaemia – short QT, no U waves.

🧬 Channelopathies & structural nuances

🇧 Brugada syndrome

Type 1 (diagnostic): coved STE ≥2 mm in V1‑V3 + negative T. Unmasked by fever, sodium blockers, cocaine.
Avoid procainamide, flecainide. ICD is only proven therapy.

🇱 Long QT syndrome (LQTS)

Romano‑Ward (AD), Jervell‑Lange (AR + deafness). QTc >500 ms high risk. Avoid QT prolonging drugs, competitive sports, hypokalaemia.

💡 “T‑wave alternans” (visible beat‑to‑beat T variation) often precedes Torsade.

💔 Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM)

Giant precordial voltages, dagger‑like Q waves in inferior/lateral leads (pseudoinfarct). Apical HCM → giant T inversion in V3‑V6 (“Yamaguchi”).

🫀 Arrhythmogenic RV dysplasia (ARVD)

Epsilon wave (small deflection after QRS in V1‑V3) + T inversion V1‑V4. Increased risk of VT of LBBB morphology.

🫁 Pulmonary embolism & pericardial syndromes

🩸 PE – specific ECG clue

Sinus tachycardia most common, but most specific for PE = simultaneous T inversion in inferior (II,III,aVF) AND right precordial (V1‑V4).
S₁Q₃T₃ (deep S in I, Q+ inverted T in III) – neither sensitive nor specific (20%).
💡 Remember: 18% of PE patients have completely normal ECG.

❤️ Acute pericarditis

Concave (“saddle‑shaped”) STE in most leads + PR depression (except aVR, V1). No reciprocal changes (unlike STEMI).
Stage I → widespread STE, Stage II → ST normalisation, Stage III → T inversion, Stage IV → normal.
Spodick’s sign – downsloping TP segment.

🩻 Paced rhythms & tricky artefacts

Pacemaker malfunction

  • Failure to capture – spike without P/QRS
  • Failure to sense – spike falls on intrinsic P/QRS (undersensing) or absent spike when expected (oversensing)
  • Pacemaker‑mediated tachycardia (PMT) – DDD mode, rate = upper tracking limit (~130 bpm). Apply magnet → VOO/DOO.
💡 Runaway pacemaker (rare, rate >200) – immediately place magnet over generator.

Electrode misplacement mimics

🔸 High V1/V2 → loss of R wave progression, Brugada‑like pattern.
🔸 Arm‑leg reversal → lead I inverted, aVR/aVL swap.
🔸 Tremor artefact – regular 8‑10 Hz oscillations mimicking atrial flutter.

👶 Paediatric ECG pearls

  • Newborn axis +30° to +190°, gradually shifts left with age.
  • Juvenile T wave pattern – T inversion in V1‑V3 normal up to adolescence.
  • RV dominance: R > S in V1 up to 6–12 months (normal).
  • Katz‑Wachtel phenomenon – biphasic (tall R + deep S) in mid‑precordial leads → biventricular hypertrophy (VSD/AVSD).

🧠 Clinical nuggets & “don’t forget”

🔸 WPW + pre‑excited AF

NEVER give AV nodal blockers (adenosine, verapamil, digoxin) – they divert conduction to accessory pathway → VF. Use procainamide or cardiovert.

🔸 Hypothermia

Osborne (J) waves at J‑point – seen in V3‑V5, proportional to degree of hypothermia. Also prolonged QT, bradycardia.

🔸 Takotsubo (stress cardiomyopathy)

ST elevation (often anterior), deep T inversion, QT prolongation, but no reciprocal changes – resolves spontaneously.

🎯 Quick clinical pearls

  • Ashman phenomenon – aberrant RBBB in AF after a long‑short cycle. Benign, not VT.
  • Electrical alternans (beat‑to‑beat QRS amplitude variation) → large pericardial effusion / tamponade.
  • LVH strain pattern – downsloping STD in lateral leads with asymmetrical T inversion, not ischaemia.
  • Inferior MI + RV infarct – V4R STE ≥1 mm, avoid nitrates/morphine, give fluids.
  • AF with very slow ventricular response – digoxin toxicity or sick sinus syndrome until proven.
