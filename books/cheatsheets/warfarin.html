📅 Warfarin Initiation Protocols

Standard vs. low-dose initiation based on patient sensitivity risk factors. All protocols require daily INR monitoring.

⚠️ Use LOW-DOSE protocol if ANY of the following present:
Advanced age (>75 years) · Low body weight / frailty · Small elderly female · Hepatic dysfunction (any degree) · Impaired renal function (use eGFR/CrCl, not serum creatinine alone — eGFR <30 mL/min indicates significant sensitivity) · Interacting medications (amiodarone, azole antifungals, metronidazole, TMP-SMX) · Reduced albumin · High baseline bleeding risk · Baseline INR >1.2

📊 INR Monitoring Schedule

Frequency:
  • Daily during initiation until target range achieved
  • 2–3×/week for first 1–2 weeks after reaching target
  • Weekly when INR stable ×2–3 consecutive readings
  • Every 2–4 weeks once stable on maintenance dose
  • Maximum interval: 4 weeks in very stable patients
Readiness for monitoring interval extension depends on multiple factors — not solely "2 consecutive days in range":
  • Indication and urgency (e.g. mechanical valve needs careful confirmation)
  • Inpatient vs outpatient setting
  • Concurrent heparin bridge being weaned
  • Concomitant interacting medications
  • Hepatic function and nutritional status
  • Rate of INR change (stable trend vs still rising)

⚙️ Quick Initiation — Step-by-Step

1

Baseline Assessment

INR, CBC, LFTs, renal function (CrCl/eGFR). Identify sensitivity factors. Document current medications for interactions.

2

Choose Protocol

No sensitivity factors → Standard (Day 1: 5 mg). Any sensitivity factor → Low-dose (Day 1: 2.5 mg). Evening dosing preferred (5–6 PM).

3

Daily INR-guided Dosing

Follow Day 1–6 tables above. Hold if INR >3.0, restart when <3.0 with dose reduction of 10–25%.

4

Therapeutic Maintenance

When INR stable in target range, transition to maintenance (typically 2.5–10 mg/day). Extend monitoring intervals gradually as described above. Dose changes: adjust by 10–20% of weekly total dose.

5

Discharge Education

Consistent vitamin K intake, evening dosing, monitoring plan, emergency contacts, alert card, avoid NSAIDs unless approved.

🩸 INR Reversal Protocols

Stratified by bleeding severity. Approach differs fundamentally between non-bleeding elevated INR and active haemorrhage.

Non-Bleeding Elevated INR

📋 Elevated INR — No Active Bleeding

INR RangeInterventionFollow-up
3.5–4.9 Reduce next dose by 10–20% or hold 1 dose. No vitamin K unless very high bleeding risk. Recheck INR in 24–48 h
5.0–8.9 Hold warfarin 1–2 days. Consider oral vitamin K 1–2.5 mg if elevated bleeding risk, urgent procedure planned, or patient elderly/frail. Not mandatory for all patients at this range. Recheck INR in 24 h
9.0–20.0 Hold warfarin. Oral vitamin K 2.5–5 mg. More urgent correction if procedure imminent. Recheck INR in 6–12 h
>20.0 Hold warfarin. Oral vitamin K 5–10 mg. Consider IV vitamin K 5–10 mg (over 30 min) if rapid correction required. Monitor for anaphylaxis (rare with slow IV infusion). Recheck INR in 4–6 h
Vitamin K route note: Oral vitamin K has predictable absorption and is preferred for non-emergency reversal. IV vitamin K (phytonadione) provides faster, more complete reversal within 6–12 h but carries rare anaphylaxis risk — always infuse slowly over ≥30 min. SC route is discouraged (erratic absorption).
Active Bleeding — Severity-Stratified Approach
⚠️ Critical principle: PCC is NOT indicated for every active bleeding. Approach is stratified by bleeding severity. Inappropriate PCC use increases thrombotic risk (DVT, PE, arterial thrombosis) — particularly dangerous in mechanical valve patients.

🟡 Minor Bleeding

Epistaxis, gum bleeding, minor skin lacerations, minor haematuria

  • Hold or adjust warfarin dose
  • Local haemostatic measures
  • Check INR and follow non-bleeding reversal table
  • Oral vitamin K 1–2.5 mg if INR markedly elevated
  • PCC NOT indicated for minor bleeding
  • Resume at lower dose once bleeding controlled

🔴 Clinically Significant / Major Bleeding

GI bleeding, haematuria requiring intervention, significant haematoma, post-surgical haemorrhage

  • Hold warfarin immediately
  • IV vitamin K 5–10 mg (over 30 min)
  • 4F-PCC: consider in significant bleeding with elevated INR, especially if rapid reversal needed or patient haemodynamically unstable — not automatic for all cases
  • FFP 15–30 mL/kg if PCC not available (slower, volume overload risk)
  • Source control (endoscopy, interventional radiology, surgery) is paramount
  • Recheck INR q30–60 min until stable

🆘 Life-Threatening / Intracranial Haemorrhage

Immediate coordinated action required — every minute matters in ICH
  1. Hold warfarin immediately
  2. IV vitamin K 10 mg slow infusion over 30 min (mandatory — prevents INR rebound after PCC)
  3. 4-factor PCC (see dosing table below) — first-line reversal agent for life-threatening bleeding
  4. FFP 15–30 mL/kg only if 4F-PCC unavailable
  5. Recheck INR 15–30 min after PCC administration; target rapid correction
  6. Neurosurgery/IR/surgical consultation for source control
INR target after PCC: Aim for rapid correction, generally to ≤1.5 in major/life-threatening bleeding, according to local protocol. Recheck INR shortly after PCC administration. The specific target may vary by indication and local practice — ≤1.5 is a widely used practical goal, not an absolute universal endpoint.
rFVIIa — NOT a routine alternative to PCC: rFVIIa is not recommended for routine warfarin reversal. Carries significant thrombotic risk (arterial + venous) and is not superior to 4F-PCC. Should not be used as a substitute for PCC. Any exceptional rescue use requires specialist haematology or haemostasis consultation.
4-Factor PCC Dosing (Kcentra / Beriplex / Octaplex)

⚖️ 4-Factor PCC — Weight-Based Dosing

Pre-treatment INRDose (units/kg Factor IX equivalent)Maximum dose
2.0–4.025 units/kg2,500 units

Concurrent IV vitamin K is mandatory — PCC alone produces only transient reversal (t½ of factors II and X is hours); vitamin K provides sustained suppression of warfarin effect. Without vitamin K, INR rebounds when PCC factors are consumed.
3-factor vs 4-factor PCC — critical distinction:
4-factor PCC (Kcentra [USA], Beriplex [Europe], Octaplex) contains factors II, VII, IX, X + Proteins C and S — preferred agent for warfarin reversal when available.
3-factor PCC (e.g. Profilnine SD, depending on local availability) contains II, IX, X but minimal or no factor VII — less effective for warfarin reversal alone; may require supplemental FFP to restore factor VII. Product availability varies by country — always verify your local formulary.
FFP: universal fallback but large volume required (15–30 mL/kg), slow onset, risk of volume overload and transfusion reactions.
Restarting Warfarin After Reversal

🔄 When to Resume Anticoagulation

INR <1.5 alone is NOT a universal criterion for warfarin restart after reversal. The decision depends on:
Factors favouring earlier restart:
  • High thromboembolic risk (mechanical valve, recent DVT/PE, AF with high CHA₂DS₂-VASc)
  • Bleeding source controlled (endoscopy, surgery)
  • Extracranial bleeding with haemostasis secured
  • Non-life-threatening bleeding episode
Factors favouring delayed restart:
  • Intracranial haemorrhage — typically delay 7–14+ days; individualize with neurosurgery
  • Ongoing or incompletely controlled bleeding
  • High rebleeding risk (unresectable lesion, coagulopathy)
  • Recent major surgery
Principle: Restart anticoagulation when adequate haemostasis is secured and the balance of thromboembolic and rebleeding risks favours resumption. Timing is indication- and bleeding-site-specific. Start with low dose (2.5 mg) and titrate based on INR response. Always document the restart decision rationale.

Anticoagulation thresholds (ESC 2020):
• Score 0 (male) or 1 (female sex only): anticoagulation not recommended
• Score 1 (male): individualised decision — consider anticoagulation
• Score ≥2 (male) or ≥3 (female including sex point): OAC recommended

Female sex category is a risk modifier, not an independent risk factor — a score of 1 in females (sex category only) does not independently meet the threshold for OAC.

Haemorrhagic risk must always be weighed against thromboembolic risk (CHA₂DS₂-VASc). Most patients with high HAS-BLED still have net benefit from OAC if CHA₂DS₂-VASc is elevated.

🔄 Perioperative Anticoagulation Management

Individualised bridging strategy — the decision is NOT universal. Driven by valve type, position, thromboembolic risk, and procedure-specific bleeding risk.

⚠️ Critical: Bridging is not required for all patients on warfarin. The BRIDGE trial (2015) demonstrated that bridging with LMWH in AF patients undergoing elective procedures was associated with increased bleeding without reducing stroke. Bridging decisions must be individualised based on thromboembolic risk stratification — particularly: valve type, valve position, additional risk factors, and procedure bleeding risk. Not all mechanical aortic valve patients require bridging.

Step 1 — Assess thromboembolic risk:

• Rheumatic mitral disease 		Bridging generally recommended
MODERATE • Aortic bileaflet mechanical valve + 1 additional risk factor (not multiple)
• Non-valvular AF with CHA₂DS₂-VASc 4–6

LOW • Modern bileaflet aortic mechanical valve (St. Jude, CarboMedics) — NO additional risk factors
• Non-valvular AF with CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≤3
• VTE >12 months ago, no other high-risk features 		Bridging often NOT required — discontinue warfarin, resume post-procedure
Step 2 — Assess procedure bleeding risk:
Low bleeding risk procedures (can often proceed at therapeutic INR):
Dental extractions, cataract surgery, dermatological procedures, minor gastrointestinal endoscopy, colonoscopy without polypectomy, pacemaker/ICD implantation (selected patients)
High bleeding risk procedures (warfarin interruption required):
Major abdominal/thoracic surgery, neurosurgery, urological procedures, bowel resection, cardiac surgery, hip/knee arthroplasty, polypectomy
Step 3 — LMWH vs UFH for bridging:
• LMWH (enoxaparin 1 mg/kg BID) preferred — outpatient, predictable, no monitoring (check anti-Xa if renal failure)
• UFH preferred: mechanical mitral valve + very high risk, renal failure (CrCl <30), when rapid reversibility needed (surgery imminent)

🔴 High-Risk Patient — Bridging Protocol

Pre-operative:
  • Stop warfarin 5 days pre-op (Day −5)
  • Check INR Day −2: if still >1.5, consider low-dose oral vitamin K
  • Start therapeutic LMWH (enoxaparin 1 mg/kg BID or dalteparin 100 units/kg BID) when INR <2.0 (typically Day −3)
  • Last LMWH dose: 24 h before procedure (BID regimen → last dose morning of Day −1)
  • If UFH: stop 4–6 h before; check aPTT <50 s before proceeding
  • Check INR on day of procedure: target <1.5
Post-operative:
  • Resume warfarin 12–24 h post-procedure when haemostasis secured (or pre-operative dose evening of surgery day)
  • Resume LMWH bridging 24–48 h post-op (48–72 h for high bleeding risk procedures)
  • Continue LMWH until INR therapeutic (≥2 consecutive readings in target range)
  • Check INR every 2–3 days until stable in range
  • For neuraxial/spinal anaesthesia: follow specific interval guidelines (ASRA guidelines) — typically LMWH ≥12–24 h after procedure, catheter removal
UFH bridging for mechanical mitral valve:
IV UFH target aPTT 60–80 s. Stop UFH 4 h before procedure. Resume 12–24 h post-op if haemostasis secure. Consider anti-Xa monitoring in obese patients or renal impairment.
Drug, Food & Herbal Interactions
Core principle: The most clinically important dietary interaction is consistency of vitamin K intake — not avoidance of green vegetables. Patients should maintain stable (not low) dietary vitamin K. Abrupt changes (e.g. starting a green juice diet or stopping it) cause INR fluctuations. Blanket avoidance of vegetables is not recommended.

🔺 Clinically Significant INR ↑ (Strong interactions)

  • Amiodarone — potent CYP2C9 inhibitor; effect may be delayed 2–6 weeks, lasts months; reduce warfarin dose by 30–50% when starting
  • Metronidazole / Tinidazole — CYP2C9 inhibition; short course can double INR
  • Trimethoprim-SMX — CYP2C9 inhibition + folate antagonism; check INR within 3–5 days of starting
  • Azole antifungals (fluconazole, itraconazole, voriconazole) — potent CYP2C9/3A4 inhibition
  • Ciprofloxacin — moderate effect; check INR 3–5 days after initiation
  • Aspirin / NSAIDs — pharmacodynamic interaction (↑ bleeding risk, not INR); major GI bleeding risk

🔻 Clinically Significant INR ↓ (Strong interactions)

  • Rifampicin / Rifampin — potent CYP2C9 inducer; may need 2–3× dose increase; takes 2 weeks to reach full effect; hazardous when stopped
  • Carbamazepine / Phenytoin / Phenobarbital — hepatic enzyme induction
  • Cholestyramine / Colesevelam — reduces warfarin absorption; take warfarin 1 h before or 4 h after
  • Nafcillin / Dicloxacillin — CYP2C9 induction
  • St. John's Wort — potent CYP2C9/P-gp inducer; unpredictable and prolonged effect
  • Dietary vitamin K — large acute changes (e.g. starting vitamin K supplement); encourage consistency rather than avoidance

⚠️ Variable or Less Predictable Interactions

These interactions are documented but the magnitude is variable and unpredictable — monitor INR more frequently rather than making pre-emptive dose changes:

  • Fish oils / omega-3 — mild antiplatelet + possible mild INR ↑; clinical significance uncertain
  • Grapefruit — CYP3A4 inhibition; minor effect on warfarin (primarily CYP2C9)
  • Mango — limited evidence; monitor if consuming regularly
  • Ginseng — possible INR ↓ via enzyme induction; variable
  • Cranberry juice — possible INR ↑ (inhibits CYP2C9); avoid large quantities
  • Simvastatin / Lovastatin — mild CYP2C9 inhibition; check INR 1–2 weeks after statin initiation
  • Avocado — contains vitamin K; large amounts may ↓ INR; maintain consistency

🧾 Patient Discharge Checklist

  • Take warfarin at the same time each day (evening preferred, 5–6 PM)
  • Do not double dose if missed — contact provider
  • Maintain consistent vitamin K intake (do not abruptly change green vegetable consumption)
  • Report any bleeding: haematuria, black/tarry stools, unusual bruising, epistaxis, gingival bleeding, haemoptysis
  • Use soft toothbrush and electric razor. Avoid NSAIDs unless explicitly approved by physician.
  • Carry warfarin alert card and complete medication list at all times
  • Limit alcohol to ≤1 drink/day (acute alcohol inhibits; chronic use induces warfarin metabolism)
  • Inform ALL prescribers and dentists of warfarin use before any new prescription or procedure
Emergency — seek immediate care if: Sudden severe headache, vision changes, weakness/numbness (possible ICH) · Coughing/vomiting blood · Black or blood-streaked stools · Fall with head trauma (ICH risk even with seemingly mild head injury)
INR monitoring reminder: Never miss a scheduled INR check. Carry the anticoagulation clinic number. Report any new prescription or OTC medication (including vitamins and supplements) before starting.
