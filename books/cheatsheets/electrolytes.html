Hyperkalemia

🔴 Aetiology (Post-Cardiac Surgery)

  • High-K⁺ cardioplegia retention (especially oliguric patients)
  • Low cardiac output / oliguric renal failure → ↓K⁺ excretion
  • Severe tissue ischaemia (IABP complication, mesenteric ischaemia, haemolysis)
  • Drugs: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, K⁺-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, β-blockers
  • Hyperglycaemia (insulin deficiency shifts K⁺ out of cells)
  • Acidosis → K⁺ shift. Note: the relationship is highly variable and depends on the type of acidosis. The classic “0.4–0.6 mEq/L per 0.1 pH unit” rule applies primarily to mineral (hyperchloraemic) acidosis — the effect is significantly attenuated or absent in lactic acidosis and organic acidoses.
  • Haemolysis (CPB, IABP haemolysis, massive transfusion)

ECG Progression

  • Early (>5.5–6.0): Peaked, symmetrical, narrow-based T waves (best in V2–V4)
  • Moderate (>6.0–6.5): PR prolongation, P wave flattening then loss
  • Severe (>6.5–7.0): QRS widening, RBBB or LBBB pattern
  • Critical (>7.0): Sine wave pattern → VF / asystole
  • Paced patients: Increased capture threshold, loss of capture, widened paced QRS — hyperkalemia may be the first sign of rising K⁺ in paced patients where P waves are absent

🚨 Emergency Treatment Criteria

Immediate treatment indicated when:
• K⁺ ≥6.5 mEq/L regardless of ECG
• K⁺ 6.0–6.4 mEq/L with any ECG changes attributable to hyperkalaemia
• Rapidly rising K⁺ in acute kidney injury (rate matters, not just absolute value)
• Any K⁺ level with haemodynamic instability or arrhythmia suggestive of hyperkalaemia

Stepwise Hyperkalemic Emergency Algorithm

🚨 Immediate Management Sequence

1

Membrane Stabilisation

Calcium gluconate 10% — 10 mL IV over 2–3 min. Repeat after 5–10 min if ECG abnormalities persist. Acts within 1–3 min. Does NOT lower serum K⁺. Give first regardless of whether K⁺ result is yet available if ECG changes present.

2

Intracellular Shift

Insulin 10 units IV + dextrose 25–50 g (adjust to baseline BGL). Onset 10–20 min. Lowers K⁺ by 0.5–1.5 mEq/L temporarily. + Albuterol 10–20 mg nebulised (additive effect via different mechanism). + Sodium bicarbonate 50–100 mEq if pH <7.2 (modest additional effect).

3

Elimination

Furosemide 40–80 mg IV if urine output adequate. Emergency haemodialysis or CRRT if K⁺ >7.0, refractory, or severe renal failure. Most rapid and reliable elimination method.

4

Identify & Treat Cause

Review medications (stop ACE-I, ARBs, K⁺-sparing diuretics). Address renal perfusion (optimise cardiac output). Check for haemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, ongoing ischaemia. Serial K⁺ every 1–2 h until stable.

Paced patients — special consideration: In patients with permanent or temporary pacing, the inability to assess native P waves may delay ECG recognition of hyperkalaemia. Monitor for increasing capture threshold (↑ output required to maintain capture) and widening of paced QRS complex — early warning signs of significant K⁺ elevation.
Hyperkalaemia + acidosis: Treat the acidosis (bicarbonate, ventilation adjustment, vasopressor for hypoperfusion) as a parallel strategy. However, bicarbonate alone is a weak anti-hyperkalaemic agent and should not replace insulin/glucose as the primary shift therapy. Bicarbonate is most effective in hyperchloraemic (normal-AG) metabolic acidosis; it has a minimal effect in lactic acidosis.
CPB-related hyperkalaemia: Post-cardioplegia K⁺ surge is common. Cardioplegia solutions (especially St. Thomas’) contain high K⁺ (20 mEq/L or more). Oliguric patients are at highest risk. Pre-emptive low-dose insulin + furosemide before rewarming may be appropriate in high-risk patients.
Hypokalemia

⚠️ Aetiology & Consequences

Causes after cardiac surgery

  • Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide) — most common cause; kaliuresis
  • Insulin infusion (redistributive; K⁺ into cells)
  • Hypomagnesaemia — impairs renal K⁺ retention and intracellular uptake; K⁺ repletion will fail until Mg²⁺ is corrected
  • Metabolic or respiratory alkalosis (alkalosis promotes K⁺ entry into cells)
  • Nasogastric drainage, vomiting, diarrhoea
  • Poor pre-operative nutrition

ECG & Clinical Consequences

  • U waves (prominent after T wave, best in V2–V4) — most characteristic
  • ST depression, T wave flattening or inversion, QT/QU prolongation
  • Atrial ectopy (PACs), atrial fibrillation, VT/VF (especially with digitalis)
  • Torsades de Pointes (TdP) if concurrent QT prolongation
  • Enhances digoxin toxicity — hypoK⁺ shifts digoxin receptor kinetics
  • Muscle weakness, ileus, rhabdomyolysis (severe <2.5 mEq/L)

⚖️ Treatment Protocol

Replacement — IV KCl

  • Central line preferred: 10–20 mEq/h; maximum 40 mEq/h only in ICU with continuous ECG monitoring on central line — 40 mEq/h is a critical care-only rate requiring continuous monitoring
  • Peripheral IV: Maximum concentration 40 mEq/L (8 mEq/100 mL) to avoid chemical phlebitis
  • Rate estimate: A rough estimate is that 10–20 mEq of KCl may increase serum K⁺ by approximately 0.1 mEq/L — however, this is highly variable and depends on total body deficit, renal function, ongoing losses, and acid-base status. Verify by serial measurement after each 20–40 mEq given.
  • Never run concentrated KCl through syringe pump without dedicated infusion pump safety guard

Always Check Magnesium First

Refractory hypoK⁺: If K⁺ does not rise despite replacement, check Mg²⁺. If Mg <1.5 mg/dL: give MgSO₄ 2–4 g IV first. Without magnesium correction, the ROMK channel in the kidney continues to waste K⁺ regardless of how much is replaced.

DKA-specific rule

In diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): insulin should generally be withheld until K⁺ ≥3.3 mEq/L. Insulin drives K⁺ into cells and can precipitate life-threatening hypokalaemia in the K⁺-depleted DKA patient despite apparent normal or elevated serum K⁺. This rule does NOT apply to routine post-cardiac surgery insulin management.

Oral Replacement (after extubation)

  • KCl 20–40 mEq PO QID for moderate deficits (K⁺ 3.0–3.5)
  • For K⁺ <3.0: may need 60–100 mEq/day PO in divided doses
  • Sustained-release preparations preferred (less GI irritation)

Hypocalcaemia

🧲 Aetiology Post-CPB

  • Haemodilution — most common cause of low ionised Ca²⁺ after CPB
  • Citrate chelation from blood products (massive transfusion) — significant with >1 unit/5 min
  • Hypothermia shifts Ca²⁺ to albumin (protein binding increases)
  • Hypoparathyroidism (after thyroid or parathyroid surgery)
  • Vitamin D deficiency (common in chronically ill patients)
  • Alkalosis (increased albumin binding → ↓ ionised fraction)
  • Furosemide and loop diuretics (calciuresis)
  • Hypomagnesaemia (impairs PTH release — concurrent deficiency common)

Clinical Features

  • Cardiac: ↓ myocardial contractility (iCa <0.9 mmol/L), ↓ vascular tone, QT prolongation, arrhythmia (rare)
  • Neuromuscular: paraesthesiae, tetany, Chvostek’s sign, Trousseau’s sign, seizures
  • Severe (<0.8): Laryngeal spasm, bronchospasm, cardiac arrest (rare)
Routine prophylactic calcium at CPB separation: Despite widespread practice, routine prophylactic calcium administration at separation from bypass has not consistently improved haemodynamic outcomes in prospective studies. Calcium use should be targeted to documented ionised hypocalcaemia (iCa <1.0–1.1 mmol/L) or specific indications rather than routine administration. Check ionised Ca²⁺ before giving.

Specific Indications Beyond Hypocalcaemia

  • Massive transfusion: Give 1 g CaCl₂ or 2 g Ca gluconate per 4–6 units of packed red cells — citrate chelation is major cause of haemodynamic instability in massive transfusion
  • Hypermagnesaemia: Calcium antagonises Mg²⁺ at the neuromuscular junction and myocardium — gives 1–2 g CaCl₂ IV while awaiting renal clearance of Mg²⁺
  • Calcium-channel blocker (CCB) overdose: High-dose CaCl₂ 1–2 g IV (repeat as needed) reverses negative inotropy and chronotropy; may require very high doses; consider calcium infusion
  • Cardiac arrest with ACLS: Calcium NOT recommended routinely (Class III: harm) unless documented hypocalcaemia, hyperkalaemia, or CCB/magnesium toxicity present
Hypophosphataemia — Underappreciated but Critical Post-CPB

☔️ Prevalence & Aetiology

Hypophosphataemia is extremely common after cardiac surgery. Phosphate falls in >50–70% of patients post-CPB due to haemodilution, increased urinary excretion, catecholamine-driven intracellular shift, and insulin therapy. Severe hypophosphataemia (PO₄³⁻ <0.5 mmol/L) may directly impair weaning from mechanical ventilation and contribute to low cardiac output.

Clinical Consequences of Severe Hypophosphataemia (<0.5 mmol/L)

  • Respiratory muscle weakness: Diaphragmatic and intercostal muscle dysfunction → inability to wean from ventilator — most clinically important consequence post-cardiac surgery
  • Cardiac: ↓ myocardial contractility (phosphate depletion impairs high-energy phosphate metabolism, ATP synthesis, and Ca²⁺ handling)
  • Haematological: Haemolytic anaemia, impaired phagocyte function
  • Metabolic: Insulin resistance, impaired gluconeogenesis
  • Refeeding syndrome: Most severe phosphate depletion occurs during rapid nutritional refeeding in malnourished patients

Hypomagnesaemia

💡 Prevalence & Clinical Impact Post-CPB

Hypomagnesaemia is present in approximately 50–70% of patients after CPB (haemodilution, diuresis, PPI-associated intestinal Mg²⁺ malabsorption). It is frequently unrecognised because routine electrolyte panels do not always include magnesium.

Clinical Consequences

  • Refractory hypoK⁺: The most important practical consequence. The ROMK channel secretes K⁺ into urine when Mg²⁺ is low; K⁺ repletion fails without Mg²⁺ correction — always check Mg²⁺ before aggressive K⁺ replacement
  • Atrial fibrillation: Hypomagnesaemia is an established risk factor for postoperative AF. Prophylactic Mg²⁺ supplementation may reduce postoperative AF in some studies — evidence is mixed and conclusions of individual meta-analyses vary (Arsenault et al., JAMA 2013: reduced AF; more recent reviews: modest/inconsistent benefit)
  • Ventricular arrhythmias: Torsades de Pointes, PVCs, VT — Mg²⁺ is first-line for TdP (even with normal serum level)
  • Coronary spasm: Mg²⁺ depletion can trigger spasm of arterial conduits (especially ITA)
  • Neuromuscular: Tremor, tetany, seizures at very low levels
Magnesium and vasoplegia: Magnesium inhibits catecholamine-induced vasoconstriction at high doses. Some in vitro and observational data suggest vasodilatory effects. However, the clinical evidence for benefit in established vasoplegic syndrome is weak and inconsistent — magnesium should not be relied upon as a primary vasopressor-sparing strategy. Norepinephrine and vasopressin remain first-line.

Sodium Disorders — Overview for Cardiac Surgery ICU

💧 Hyponatraemia (Na <135 mEq/L)

Common causes post-cardiac surgery

  • Excessive hypotonic fluid administration (D5W, 0.45% NaCl)
  • SIADH (stress response, mechanical ventilation, drugs — morphine, amiodarone)
  • Heart failure / fluid overload (dilutional)
  • Adrenal insufficiency (consider post-steroid tapering)

Management principles

  • Identify mechanism: hypovolaemic (replace Na + volume), euvolaemic (fluid restrict, SIADH → tolvaptan or demeclocycline), hypervolaemic (fluid restrict, diuresis)
  • Correction rate: maximum 8–10 mEq/L per 24 h (risk of osmotic demyelination if too rapid)
  • Symptomatic severe hyponatraemia (seizures, coma, Na <120): 3% NaCl 1–2 mL/kg/h; correct by 1–2 mEq/L/h until symptoms resolve, then slow to max 8 mEq/L/24 h
  • In post-cardiac surgery: evaluate for low CO (↑ADH from baroreceptor activation)

💧 Hypernatraemia (Na >145 mEq/L)

Causes in cardiac surgery ICU

  • Excessive loop diuresis without free water replacement
  • Diabetes insipidus (cranial: after intracranial events; nephrogenic: lithium, amphotericin)
  • Insensible losses (fever, tachypnoea, open wounds)
  • Hypertonic saline or sodium bicarbonate administration

Management

  • Calculate free water deficit: FWD (L) = 0.6 × IBW × (Na/140 − 1)
  • Replace with D5W or 0.45% NaCl; correct at max 8–10 mEq/L per 24 h
  • Cranial DI: desmopressin (DDAVP) 1–4 mcg IV q12–24 h
  • Monitor Na every 2–4 h during active correction
Na & cardiac output: In low-output states, aldosterone and ADH are both activated, promoting Na and water retention. Treating hyponatraemia in decompensated HF requires optimising cardiac output first (inotropes, diuretics) — fluid restriction alone is insufficient.
