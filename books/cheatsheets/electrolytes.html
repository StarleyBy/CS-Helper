⚡ Hyperkalemia — causes, ECG, emergency therapy

Etiology (post‑cardiac surgery)

  • High‑potassium cardioplegia (especially in oliguric patients)
  • Low cardiac output / oliguric renal failure
  • Severe tissue ischemia (IABP complication, mesenteric ischemia)
  • Medications: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, potassium‑sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, β‑blockers
  • Hyperglycemia (insulin deficiency shifts K⁺ out of cells)
  • Acidosis (each 0.1 ↓ pH → ↑ K⁺ ~0.4–0.6 mEq/L)

ECG progression (rate‑dependent)

  • Peaked T waves (first sign, often >6.0 mEq/L)
  • PR prolongation, loss of P wave
  • QRS widening → sine wave → ventricular fibrillation/asystole
  • Paced patients: loss of capture, widening of paced QRS
🆘 Hyperkalemic emergency
Indicated by ECG changes OR K⁺ >6.5 mEq/L (or >5.5 with rapid rise/acute renal failure).
Immediate steps: calcium gluconate (membrane stabilization), insulin+glucose (shift into cells), then elimination (loop diuretics / hemodialysis).

⚠️ Hypokalemia — causes, risks, replacement protocol

Etiology after cardiac surgery

  • Loop diuresis (furosemide, bumetanide) – kaliuresis
  • Insulin for hyperglycemia (redistributive hypokalemia)
  • Hypomagnesemia (impairs K⁺ repletion)
  • Metabolic or respiratory alkalosis (“L” in alkalosis = low K⁺)
  • Nasogastric drainage, vomiting

ECG & clinical consequences

  • U waves, flattened T waves, ST depression, QT prolongation
  • Atrial / ventricular ectopy, paroxysmal atrial tachycardia, VT/VF
  • Enhances digoxin toxicity
  • Muscle weakness, ileus

Treatment – IV potassium chloride (KCl)

  • Central line preferred: 10–20 mEq/h (max 40 mEq/h with close monitoring)
  • Peripheral: max concentration 60 mEq/L (e.g., 20 mEq in 250 mL)
  • Never run KCl through a syringe pump without infusion pump safety
  • Each 2 mEq KCl raises serum K⁺ ~0.1 mEq/L (estimates vary)
  • Check magnesium first – correct hypomagnesemia (MgSO₄ 2 g) to enable K⁺ repletion
📌 Oral replacement (after extubation): 10–40 mEq KCl tabs TID–QID. For severe deficits (K⁺ <3.0), may need 60–80 mEq/day.
📌 Diabetic caution: Before treating hyperglycemia with insulin, ensure K⁺ >4.0 mEq/L; otherwise give KCl before insulin to avoid severe hypokalemia.
⚠️ Contraction alkalosis + hypokalemia: Must replete both chloride and potassium. Use NaCl 0.9% + KCl to break alkalosis (enhances renal bicarbonate excretion).
🦴 Hypocalcemia — ionized calcium, hemodynamic effects, correction

Why ionized calcium (iCa) matters

  • Normal iCa = 1.1–1.3 mmol/L (total Ca may be misleading due to hypoalbuminemia)
  • Causes post‑CPB: hemodilution, citrate from blood products, hypomagnesemia, alkalosis
  • ECG: prolonged QT interval, may reduce digoxin sensitivity

When to treat

  • iCa <1.0 mmol/L and hemodynamic instability or myocardial dysfunction
  • Routine “empiric” calcium at termination of CPB is common, but may attenuate catecholamine effects (dobutamine, epinephrine). Does not improve outcomes when used prophylactically.
  • Emergency: refractory hypotension / low CO while investigating cause → calcium chloride 0.5–1 g slow IV (central line) provides transient support.

💡 Hypomagnesemia — rhythm benefits, dosing, synergy with K⁺

Prevalence & causes

  • Present in >50% of patients after CPB (hemodilution, diuresis, PPI use)
  • Loop diuretics, thiazides, PPIs (chronic), poor intake
  • Normal Mg range: 1.5–2.0 mEq/L (0.75–1.0 mmol/L)

Clinical impact

  • Refractory hypokalemia (K⁺ repletion fails without Mg)
  • Increased risk of postoperative atrial fibrillation (AF)
  • Ventricular arrhythmias (Torsade de pointes, PVCs)
  • Coronary spasm, low cardiac output syndrome
  • Prolonged ICU stay, higher mortality

Treatment protocol

  • Prophylaxis (common practice): MgSO₄ 2 g IV over 1 hour at the end of CPB and on POD#1 morning → reduces AF and ventricular arrhythmias.
  • Therapeutic: MgSO₄ 2–4 g IV (diluted in 50–100 mL NS) over 15–30 min, followed by infusion 1–2 g/h if severe deficit.
  • Torsade de pointes: MgSO₄ 2 g IV push (over 1–2 min) as first‑line therapy (even with normal Mg level).
📌 Magnesium inhibits epinephrine‑induced vasoconstriction without blunting its inotropic effect → ideal in post‑bypass vasoplegia + need for inotrope.
⚠️ Always check Mg before aggressive K⁺ repletion. If Mg <1.5 mg/dL, give MgSO₄ first; otherwise K⁺ will not stay inside cells.
📈 ECG patterns — hyperkalemia, hypokalemia, hypocalcemia

Hyperkalemia (progressive)

  • Early: Tall, peaked T waves (symmetrical, narrow base)
  • Moderate: PR prolongation, P wave flattening/absence
  • Severe: Wide QRS, sine wave, ventricular fibrillation, asystole
  • Paced rhythm: increased capture threshold, loss of capture

Hypokalemia

  • U wave (prominent after T wave, best seen in V2–V4)
  • ST depression, flattened T wave, prolonged QT interval
  • Ectopy (PACs, PVCs), torsade risk if QT very long

Hypocalcemia

  • Prolonged QT interval (due to prolonged ST segment)
  • Normal T wave morphology
  • May predispose to ventricular arrhythmias but rarely torsade.
💡 Differentiation: Hypocalcemia → QT prolongation with normal T wave; hypokalemia → QU prolongation with U wave.
⚡ In hyperkalemic emergency, do not wait for ECG confirmation if clinical suspicion high — give calcium immediately.
📌 Clinical pearls & electrolyte synergy
  • Hypomagnesemia → hypokalemia → refractory arrhythmias. Always check Mg before or together with K⁺.
  • Alkalosis lowers K⁺ and ionized calcium. Correct chloride depletion (NaCl, KCl) to break metabolic alkalosis.
  • Calcium and digoxin: Give calcium slowly over 20–30 min in digoxin‑toxic patients; use Digibind for life‑threatening arrhythmias.
  • Renal dosing: In oliguric patients, reduce potassium infusion rate (max 5–10 mEq/h) and recheck frequently.
  • Post‑cardiotomy vasoplegia: Norepinephrine + vasopressin preferred; magnesium infusion may improve vascular tone.
⚕️ Sources: Bojar 6th Edition, Chapter 12 (Fluid Management, Renal, Metabolic and Endocrine Problems), ACC/ESC 2025 electrolyte management consensus, KDIGO 2025 guidelines. Always individualize therapy based on continuous monitoring, rhythm strips, and serial electrolytes.
