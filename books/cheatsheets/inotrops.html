💉 Epinephrine (Adrenaline)

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Epinephrine is an endogenous catecholamine that acts on all adrenergic receptors (α₁, α₂, β₁, β₂) with potency that is dose-dependent. It increases myocardial cAMP via adenylyl cyclase activation, enhancing calcium influx through L-type channels, increasing the rate and force of myocardial contraction (positive inotropy and chronotropy). Simultaneously, it accelerates Ca²⁺ reuptake into the SR (positive lusitropy).

Receptors: β₁ ●●●● β₂ ●●● α₁ ●●● α₂ ●●

✅ Indications

  • First-line inotrope for borderline CO without tachycardia/ectopy post-CPB
  • Hypertrophied hearts, slow sinus node (better HR response than dobutamine at equiinotropic doses)
  • Cardiac arrest — ventricular fibrillation, PEA, asystole (ACLS)
  • Anaphylaxis (IM or IV)
  • Bronchospasm with hemodynamic compromise
  • Bridge to IABP or ECMO in refractory cardiogenic shock

❌ Contraindications / Cautions

  • Significant tachycardia (>100–110 bpm) — further ↑HR worsens diastolic filling
  • Active myocardial ischemia — ↑MVO₂ can extend infarct
  • Hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy (HOCM) — ↑obstruction
  • Ventricular arrhythmias — lowers threshold for VT/VF
  • Halothane anesthesia (sensitises myocardium — not used in CS)

⭐ Advantages vs Other Inotropes

  • Unique hemodynamic profile: at low doses, combines inotropy + mild vasodilation → improves CO without ↑afterload
  • Chronotropic advantage: less tachycardia than dobutamine at equipotent inotropic dose (≈2 μg/min epi ≈ 10 μg/kg/min dobutamine)
  • Versatility: can be titrated from inodilator to vasopressor in one drug
  • Short t½: immediate response to dose changes, easy to wean
  • Bronchodilation (β₂): useful in bronchospasm complicating post-op course
⚠️ Metabolic note: Epinephrine causes dose-dependent lactic acidosis via β₂-mediated hepatic glycogenolysis and inhibition of pyruvate dehydrogenase — this is not an indicator of tissue hypoperfusion. Distinguish from true ischemic lactate by clinical context and mixed venous O₂ saturation.
💉 Dobutamine

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Dobutamine is a synthetic catecholamine existing as a racemic mixture. The (+) isomer is a potent β₁ and β₂ agonist; the (−) isomer is a mild α₁ agonist. Net result: dominant β₁ inotropy with mild β₂-mediated vasodilation and negligible net α effect. Increases cAMP → PKA-mediated phosphorylation of cardiac proteins → ↑Ca²⁺ transient → ↑contractility. Unlike catecholamines, does NOT stimulate dopamine receptors.

  • Uncorrected hypovolemia — vasodilator effect drops MAP
  • Atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response — can ↑rate further
  • Hypersensitivity to sulfite preservatives (some formulations)
💡 Tachyphylaxis: After 72–96h continuous infusion, β-receptor downregulation reduces efficacy. Consider switching to or adding milrinone (cAMP-independent inotropy path) if prolonged support needed. Dobutamine does not require renal dose adjustment, unlike milrinone.
💉 Milrinone (Primacor) — PDE-III inhibitor

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Milrinone selectively inhibits phosphodiesterase type III (PDE-III) in both myocardium and vascular smooth muscle. By preventing cAMP degradation, it amplifies the downstream effects of cAMP: in the heart — ↑Ca²⁺ cycling → positive inotropy + lusitropy (enhanced relaxation); in vasculature — ↑PKA → smooth muscle relaxation → ↓SVR and ↓PVR. Mechanism is post-receptor — bypasses downregulated β-receptors, hence additive with catecholamines even in receptor-saturated states.

Maintenance: 0.25–0.5 μg/kg/min

✅ Indications

  • Persistent low CO despite adequate catecholamine support
  • RV failure with elevated PVR — post-mitral valve surgery, orthotopic heart transplant, pulmonary hypertension
  • Diastolic dysfunction — improves LV compliance (lusitropy)
  • Coronary artery spasm / conduit spasm (ITA, radial) — smooth muscle relaxation
  • Inhaled milrinone: selective pulmonary vasodilation without systemic hypotension
  • Advanced decompensated HF — β-receptor downregulated patients

❌ Contraindications / Cautions

  • Severe hypotension without adequate vasopressor support
  • Significant renal failure — halve dose, avoid loading bolus
  • Outflow tract obstruction (HOCM)
  • Atrial fibrillation with rapid response — may ↑ventricular rate via ↑AV conduction
  • Pre-existing ventricular arrhythmias — can be pro-arrhythmic
⚠️ Mortality signal: OPTIME-CHF trial (2002) showed milrinone increased arrhythmias and hypotension without mortality benefit in decompensated HF. However, in post-cardiotomy low-output state with high PVR, milrinone remains the preferred inodilator. Inhaled milrinone (5 mg nebulized) provides selective pulmonary vasodilation with minimal systemic effects — excellent for RV failure post-transplant or post-mitral surgery.
💉 Dopamine

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Dopamine is the immediate precursor of norepinephrine. It acts on dopaminergic (DA₁, DA₂), β₁, β₂, and α₁ receptors with markedly different receptor affinity depending on dose. DA₁ receptors in renal, mesenteric, and coronary vasculature mediate vasodilation via cAMP; β₁ receptors mediate inotropy at intermediate doses; α₁ receptors cause vasoconstriction at high doses. Dopamine also releases norepinephrine from sympathetic nerve terminals (indirect effect).

"Renal dose" 1–3 μg/kg/min
DA₁-mediated renal and mesenteric vasodilation → ↑urine output. Does NOT confer renal protection in clinical trials (ANZICS 2000, numerous meta-analyses). Concept now discredited for "kidney protection." Use if urine output drops, but don't rely on it for AKI prevention.

Predominantly α₁ → ↑↑SVR. Visceral ischemia risk (splanchnic vasoconstriction). Arrhythmias common. In this range, norepinephrine is preferred — more predictable, less arrhythmogenic, better hemodynamic profile.

✅ Limited Indications (post-CS)

  • Low CO + low SVR when epinephrine/norepinephrine combination not available
  • Oliguria post-CPB — augment urine output (low dose, not as AKI prophylaxis)
  • Bradycardia with hemodynamic compromise (if pacing unavailable)

❌ Relative Contraindications / Disadvantages

  • Highest arrhythmia risk among catecholamines — avoid in AF, VT history
  • Pheochromocytoma — paradoxical hypertension
  • Significant tachycardia — chronotropic effect poorly controlled
  • MAO inhibitor therapy — markedly potentiated effect
⚠️ Clinical position 2025: Dopamine has largely been replaced by epinephrine + norepinephrine in cardiac surgery. The SOAP-II trial showed higher mortality and arrhythmia rate vs norepinephrine in shock. ESC/ACC guidelines now prefer norepinephrine as first-line vasopressor. Dopamine may still be used in specific bradycardic low-output states or when alternatives are unavailable.
💉 Isoproterenol (Isoprenaline)

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Isoproterenol is a synthetic catecholamine with pure β₁ and β₂ agonism — no α-adrenergic activity whatsoever. β₁ stimulation produces marked ↑HR and ↑contractility; β₂ stimulation causes systemic and pulmonary vasodilation (↓SVR, ↓↓PVR) and bronchodilation. The dominant effect is profound tachycardia, often the dose-limiting factor. It also ↑conduction velocity through the AV node (useful in complete heart block).

  • Bronchospasm with need for inotropy
  • Torsades de Pointes (↑HR → shortens QT)

❌ Contraindications

  • Tachycardia or tachyarrhythmias — absolute contraindication
  • Active myocardial ischemia — extreme ↑MVO₂
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy — worsens obstruction and ischemia
  • Digitalis toxicity — arrhythmia sensitization
  • Aortic stenosis — fixed obstruction limits CO response
💉 Norepinephrine (Levophed) — First-line vasopressor

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Norepinephrine is the primary endogenous vasopressor catecholamine, released from sympathetic nerve terminals. It has potent α₁ and moderate β₁ activity with minimal β₂. The dominant effect is vasoconstriction (↑SVR) via α₁ in arterioles, raising MAP. β₁ stimulation provides mild inotropy and chronotropy — usually offset by baroreflex-mediated bradycardia (↑MAP → ↑vagal tone). Reflex bradycardia can actually be beneficial by improving diastolic filling.

Moderate: 0.05–0.15 μg/kg/min

Severe vasoconstriction — visceral ischemia, digital necrosis, metabolic acidosis. Indicates catecholamine-refractory vasoplegic shock. Add vasopressin, consider methylene blue, evaluate for underlying cause.

✅ Indications (first-line vasopressor)

  • Vasoplegic syndrome post-CPB — low SVR + adequate CO (warm, pink patient)
  • Low CO + low SVR — combined with inotrope (epi/dobutamine)
  • Septic shock — preferred over dopamine (SOAP-II trial: ↓arrhythmia, ↓mortality trend)
  • Rebound hypotension after milrinone loading
  • Anaphylaxis (adjunct to epinephrine)

❌ Contraindications / Cautions

  • Uncorrected hypovolemia — vasoconstriction without volume = ↓CO
  • Arterial graft spasm risk (ITA, radial artery) — α-agonists can precipitate conduit spasm; maintain diltiazem/NTG
  • High PVR — NE ↑PVR; avoid if RV failure dominant
  • Peripheral vascular disease — distal ischemia
💡 Arterial graft consideration: Norepinephrine causes spasm of ITA and radial artery conduits via α₁ receptors in the graft wall. When NE is required, maintain concurrent intracoronary or systemic diltiazem (0.1 mg/kg/h) or nitroglycerin (0.5–1 μg/kg/min) to protect graft patency.
💉 Phenylephrine (Neo‑Synephrine) — Pure α₁ agonist

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Phenylephrine is a pure selective α₁ agonist with essentially no β activity. It causes dose-dependent peripheral vasoconstriction (arterial > venous) → ↑SVR → ↑MAP → reflex bradycardia (baroreceptor-mediated ↑vagal tone). Has no direct inotropic, chronotropic, or lusitropy effect. Because CO = HR × SV, the reflex bradycardia combined with fixed SV may actually ↓CO — this is the key limitation. Venous constriction also ↑preload (↑CVP).

  • Arterial graft conduits — highest spasm risk among vasopressors
  • Severe bradycardia — reflex ↓HR may be dangerous
  • Aortic insufficiency — ↑SVR worsens regurgitation
💉 Vasopressin (ADH / AVP) — Non-catecholamine vasopressor

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Vasopressin (antidiuretic hormone) acts on V₁ receptors in vascular smooth muscle → Gq protein → IP₃ pathway → ↑intracellular Ca²⁺ → vasoconstriction. This pathway is entirely independent of adrenergic receptors — hence effective in catecholamine-refractory shock (downregulated α-receptors). V₁ stimulation also causes mesenteric, renal, and coronary vasoconstriction. V₂ receptors in renal collecting duct → water reabsorption (antidiuretic effect). At therapeutic doses, vasopressin does NOT increase PVR (selective for systemic vasculature) — a major advantage over NE/phenylephrine in patients with RV failure.

Physiologic replacement: 0.01–0.04 U/min

Moderate: 0.04–0.1 U/min

High: >0.1 U/min

✅ Indications

  • Vasoplegic syndrome refractory to catecholamines — post-CPB, protamine reaction, post-VAD
  • NE dose reduction strategy — add vasopressin to ↓NE requirement (catecholamine-sparing)
  • RV failure + high PVR — preferred over NE/phenyephrine (doesn't ↑PVR)
  • Diabetes insipidus post-operative (V₂-mediated ADH replacement)

❌ Contraindications / Cautions

  • Low CO state without adequate inotrope — pure vasoconstriction ↑afterload, ↓CO
  • Mesenteric ischemia risk — monitor bowel sounds, lactate, pH
  • CAD — coronary vasoconstriction at high doses
  • Hyponatraemia (relative) — V₂-mediated water retention
🧬 Pharmacological rationale: In prolonged vasodilatory shock, endogenous AVP stores are depleted (half-life of depletion ~1–2h). Exogenous vasopressin at 0.03 U/min restores plasma levels to normal — this is replacement therapy, not pharmacologic vasoconstriction. The VASST trial showed non-inferior outcomes vs NE in vasoplegic shock, with lower NE requirements and reduced progression to severe shock.
⚡ Levosimendan (Simdax) — Calcium sensitizer + inodilator

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Levosimendan has a dual mechanism unique among inotropes. (1) Calcium sensitization: binds troponin C in a Ca²⁺-dependent manner → stabilizes troponin C-Ca²⁺ complex during systole → ↑contractile force without increasing intracellular Ca²⁺ concentration → no ↑MVO₂. During diastole, the compound dissociates as Ca²⁺ falls — normal relaxation preserved. (2) KATP channel opening in vascular smooth muscle and mitochondria → vasodilation (↓SVR, ↓↓PVR) + cardioprotection via ischemic preconditioning-like mechanism. Active metabolite OR-1896 (t½ ~75–80h) perpetuates effects for up to 7–9 days after a single 24h infusion.

Mechanism: TnC sensitizer KATP opener PDE-III (mild)

📈 Dosing Protocol

Loading bolus: 12–24 μg/kg over 10 min
Produces rapid ↑CO and ↓PCWP. Often causes transient hypotension (vasodilation) — requires α-agonist co-administration. Consider omitting in systolic BP <90 mmHg or in STEMI context.
Maintenance: 0.05–0.2 μg/kg/min × 24h
Sustained inodilator effect. ↑CI 25–40%, ↓SVR 20–30%, ↓PVR 30–40%. Notably: no ↑MVO₂ despite inotropy. Most patients require α-agonist (NE 0.05–0.1 μg/kg/min) throughout infusion.
Post-infusion (OR-1896 phase): 24h–9 days
Hemodynamic benefit persists. Plasma NE levels fall (↓catecholamine dependence). Gradual HR normalization. Monitor for delayed hypotension if vasopressors weaned too quickly.

✅ Indications

  • Pre-operative optimization of high-risk cardiac surgery (preconditioning)
  • Post-cardiotomy RV or LV failure — ↓PVR + ↑RV contractility
  • Weaning from CPB in severely impaired LV (EF <30%)
  • Decompensated HF in β-receptor downregulated patients (LIDO, RUSSLAN trials)
  • Ischemic cardiomyopathy — possible cardioprotective effect via mitochondrial KATP
  • Heart transplant — post-reperfusion primary graft dysfunction

❌ Contraindications / Cautions

  • Severe hypotension (SBP <85 mmHg) without vasopressor support
  • Significant hypovolemia — vasodilation ↓↓MAP
  • Severe renal failure (CrCl <15 mL/min) — OR-1896 accumulation
  • Severe hepatic failure — impaired parent drug clearance
  • Tachycardia — ↑HR common; use with caution in AF with fast ventricular rate
  • Hypokalemia — ↑arrhythmia risk via KATP opening; correct K⁺ before/during infusion
⚡ Key advantage: Levosimendan is the only inotrope that improves contractility WITHOUT increasing MVO₂ (calcium sensitization ≠ ↑Ca²⁺ cycling). This makes it uniquely suitable for ischemic hearts where catecholamine-induced Ca²⁺ overload could extend injury. The SURVIVE trial (vs dobutamine in AHF) did not show mortality benefit, but the post-CS RV failure literature and CABG pre-conditioning data are more favorable.
⚡ Calcium Chloride (CaCl₂)

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Extracellular Ca²⁺ is the trigger for intracellular Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release (CICR) from the SR. IV calcium bolus transiently ↑plasma ionized Ca²⁺ → ↑Ca²⁺ influx via L-type channels → ↑SR release → ↑contractility. Also ↑SVR via vascular smooth muscle contraction. However: in the post-ischemic/reperfused heart, intracellular Ca²⁺ is already elevated — additional Ca²⁺ may worsen Ca²⁺ overload and reperfusion injury. Calcium also attenuates catecholamine effects by ↑membrane potential and inhibiting adenylyl cyclase (high concentrations).

⚠️ Clinical Evidence — Controversial Use

Routine use post-CPB is NOT recommended.
Multiple studies (DiNardo, Royster, Hensley) show: (1) Transient contractility ↑ but equivalent to catecholamines; (2) May worsen reperfusion injury in stunned myocardium; (3) No mortality benefit. Many centers have abandoned routine CaCl₂ at separation from CPB.
CaCl₂ vs Calcium Gluconate:
CaCl₂ 1g = 272 mg elemental Ca²⁺. Gluconate 1g = 90 mg elemental Ca²⁺. CaCl₂ preferred for immediate effect — does not require hepatic metabolism for ionized Ca²⁺ release. Use CaCl₂ in emergencies; gluconate acceptable for prophylaxis/maintenance.

✅ Evidence-Based Indications

1. Hypocalcemia
Ionized Ca²⁺ <1.1 mmol/L (common post-CPB due to citrate from blood products, hemodilution, hypothermia). True indication: replace to normal before calling for inotropes.
2. Hyperkalemia
K⁺ >6.0–6.5 mEq/L — calcium stabilizes myocardial membrane (cardiac membrane protection), not a treatment for hyperkalemia per se. Acts within 1–2 min. Duration ~30 min. Must treat underlying cause.
3. Calcium channel blocker toxicity
IV CaCl₂ reverses negative inotropy/chronotropy from CCB overdose or iatrogenic hypotension (verapamil, diltiazem bolus). High doses may be needed (repeated 1g boluses).
⚠️ ACLS 2025: Routine calcium in cardiac arrest is NOT recommended (Class III: harm) unless in the context of hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia, or CCB toxicity. In cardiac surgery, avoid routine bolus at separation from CPB — measure ionized Ca²⁺ first and replace only if <1.1 mmol/L.
⚡ Triiodothyronine (T₃) — Salvage inotrope

🔬 Mechanism of Action

T₃ (liothyronine) is the active form of thyroid hormone. It acts on nuclear thyroid hormone receptors to ↑transcription of: α-myosin heavy chain (↑contractile protein), SERCA2a (↑Ca²⁺ reuptake → improved contractility and lusitropy), β₁-adrenergic receptors (↑catecholamine sensitivity), and Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. Genomic effects take hours; non-genomic effects (↑Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger, ↓SVR via vascular K⁺ channels) occur within minutes. Mechanism is entirely β-receptor independent — works even in fully downregulated states.

Post-CPB euthyroid sick syndrome: Cardiac surgery reliably causes T₃ levels to fall 50–70% within hours of CPB (even without pre-existing thyroid disease) — "non-thyroidal illness syndrome." Low T₃ correlates with low CO syndrome and poor prognosis.

✅ Indications

  • Salvage: IABP + maximal catecholamines + milrinone insufficient (last-resort before MCS)
  • Refractory low CO with suspected post-CPB T₃ deficiency
  • Brain-dead donor heart preparation — maintains T₃ levels in donor
  • Neonatal/pediatric cardiac surgery — higher T₃ sensitivity
Dose: 0.8 μg/kg IV bolus (max 20 μg) → infusion 0.113 μg/kg/h × 6h. Alternative: 10–20 μg IV bolus. Evidence base: mainly observational; TRICC-HF trial ongoing.

⚠️ Limitations & Evidence

  • Randomized data limited — Klemperer 1995, Bennett-Guerrero 1996 show modest benefit
  • Tachycardia and arrhythmia risk at higher doses
  • Drug availability — limited in many countries (access, cost)
  • Not a substitute for mechanical support when truly indicated
Considered experimental/salvage — use only when conventional inotropes, IABP, and levosimendan have failed or are contraindicated. Some centres use prophylactically in high-risk reoperations or transplant.

2

CI <2.2 — Low output state

First-line: Epinephrine 1–4 μg/min (0.01–0.05 μg/kg/min) — best balance of inotropy vs tachycardia
Alternative: Dobutamine 5–10 μg/kg/min if SVR elevated
If tachycardia limiting: Prefer milrinone over dobutamine

3

CI <2.2 despite catecholamines

Add Milrinone: 25–50 μg/kg bolus → 0.25–0.5 μg/kg/min (synergistic with β-agonists; avoid in hypotension without NE cover)
Consider Levosimendan if ischemic etiology or β-receptor downregulation suspected: 12 μg/kg load → 0.1 μg/kg/min × 24h
Add norepinephrine to counteract vasodilation from PDE-III/levosimendan

4

Refractory low output (CI <1.8 despite above)

IABP — ↑diastolic perfusion, ↓afterload; most immediate MCS option
T₃ salvage: 0.8 μg/kg IV if post-CPB euthyroid sick syndrome suspected
Impella CP / VA-ECMO — if no improvement with IABP in 1–2h
Consider re-exploration to exclude surgical cause, tamponade, or tension pneumothorax

🎯 Special Situations

RV failure + ↑PVR
Milrinone (± inhaled) + Isoproterenol or Levosimendan + Inhaled NO/prostacyclin. Avoid NE and phenylephrine (↑PVR). Target: PVR <240 dyn·s/cm⁵, RV-PA coupling ratio >0.7.
Vasoplegic syndrome (warm, high CO, low BP)
NE first-line + Vasopressin 0.03 U/min. If refractory: Methylene blue 1.5 mg/kg IV over 1h. Angiotensin II (investigational). Exclude protamine reaction, anaphylaxis, adrenal insufficiency.
ITA / radial artery graft spasm risk
Minimise α-agonists. Maintain diltiazem 0.1 mg/kg/h IV. NTG 0.5–1 μg/kg/min. If vasopressor needed: NE < phenylephrine for graft spasm risk (NE has β₁ co-activation that ↑graft flow).
Tachycardia + low output
Prefer milrinone ± low-dose epinephrine (<2 μg/min) over dobutamine or dopamine. Rate control (β-blockade only if tolerated). If AF: amiodarone or cardioversion before increasing inotropes.
Post-transplant denervated heart
Isoproterenol first-line (target HR 90–110 bpm — CO is HR-dependent). Milrinone for PVR. Avoid drugs requiring cardiac nerves (digoxin, atropine ineffective on rate). Early CRT if bradycardia persists.
Weaning order
① Wean α-agonists first (as patient warms, SVR normalises) → ② ↓catecholamines (epi to <2 μg/min, dobu to <5 μg/kg/min) → ③ Wean milrinone last (long t½ — halve every 4h; watch for rebound ↑SVR). IABP removal: CI ≥2.2 + single low-dose inotrope.

📋 Daily SICU Monitoring Checklist

  • CI / SvO₂ / ScvO₂ — trend every 4h or continuous PA cath
  • MAP — target 65–85 mmHg (coronary: ≥70 in CAD)
  • HR — target 70–90 bpm; >100 = reassess drug choice
  • Urine output — target ≥0.5 mL/kg/h
  • Lactate — normal <2 mmol/L; rising lactate = ↓CO or ↑vasoconstriction
  • Ionized Ca²⁺ — replace if <1.1 mmol/L
  • K⁺ — replace to ≥3.5 mEq/L
  • Temperature — cool patients have ↑SVR; warm to normothermia
  • Hemoglobin — maintain ≥7 g/dL (higher in ischemic heart disease)
  • Assess volume status before increasing inotrope dose

⬇️ Weaning Strategy

Order of weaning (Bojar 6th ed. protocol):
1. As patient warms → SVR normalises → ↓phenylephrine / vasopressin first
2. Reduce NE when MAP stable >70 without rising lactate
3. Taper epinephrine to ≤1–2 μg/min; dobutamine to ≤5 μg/kg/min
4. Wean milrinone last (t½ 2h) — halve dose every 4–6h; observe for rebound ↑SVR (may require brief NE bump)
5. IABP removal: CI ≥2.2, on single low-dose inotrope (epi ≤1 μg/min or dobu ≤10 μg/kg/min), MAP stable ≥70
6. Levosimendan: no active weaning needed (OR-1896 effect self-resolves over 7–9 days)
