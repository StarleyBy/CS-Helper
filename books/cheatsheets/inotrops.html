📌 Individual drug profiles (click to expand)

💉 Epinephrine (Adrenaline)

Hemodynamic effects

  • Potent β₁ inotrope, increases CO by ↑HR + contractility. Low dose (<2 μg/min) → β₂ vasodilation; higher dose → α vasoconstriction, SVR ↑.
  • Strong β₂ bronchodilation; may cause less tachycardia than dobutamine at equipotent doses (2 μg/min).

Indications

  • First‑line for borderline CO without tachycardia/ectopy; excellent for hypertrophied heart, slow sinus node.
  • Bronchospasm, anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest.

Starting dose

1 μg/min (≈0.01 μg/kg/min) via 1 mg/250 mL; up to 4 μg/min (0.05 μg/kg/min). Higher doses rarely needed post‑op.

Note: Metabolic acidosis may occur even without α effect; not related to hypoperfusion.
💉 Dobutamine

Hemodynamics

Strong β₁ inotrope + mild β₂ vasodilation → ↓SVR, ↓PCWP, maintains MAP via improved CO. Tachycardia dose‑dependent, but less arrhythmogenic than dopamine. Synergistic with milrinone.

Indications

Marginal CO + mild ↑SVR; moderate pulmonary vasodilator (RV afterload reduction). Often combined with PDE inhibitors in advanced HF/transplant candidates.

Dosage

Start 5 μg/kg/min (500 mg/250 mL); up to 20 μg/kg/min.

💉 Milrinone (Primacor)

Inodilator (PDE III inhibitor)

  • Reduces SVR/PVR, modest ↑HR and inotropy, lowers filling pressures. Lusitropic effect improves compliance.
  • Long half‑life (1.5–2 h); additive with catecholamines. Often requires α‑agonist (norepinephrine/vasopressin) to maintain BP.
  • Inhaled milrinone → selective pulmonary vasodilation without systemic effects.

Indications

Persistent low CO despite catecholamines; RV dysfunction + elevated PVR (mitral disease, post‑transplant); diastolic dysfunction / conduit spasm.

Dosing

50 μg/kg IV bolus over 10 min, then 0.25–0.75 μg/kg/min (20 mg/200 mL).

⚠️ Meta‑analyses show mixed mortality signals, but beneficial in low CO state when added after inadequate response to catecholamines.
💉 Dopamine

Hemodynamic effects (dose‑dependent)

  • “Renal dose” 2–3 μg/kg/min ↑ urine output but no kidney protection; 3–8 μg/kg/min β₁ inotropy; >8 μg/kg/min α‑vasoconstriction.
  • Higher tachycardia / arrhythmia risk; may worsen V/Q mismatch.

Indications

Limited by side effects – other inotropes preferred. May be used when SVR low and BP marginal, but epinephrine/dobutamine are better.

Not recommended for routine renal protection; no outcome benefit.
💉 Isoproterenol

Effects

Pure β₁+β₂ agonist → marked ↑HR, moderate inotropy, ↓SVR/PVR. High O₂ demand, arrhythmogenic.

Indications

RV failure + high PVR (post‑mitral surgery, post‑heart transplant to maintain HR ~100 bpm); bronchospasm + inotrope need; bradycardia without pacing wires.

Dose

Start 0.5 μg/min (1 mg/250 mL); up to 10 μg/min (0.0075–0.1 μg/kg/min).

💉 Norepinephrine (Levophed)

Effects

α + β₁ effects → ↑SVR, ↑contractility, ↑HR. Increases myocardial O₂ demand but improves perfusion pressure. Often used when low CO + low SVR (vasodilated state).

Indications

  • Low CO + low SVR/hypotension (warming phase, vasoplegia).
  • If CI <2.0 L/min/m², add separate inotrope (epinephrine/dobutamine).

Starting dose

1 μg/min (0.01 μg/kg/min) of 4 mg/250 mL; titrate to MAP >70 mmHg. Avoid high doses (>20 μg/min) – visceral ischemia risk.

⚠️ Monitor for reduced flow in arterial grafts (ITA/radial); combine with vasodilator if possible.
💉 Phenylephrine (Neo‑Synephrine)

Pure α‑agonist

Increases SVR, reflex bradycardia; no direct inotropic effect. Use only when CO satisfactory but hypotension persists (vasoplegia).

Indications

  • Hypotension with CI >2.2 L/min/m² (post‑CPB warming, HOCM, SAM after mitral repair).
  • Preoperative ischemia with NTG; maintain MAP while reducing preload.

Dose

5 μg/min (40 mg/250 mL); range 0.05–1.5 μg/kg/min. May become refractory after several hours → switch to norepinephrine.

⚠️ Caution: may provoke arterial graft spasm (ITA/radial). Prefer to reduce vasodilator than increase α‑agonist.
💉 Vasopressin (ADH)

Effects

V₁ receptor → ↑SVR, no direct cardiac effect. Improves MAP without raising PA pressures; beneficial in vasoplegic shock refractory to catecholamines.

Indications

  • Low BP poorly responsive to norepinephrine/phenylephrine.
  • Vasodilatory shock (vasoplegia) after CPB with preserved CO.

Dosage

0.01–0.1 units/min (mix: 40 units/80 mL). May cause mesenteric ischemia if CO marginal.

⚡ Calcium chloride (CaCl₂)

Effects

↑SVR, transient ↑contractility (only if hypocalcemia). May attenuate catecholamine effects. Routine use post‑CPB controversial – may worsen reperfusion injury.

Indications

  • Emergency support during weaning from CPB (if hypocalcemia present).
  • Hyperkalemia (>6.0 mEq/L).

Dose

0.5–1 g slow IV bolus. Avoid routine use during cardiac arrest (ACLS 2025).

⚡ Triiodothyronine (T₃) & Levosimendan

Triiodothyronine (T₃)

  • Increases contractility independent of β‑receptors; low T₃ post‑CPB linked to low CO syndrome.
  • Salvage therapy when IABP and maximal inotropes fail; improves donor heart function.
  • Dose: 10–20 μg IV bolus (or 0.8 μg/kg + infusion).

Levosimendan

  • Calcium sensitizer + KATP channel opener: inodilator without ↑MvO₂. Long half‑life (~70 h).
  • Use: anticipated post‑cardiotomy RV/LV dysfunction, weaning from CPB.
  • Dose: 12–24 μg/kg load over 10 min, then 0.1 μg/kg/min. May require α‑agonist for vasodilation.
  • Role remains undefined (mixed outcome data).
📐 Algorithm: Choosing the right vasoactive drug

1️⃣ Preload & rhythm first

Optimize volume (CVP 8–12, PCWP 12–16), correct brady/tachy, consider pacing.

2️⃣ CI >2.2 L/min/m² but MAP <70 mmHg

→ α‑agent: start norepinephrine (preferred for β reserve) or phenylephrine. If refractory → add vasopressin.

3️⃣ CI <2.0 L/min/m² (low output)

→ First‑line: epinephrine (1‑4 μg/min) or dobutamine (5‑10 μg/kg/min). Avoid tachycardia.

If still suboptimal → add milrinone (bolus+infusion). Synergistic inotropic effect, often requires norepinephrine to maintain SVR.

4️⃣ Refractory low output / persistent shock

→ IABP insertion, consider levosimendan or T₃ as rescue. If still no improvement → mechanical circulatory support (ECMO/VAD).

📌 Special situations
  • RV failure + high PVR: milrinone, inhaled milrinone, isoproterenol, levosimendan.
  • Vasoplegic shock (post‑pump, warm, high CO): norepinephrine + vasopressin (0.03‑0.04 U/min).
  • Arterial grafts (ITA/radial): minimise α‑agonists, maintain diltiazem/nitroglycerin to prevent spasm.
  • Tachycardia/arrhythmia: prefer milrinone or low‑dose epinephrine over dobutamine/dopamine.
🚨 Weaning order: first α‑agonists (as patient warms) → then catecholamines (epinephrine/dobutamine to low doses) → wean milrinone last due to long half‑life. IABP can be removed when on low‑dose single inotrope (epi ≤1 μg/min or dobutamine <10 μg/kg/min).
⚠️ Adverse effects & weaning strategy

Common adverse effects

  • Arrhythmias / tachycardia (epinephrine, dobutamine, isoproterenol)
  • Vasoconstriction / organ hypoperfusion (norepinephrine, vasopressin, high‑dose phenylephrine)
  • Hypotension requiring α‑agonists (milrinone, levosimendan)
  • Cyanide toxicity (nitroprusside), methemoglobinemia (IV NTG)

Weaning protocol

  • Wean α‑agonists first when MAP stable & patient awake.
  • Reduce epinephrine to <2 μg/min, dobutamine to <10 μg/kg/min.
  • Milrinone: halve dose every 4‑6h; if stable, discontinue. Observe for delayed haemodynamic deterioration (long half‑life).
  • IABP removal: acceptable when CI ≥2.2, on low inotropic support (epi ≤1 μg/min or dobutamine <10 μg/kg/min).
✔️ Daily checklist: check CI, MAP, SvO₂, lactate, urine output. Reassess volume status before increasing inotrope.
