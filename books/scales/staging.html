Aortic Stenosis (AS) – 2020 AHA/ACC Stages

📊 Stage A → At risk

  • Bicuspid aortic valve, aortic sclerosis, or other congenital anomaly
  • No hemodynamic obstruction (peak velocity <2 m/s, mean gradient <10 mmHg)
  • Management: Risk factor modification, echo every 3‑5 years

📈 Stage B → Progressive AS

  • Mild‑moderate leaflet calcification, Vmax 2‑3.9 m/s, mean ΔP <40 mmHg, AVA >1.5 cm²
  • Asymptomatic, LVEF normal
  • Management: Echo every 1‑2 years, avoid dehydration

⚠️ Stage C → Severe AS, asymptomatic

  • C1: Vmax ≥4 m/s or mean ΔP ≥40 mmHg, AVA ≤1.0 cm², LVEF ≥50%
  • C2: Same hemodynamics + LVEF <50% or abnormal exercise test
  • AVR indicated for C2; C1 → consider AVR if very severe (Vmax ≥5 m/s) or rapid progression

🚨 Stage D → Symptomatic severe AS

  • D1 (high gradient): Symptoms + Vmax ≥4 m/s, AVA ≤1.0 cm²
  • D2 (low flow/low gradient): LVEF <50%, dobutamine stress echo
  • D3 (paradoxical low flow): LVEF ≥50%, indexed AVA ≤0.6 cm²/m²
  • AVR (SAVR or TAVI) Class I recommendation
💡 Key thresholds: Vmax ≥4 m/s = severe; mean gradient ≥40 mmHg; AVA ≤1.0 cm². Low‑flow states require dobutamine stress or CT calcium scoring.

Aortic Regurgitation (AR) – 2020 AHA/ACC Stages

Stage A: At risk

  • Bicuspid valve, aortic root dilation, hypertension
  • No or trivial AR, normal LV size/function
  • Monitor every 3‑5 years

Stage B: Mild‑moderate AR

  • Jet width 25‑64% of LVOT, vena contracta 0.3‑0.6 cm, RVol 30‑59 mL/beat
  • LV normal size, LVEF normal, asymptomatic
  • Annual echo, treat hypertension (target SBP <130 mmHg)

Stage C: Severe AR, asymptomatic

  • C1: LVEF ≥50%, LVEDD <60 mm (or <35 mm/m²)
  • C2: LVEF <50% OR LVEDD ≥60 mm (or ≥35 mm/m²)
  • Surgery indicated for C2; C1 if progressive dilation or decline in LVEF

Stage D: Symptomatic severe AR

  • NYHA II‑IV, angina, or syncope
  • Any severe AR parameters (vena contracta ≥0.6 cm, regurgitant fraction ≥50%)
  • Aortic valve replacement (Class I)
📌 AR severity: vena contracta ≥0.6 cm, pressure half‑time <200 ms, holodiastolic flow reversal in descending aorta.

Primary Mitral Regurgitation (MR) – 2020 AHA/ACC Stages

Stage A: At risk

  • Mitral valve prolapse (MVP) without MR, mild leaflet thickening
  • Asymptomatic, normal LV
  • Echo every 3‑5 years

Stage B: Moderate MR

  • EROA 0.20‑0.39 cm², RVol 30‑59 mL/beat, vena contracta 0.3‑0.69 cm
  • LVEF ≥60%, LVESD <40 mm, asymptomatic
  • Annual echo, treat hypertension

Stage C: Severe MR, asymptomatic

  • C1: LVEF ≥60%, LVESD <40 mm
  • C2: LVEF 40‑59% or LVESD ≥40 mm
  • Mitral surgery recommended for C2; consider for C1 if atrial fibrillation or pulmonary hypertension

Stage D: Symptomatic severe MR

  • NYHA II‑IV, or acute MR due to chordal rupture
  • EROA ≥0.40 cm², RVol ≥60 mL/beat, vena contracta ≥0.7 cm
  • Mitral valve repair (preferred) or replacement Class I
🔧 Mitral repair recommended over replacement in degenerative MR when feasible (better outcomes).

Mitral Stenosis (MS) – 2020 AHA/ACC Stages

Stage A: At risk

  • Rheumatic heart disease, mild valve thickening without obstruction
  • Asymptomatic, normal pressures
  • Echo every 3‑5 years

Stage B: Moderate MS

  • MVA 1.6‑2.0 cm², mean gradient 5‑10 mmHg, normal PAP
  • Asymptomatic
  • Echo every 1‑2 years, avoid tachycardia

Stage C: Severe MS, asymptomatic

  • MVA ≤1.5 cm², mean gradient >10 mmHg, PAP ≥50 mmHg (at rest or exercise)
  • Consider PMC if valve morphology favourable and PAP ≥50 mmHg

Stage D: Symptomatic severe MS

  • NYHA II‑IV with MVA ≤1.5 cm² and favourable anatomy → Percutaneous mitral commissurotomy (PMC)
  • Unfavourable anatomy or failed PMC → mitral valve surgery
🩺 Wilkins score (mobility, thickening, calcification, subvalvular) guides PMC suitability. Class I for symptomatic MS with favourable anatomy.

Chronic Coronary Syndromes (CCS) – 2019 ESC Classification

📋 CCS Clinical Categories

  • Suspected CAD with “stable” angina/ dyspnoea
  • New‑onset heart failure or LV dysfunction, suspected CAD
  • Asymptomatic or stable symptoms after ACS / revascularisation (>1 year)
  • Angina equivalent with vasospastic / microvascular disease
  • Incidentally found coronary calcification on CT

🫀 Angina CCS Class (Canadian Cardiovascular Society)

ClassDescriptionManagement implication
0Asymptomatic (silent ischemia)Risk factor control, aspirin, statin
IAngina only with strenuous / prolonged exertionBeta‑blocker / CCB, lifestyle modification
IISlight limitation (walking >2 blocks, climbing 1 flight)Add anti‑anginal therapy; consider functional testing
IIIMarked limitation (walking 1‑2 blocks, <1 flight)Intensive medical therapy; consider coronary angiography
IVInability to perform any activity without angina (or angina at rest)High‑risk phenotype; urgent revascularisation evaluation

📊 ESC Risk Stratification (Pre‑test probability)

  • Low probability (<15%) → Rule out with CT coronary calcium score or CT angiography
  • Intermediate (15‑50%) → Functional imaging (stress echo, CMR, SPECT, PET)
  • High (>50%) → Direct invasive angiography if symptoms refractory
💊 Guideline‑directed medical therapy: Aspirin / clopidogrel, statin, beta‑blocker, ACEi/ARB, plus SGLT2i if diabetes or LVEF ≤40%. Revascularisation (PCI/CABG) for left main, multi‑vessel disease, or severe symptoms despite optimal therapy.
