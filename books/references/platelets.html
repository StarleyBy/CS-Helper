🏥 Post-CABG Antithrombotic Therapy — 2023–2025 Guidelines

ESC 2023 ACS · ACC/AHA 2021 Revascularization · ESC 2024 CCS · STS/EACTS Consensus

Core principle: DAPT after CABG is indicated primarily based on the clinical presentation (ACS vs CCS), not the surgical procedure itself. The surgery modifies timing and agent selection, not the fundamental indication.
1 · Lifelong Aspirin — Universal Foundation

✅ Aspirin Monotherapy — Class I, Level A

Timing: Initiate as early as possible post-operatively — preferably within 6 hours, no later than 24 hours — provided no active surgical bleeding.

The 6-hour golden window: Aspirin within 6 hours of CABG significantly reduces acute SVG thrombosis and perioperative mortality.
  • Loading dose: 150–325 mg orally (or 150–300 mg rectally / 75–250 mg IV if oral unavailable)
  • Maintenance: 75–100 mg once daily (81 mg preferred in North America)
  • Duration: Lifelong — no stopping
⚠️ Key clinical pearls:
• Aspirin is indicated in virtually ALL CABG patients unless absolute contraindication (active GI bleed, true allergy with no desensitisation option)
• OAC does NOT replace aspirin in the first year post-CABG in ACS patients
• Do NOT switch to clopidogrel as aspirin substitute without specific indication
Gastroprotection (Class I, Level B): Prescribe PPI for all patients on DAPT or OAC combination. Prefer pantoprazole or rabeprazole — minimal CYP2C19 inhibition (avoid omeprazole with clopidogrel).
2 · CABG for ACS — DAPT Mandatory

🔴 ACS-CABG: Aspirin + P2Y₁₂ — 12 Months (Class I, Level A)

For STEMI, NSTEMI, Unstable Angina: Resume DAPT post-operatively. The ACS presentation mandates P2Y₁₂ therapy — the bypass surgery does not eliminate this need.

  • Switch to clopidogrel if HBR but ischemic risk demands P2Y₁₂ continuation
  • At 12 months: de-escalate to aspirin monotherapy in most patients
De-escalation strategy: In high-bleeding-risk ACS patients, consider switching ticagrelor → clopidogrel at 1–3 months to reduce bleeding without major ischemic risk increase.
3 · CABG for CCS (Stable CAD) — Aspirin Monotherapy Default

🔵 CCS-CABG: Routine DAPT NOT Recommended (Class III, Level A)

Elective CABG for stable coronary disease: Routine DAPT provides no survival benefit and significantly increases major bleeding. Standard of care = aspirin monotherapy (Class I, Level A).
Exception — Class IIb (Level B): Consider DAPT 6–12 months in selected high-risk CCS patients:
High Anatomical / Surgical Risk:
  • Multiple SVGs: DACAB trial — DAPT (aspirin + ticagrelor or clopidogrel) significantly improves 1-year SVG patency
  • Diffuse small-vessel disease: Target vessel diameter <1.5 mm, poor distal run-off
  • Coronary endarterectomy: Highly thrombogenic raw vessel wall after plaque removal
  • Redo-CABG or history of early graft failure
High Systemic Atherothrombotic Risk:
  • Multifocal atherosclerosis: Severe PAD or carotid artery stenosis
  • Complex diabetes mellitus: Poorly controlled or insulin-dependent (high platelet reactivity)
  • CKD Stage 3+: Accelerates atherothrombosis — balance against bleeding risk
SVG patency: Benefit of DAPT is almost entirely realised in vein graft protection. Arterial grafts (LIMA/RIMA, radial) have excellent patency ≥90% at 10 years — minimal additional benefit from DAPT vs aspirin alone.
4 · Off-Pump CABG (OPCAB)

⚡ OPCAB — Consider DAPT 6–12 Months (Class IIb, Level B)

OPCAB is associated with a hypercoagulable state post-operatively, which may compromise early graft patency compared to on-pump CABG. DAPT (aspirin + clopidogrel or ticagrelor) should be strongly considered for 6–12 months provided bleeding risk is low.

Mechanism: On-pump CABG activates the complement and coagulation cascade via CPB, but post-CPB anticoagulation reversal creates a defined haemostatic state. OPCAB avoids CPB but leaves a more sustained pro-thrombotic milieu in the early post-op period.
5 · Concomitant Oral Anticoagulation (OAC) — Triple to Double Strategy

🟣 Triple Antithrombotic Therapy → Minimise Duration

Patients with AF, mechanical prosthetic valves, or recent VTE require OAC. The modern paradigm: minimise triple therapy duration to avoid life-threatening bleeding. Never use ticagrelor or prasugrel in triple regimens.

Adjunct Haemostatic Agents

💉 DDAVP (Desmopressin) 0.3 mcg/kg IV

Releases endogenous vWF and factor VIII from endothelial Weibel-Palade bodies → ↑ platelet adhesion and aggregation via GPIb-vWF interaction. Partial reversal of aspirin and P2Y₁₂ effect.

Dose: 0.3 mcg/kg IV over 20–30 min (max 20 mcg). Onset 30–60 min, duration 4–8 h.
Use: Aspirin ± clopidogrel bleeding; uraemia; von Willebrand disease type 1; post-CPB microvascular bleeding.
Caution: Hyponatraemia, fluid retention (water retention via V2 receptor), cardiovascular events at higher doses. Tachyphylaxis with repeat dosing — limit to 2 doses.

💊 Antifibrinolytics

  • Tranexamic acid (TXA): Lysine analogue → blocks plasminogen binding to fibrin → inhibits fibrinolysis. First-line antifibrinolytic in cardiac surgery. Dose: 1–2 g IV loading + 1–2 g/h infusion during CPB (site-specific protocols vary). Reduces transfusion requirements 30–40%.
  • Epsilon-aminocaproic acid (EACA): Similar mechanism, less potent than TXA. Loading 5 g IV then 1 g/h. Less commonly used where TXA is available.
  • Aprotinin: Serine protease inhibitor (broad). Withdrawn in many countries due to renal concerns (BART trial); under reassessment. Where available, effective in re-do cardiac surgery and complex CPB.
Emerging Reversal Agent

🔬 Bentracimab — Specific Ticagrelor Reversal (Phase 3)

Bentracimab (PB2452) is a fully human antigen-binding fragment (Fab) antibody that binds and sequesters ticagrelor and its active metabolite in plasma, preventing receptor binding. Provides rapid, complete, and sustained reversal of ticagrelor's antiplatelet effect within minutes.

Status 2024: Phase 3 clinical trials completed (REVERSE-IT). NDA submission in progress. Will be the first specific reversal agent for a P2Y₁₂ inhibitor — fills a major unmet need in emergency CABG/surgery on ticagrelor.
Dose (investigational): 6 g IV bolus + 6 g IV infusion over 4 h.
Clinical impact: Can restore platelet function within minutes — potential game-changer for urgent cardiac surgery on ticagrelor.
Urgent CABG Decision Framework on P2Y₁₂ Inhibitor

🚨 Step-by-Step Protocol — Emergency CABG on Antiplatelet Therapy

  1. Determine true urgency: STEMI / haemodynamic instability → operate immediately regardless of antiplatelet status. Threatening ischaemia → operate within hours. Urgent but stable → reassess timing with platelet function testing.
  2. Perform platelet function assay (VerifyNow PRUtest, Multiplate ADP, or TEG Platelet Mapping) to objectively quantify residual inhibition — guides proceed vs. wait decision.
  3. Antifibrinolytic prophylaxis: Tranexamic acid 1–2 g IV loading dose before skin incision in all cases.
  4. Alert blood bank: Reserve platelets (minimum 2 adult pools), FFP, cryoprecipitate, fibrinogen concentrate. Activate massive transfusion protocol if needed.
  5. Intraoperative cell saver: Use routinely if on P2Y₁₂ inhibitor — salvages red cells and reduces allogeneic transfusion.
  6. Post-CPB haemostasis: Assess with TEG/ROTEM at separation from bypass → targeted factor replacement. Viscoelastic testing guides specific product use (platelets vs FFP vs cryoprecipitate vs fibrinogen).
  7. Platelet transfusion timing: If on clopidogrel/prasugrel → transfuse at pump-off (irreversible binding, drug won't redistribute). If on ticagrelor → wait ≥30 min after last dose/infusion before transfusing to maximise efficacy.
  8. DDAVP: 0.3 mcg/kg IV at wound closure if excess microvascular bleeding persists despite platelet transfusion.
Platelet function testing interpretation (VerifyNow PRU):
PRU >208 (<20% inhibition) → low bleeding risk; proceed without platelet transfusion
PRU 85–208 (20–50% inhibition) → intermediate; consider timing or 1 platelet pool
PRU <85 (>50% inhibition) → high bleeding risk; platelet transfusion + antifibrinolytic; consider delaying if haemodynamics permit
Clopidogrel vs Ticagrelor — key difference for urgent surgery:
Clopidogrel: platelet transfusion effective — irreversible binding, drug does not redistribute to fresh platelets
Ticagrelor: active drug redistributes to transfused platelets, reducing efficacy. Allow ≥3 days if at all possible. If truly urgent: proceed but expect more bleeding; have bentracimab available if/when approved.
Bridging with Cangrelor: For high-risk patients who need continued P2Y₁₂ coverage until surgery (e.g. recent DES stent), bridge with IV cangrelor infusion 0.75 mcg/kg/min → stop 1–2 h before surgery → platelet function recovers fully.

🔬 Platelet Function Testing

Point-of-care and laboratory assays for monitoring antiplatelet therapy and guiding perioperative decisions.

Why platelet function testing matters: Standardised waiting periods are conservative population estimates. Individual patients vary widely in drug absorption, metabolism (CYP2C19 polymorphisms for clopidogrel), and platelet turnover. POC testing allows objective, real-time assessment of residual inhibition to guide the proceed vs. delay decision in urgent surgery.
Point-of-Care Assays

VerifyNow PRUtest (Lumitronics / formerly Accumetrics)

Optical aggregometry using ADP as agonist. Reports P2Y₁₂ Reaction Units (PRU) and % inhibition. Fastest and most widely validated POC assay for P2Y₁₂ inhibitors.

  • PRU >208 (<20% inhibition): Low residual inhibition — safe to proceed without platelet transfusion
  • PRU 85–208 (20–50% inhibition): Moderate — intermediate risk; clinical judgment required
  • PRU <85 (>50% inhibition): High inhibition — platelet transfusion + delay if possible
  • VerifyNow Aspirin (ARU): ARU >550 = aspirin non-responder / non-compliant
Validated for clopidogrel, prasugrel, and ticagrelor. Results available in ~10 minutes. Whole blood — no preparation required.

Multiplate Analyzer (Roche Diagnostics)

Multiple electrode aggregometry (MEA). Uses whole blood. Multiple agonist channels: ADP, ASPI (aspirin), TRAP (GP IIb/IIIa), RISTO (vWF).

  • ADP test (6.4 µM ADP): P2Y₁₂ inhibitor assessment — <19 ADP-AU·min = significant inhibition
  • ASPI test (0.5 mM arachidonic acid): COX-1 inhibition (aspirin) — <30 ASPI-AU·min = significant
  • TRAP-6 test: General platelet function / GP IIb/IIIa assessment
  • AUC interpretation: Area under aggregation curve in arbitrary units (AU·min)
Advantages: whole blood, multiple agonists, widely used in European cardiac surgery centres. ROTEM-compatible environments. Results in 15 minutes.

TEG 6s Platelet Mapping (Haemonetics)

Thromboelastography-based viscoelastic assay. Maps percentage platelet contribution via ADP and AA pathways. Provides simultaneous coagulation and platelet function assessment.

  • ADP % inhibition: P2Y₁₂ pathway (clopidogrel/ticagrelor/prasugrel)
  • AA % inhibition: COX-1 pathway (aspirin)
  • >70% ADP inhibition: Significant P2Y₁₂ blockade — high bleeding risk
  • MA (Maximum Amplitude): Clot strength — guides platelet vs fibrinogen transfusion
Advantage: simultaneous coagulation and platelet assessment in one test — ideal for post-CPB haemostasis management. Guides targeted transfusion.

ROTEM Platelet Module (Instrumentation Laboratory)

Rotational thromboelastometry with platelet mapping channels using ADP and ASPI agonists. Fully integrates with ROTEM SIGMA for comprehensive POC coagulation panel.

  • ROTEM EXTEM/INTEM: Global clot formation and strength
  • ROTEM FIBTEM: Fibrinogen contribution (subtract from EXTEM for platelet component)
  • ROTEM Platelet ADP: P2Y₁₂ inhibitor assessment
  • ROTEM Platelet ASPI: Aspirin effect (COX-1 pathway)
Integrates seamlessly with ROTEM SIGMA for comprehensive coagulation monitoring. Platelet module added to existing ROTEM workflow without additional equipment.
Laboratory (Reference) Platelet Assays

Light Transmission Aggregometry (LTA) — Gold Standard

Uses platelet-rich plasma (PRP) + various agonists (ADP, arachidonic acid, collagen, epinephrine, ristocetin). Measures % light transmission change as platelets aggregate.

Limitations: labour-intensive, PRP preparation required, 2–4 hour turnaround — not practical for urgent surgical decisions. Used for research, drug trials, and investigation of platelet disorders.

Flow Cytometry (P-selectin / PAC-1)

Measures surface expression of P-selectin (platelet activation marker) and PAC-1 binding (GP IIb/IIIa activation state). Most sensitive method for platelet activation assessment.

Research and specialised centres only. Not practical for urgent perioperative decisions. Used in platelet disorder diagnosis and pharmacodynamic studies.

No later than 24 h if early bleeding concern. Initiate as soon as haemostasis secured.
Early initiation reduces acute SVG thrombosis and perioperative mortality. If oral route unavailable: 150–300 mg rectal / 75–250 mg IV.
Duration: Lifelong — Class I, Level A
Discontinuation increases risk of SVG occlusion and secondary cardiac events.
Dosing:
  • Maintenance: 75–100 mg once daily (81 mg preferred in North America)
  • Loading dose (if not pre-loaded): 150–325 mg oral
  • No benefit from higher doses (>100 mg) — only more GI bleeding
  • Enteric-coated formulation does not reduce GI complications significantly
⚠️ Absolute contraindications: Active life-threatening GI bleeding (temporary hold only), documented ASA hypersensitivity with anaphylaxis (consider desensitisation protocol in cardiac patients).
Pillar 2 — Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT)

🔴 CABG for ACS (STEMI / NSTEMI / Unstable Angina)

Indication: DAPT mandatory post-CABG for ACS — the antiplatelet requirement is driven by the ACS event, not the surgical procedure. Class I, Level A
Standard regimen (low bleeding risk):
  • Aspirin 75–100 mg + Ticagrelor 90 mg BID — preferred Class I
  • Aspirin + Prasugrel 10 mg OD — alternative (if no prior stroke/TIA) Class I
  • Duration: 12 months as the default strategy
Clopidogrel reserved for: Contraindications to potent agents · Concurrent OAC requirement · High bleeding risk (HBR) · CYP2C19-based de-escalation
High Bleeding Risk (HBR) modifications:
  • Shorten DAPT to 1–6 months based on ARC-HBR criteria or PRECISE-DAPT score ≥25 Class IIa, Level B
  • Consider Aspirin + Clopidogrel instead of potent P2Y₁₂ agent
  • Reassess at 3 months — switch to monotherapy if stable and HBR confirmed
De-escalation strategy: Guided de-escalation (e.g., Ticagrelor → Clopidogrel at 1 month based on platelet function testing or genotyping) is reasonable in HBR patients — reduces bleeding without significant ischaemic penalty. Class IIb

🔵 CABG for CCS / Elective Stable CAD

Routine DAPT NOT recommended after elective CABG in stable CAD — no survival benefit, significant increase in major bleeding. Class III (No Benefit), Level A
Standard of care: Aspirin monotherapy lifelong. Class I, Level A
Exception — DAPT may be considered (6–12 months) in high-risk CCS:
Class IIb, Level B
  • Multiple SVGs — DACAB trial: DAPT (ASA + Ticagrelor or Clopidogrel) significantly improves 1-year SVG patency
  • Small-vessel disease (<1.5 mm vessel diameter, poor run-off)
  • Coronary endarterectomy performed during CABG
  • Prior graft failure / redo-CABG
  • Multifocal atherosclerosis (severe PAD or carotid disease)
  • Complex DM (insulin-dependent, poorly controlled)
  • CKD stage 3+ (accelerated atherothrombosis)

Special Situations — OPCAB & Graft Type Considerations

⚡ Off-Pump CABG (OPCAB)

OPCAB is associated with a postoperative hypercoagulable state that may compromise early graft patency.

Recommendation: DAPT (ASA + Clopidogrel or Ticagrelor) strongly considered for 6–12 months if bleeding risk is low. Class IIb, Level B

🫀 Arterial vs. Venous Grafts

  • Arterial grafts (LIMA/RIMA, Radial): ≥90% patency at 10 years. Minimal additional patency benefit from DAPT vs. aspirin alone.
  • Saphenous Vein Grafts (SVG): 10–15% occlusion rate at 1 year. DAPT benefit (Class IIb) is almost entirely realised in protection of vein grafts. The primary target of the SVG patency literature (DACAB, SAFIRE-SVG, POST-CABG trials).
Mandatory Gastroprotection

🛡️ Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPI) — Class I, Level B

PPI co-prescription mandatory with DAPT or combination antithrombotic therapy in patients with GI bleeding risk factors:

  • History of GI bleeding / peptic ulcer disease
  • Age ≥65 years
  • Concurrent anticoagulant use
  • Systemic corticosteroids or NSAIDs
  • H. pylori infection (test and treat)
Preferred PPIs: Pantoprazole or Rabeprazole
Minimal competitive inhibition of CYP2C19 enzyme → does not compromise Clopidogrel's antiplatelet efficacy.

Avoid with Clopidogrel: Omeprazole and Esomeprazole (strong CYP2C19 inhibitors → ↓ Clopidogrel active metabolite by up to 40%).

Adjunct Haemostatic Agents

💉 DDAVP (Desmopressin) 0.3 mcg/kg IV

Releases vWF and factor VIII from endothelial stores → enhances platelet adhesion and aggregation. Partial reversal of platelet inhibitor effect.

Dose: 0.3 mcg/kg IV over 20–30 min (max 20 mcg). Onset 30–60 min, duration 4–8 h.
Use: Aspirin ± clopidogrel bleeding; uremia; vWD type 1; diffuse microvascular bleeding post-CPB.
⚠️ Critical caution — tachyphylaxis: Repeat doses within 24–48 h produce markedly diminished response (endothelial vWF stores depleted after first dose). Plan for only ONE effective dose; second dose rarely beneficial. Also: hyponatraemia (monitor sodium), fluid retention, and risk of cardiovascular events at higher doses in CAD patients.

💊 Antifibrinolytics

  • Tranexamic acid (TXA): Lysine analogue → blocks plasminogen binding → inhibits fibrinolysis. Standard prophylaxis in cardiac surgery. Weight-based dosing: 10–30 mg/kg loading (commonly 1–2 g in practice); many cardiac centres use 10–15 mg/kg + CPB pump prime addition (1 g). Infusion 1 g/8 h or by institutional protocol. Always follow your centre's protocol — dosing strategies vary widely. Reduces blood product transfusion by 30–40%.
  • Epsilon-aminocaproic acid (EACA): Less potent than TXA; similar mechanism. Loading 5–10 g IV over 1 h, then 1–2 g/h.
  • Aprotinin: Serine protease inhibitor (broad spectrum). Withdrawn in many countries; under reassessment — some centres use in re-do CABG on antiplatelets. Risk of anaphylaxis on re-exposure.
Emerging Reversal Agent

🔬 Bentracimab — Specific Ticagrelor Reversal Agent

Bentracimab (PB2452) is a fully human, antigen-binding fragment (Fab) antibody that binds and sequesters ticagrelor and its active metabolite in plasma, preventing P2Y₁₂ binding.

Status: Phase 3 clinical trials completed (REVERSE-IT). NDA submission in progress. Will be the first specific reversal agent for any oral antiplatelet drug.
Dose: 6 g IV over 10 min (loading) + 6 g over subsequent 12 h in Phase 3.
Onset: Platelet function restoration within minutes of loading dose — addresses the key limitation of ticagrelor reversal in emergency CABG.
Urgent CABG on P2Y₁₂ Inhibitor — Decision Framework

🚨 Practical Step-by-Step Protocol

  1. Determine true urgency: STEMI / haemodynamic instability → operate immediately. NSTEMI stable → assess if 24–48 h delay feasible for washout.
  2. Perform platelet function assay — VerifyNow PRUtest, Multiplate ADP test, or TEG Platelet Mapping to quantify residual inhibition.
  3. Antifibrinolytic prophylaxis: TXA 1–2 g IV loading dose as soon as surgery decided.
  4. Alert blood bank: Reserve platelets, FFP, cryoprecipitate, fibrinogen concentrate.
  5. Intraoperative cell saver: Use routinely if on P2Y₁₂ inhibitor — reduces allogenic transfusion.
  6. Post-CPB haemostasis: TEG/ROTEM-guided targeted therapy — treat the specific deficit shown.
  7. Platelet transfusion: Clopidogrel/prasugrel — effective (1–2 pools). Ticagrelor — partial; may need multiple units.
  8. DDAVP: 0.3 mcg/kg IV at wound closure if diffuse microvascular bleeding persists.
Platelet function testing interpretation:
<20% P2Y₁₂ inhibition → proceed without platelet transfusion
20–50% inhibition → consider timing delay or 1 platelet pool
>50% inhibition → platelet transfusion + antifibrinolytic; delay if haemodynamics allow
Clopidogrel vs Ticagrelor — critical difference:
Clopidogrel: platelet transfusion effective (irreversible, drug not redistributed)
Ticagrelor: active drug in plasma redistributes to transfused platelets — efficacy reduced. Allow ≥3 days washout when at all possible.

🔬 Platelet Function Testing — Clinical Guide

Perioperative and post-DAPT monitoring: which test to use, when, and how to interpret results in cardiac surgery.

Point-of-Care Tests

VerifyNow P2Y₁₂ (Accumetrics)

  • Principle: ADP-induced platelet aggregation in whole blood → optical turbidimetry. Result as PRU (P2Y₁₂ Reaction Units).
  • Normal (no inhibition): PRU 188–285
  • Therapeutic range (on DAPT): PRU <208 associated with ↓ ischaemic events; PRU <85 → ↑ bleeding risk
  • Pre-CABG P2Y₁₂ inhibition thresholds:
    PRU >185 Low residual inhibition — proceed with CABG; bleeding risk acceptable
    PRU 100–185 Moderate inhibition — one adult therapeutic dose + TXA peri-CABG; individualise
    PRU <100 High residual inhibition — delay CABG if haemodynamically stable; 2+ therapeutic doses; DDAVP
    PRU <85 associated with ↑ bleeding risk even on P2Y₁₂ therapy. PRU >208 = inadequate antiplatelet response ("non-responder").
  • Use case: Monitoring clopidogrel/prasugrel/ticagrelor; guiding CABG timing; identifying non-responders
  • Limitation: Not validated for GP IIb/IIIa agents; influenced by heparinisation

Multiplate Analyzer (Roche)

  • Principle: Whole blood impedance aggregometry. Multiple channels with different agonists: ADP (P2Y₁₂), ASPI (COX-1), TRAP (GP IIb/IIIa pathway), COL (collagen), RISTO (ristocetin/vWF).
  • Unit: Area Under Curve (AUC) in Units (U)
  • Reference ranges:
    • ADPtest: 53–122 U (inhibition: <53)
    • ASPItest: 71–115 U (aspirin effect: <40)
    • TRAPtest: 84–128 U
  • Advantages: Multiple pathways simultaneously; COL and TRAP assess global platelet function; RISTO screens for vWD
  • Pre-CABG: ADPtest <40 U → high residual P2Y₁₂ inhibition → increase platelet support

TEG Platelet Mapping (Haemonetics)

  • Principle: Thromboelastography with platelet-specific channels (ADP and AA agonists). Measures % platelet inhibition vs. maximally activated baseline.
  • Outputs:
    • % P2Y₁₂ inhibition (ADP channel)
    • % COX-1 inhibition (AA channel)
    • MAADP, MAAA, MAFibrin
  • Advantages: Also provides full coagulation assessment (R time, K, α angle, MA, LY30) — guides blood product selection in complex post-CPB coagulopathy
  • Pre-CABG guidance: >50% ADP inhibition → increase platelet transfusion threshold
  • Particularly useful: Ticagrelor on board — differentiates residual drug effect from thrombocytopaenia or surgical bleeding

ROTEM (Thromboelastometry)

  • Principle: Rotational thromboelastometry — viscoelastic analysis of clot formation and lysis in whole blood.
  • Channels: INTEM (intrinsic path), EXTEM (extrinsic), FIBTEM (fibrinogen), APTEM (fibrinolysis)
  • Platelet function: EXTEM − FIBTEM clot firmness = platelet contribution. Not P2Y₁₂-specific but detects global platelet dysfunction.
  • Best use: Post-CPB coagulopathy diagnosis; guiding platelet, FFP, and fibrinogen concentrate transfusion in real time
  • Limitation: Cannot specifically quantify P2Y₁₂ or COX-1 inhibition
  • ROTEM decision algorithm — cardiac surgery:
    EXTEM clot firmness (MCF) low
    ├── FIBTEM MCF normal → Platelets (platelet contribution reduced)
    └── FIBTEM MCF low → Fibrinogen concentrate / Cryoprecipitate first

    EXTEM CT prolonged → FFP / Factor concentrate (coagulation factor deficiency)

    EXTEM LY30 elevated (>15%) → TXA (hyperfibrinolysis — give immediately)

    INTEM CT prolonged + EXTEM CT normal → Heparin effect (protamine supplementation)
Laboratory Tests

Light Transmission Aggregometry (LTA) — Gold Standard

  • Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) + agonist → measure % light transmission change
  • Multiple agonists: ADP (1–10 μM), collagen, AA, epinephrine, ristocetin
  • Most sensitive and specific but requires: skilled technician, platelet separation, 2–3 h TAT — impractical perioperatively
  • Best use: formal assessment of platelet disorders; pre-op workup in elective patients

PFA-100 / PFA-200 (Siemens)

  • Measures closure time of platelet plug under high shear through membrane coated with collagen/ADP or collagen/epinephrine
  • Screens for: vWD, aspirin effect (collagen/epinephrine cartridge), and acquired platelet disorders
  • Not sensitive to P2Y₁₂ inhibitors (requires collagen/ADP cartridge for ADP pathway — limited specificity)
  • Useful for: pre-op vWD screening, aspirin resistance evaluation
