🏥️ CABG-Specific Recommendations

Elective and urgent CABG timing, aspirin management, dual antiplatelet therapy, and risk stratification.

Core principle: The optimal time to stop antiplatelet therapy before CABG balances thrombotic risk (stent thrombosis, ACS recurrence) against bleeding risk (transfusion, reoperation, tamponade). Decisions require individualisation based on indication, time since stent, haemodynamic stability, and platelet function testing.

Aspirin and CABG — The Evidence

✓ Arguments for continuing aspirin

  • Graft patency: Aspirin significantly reduces early vein graft occlusion
  • No increase in major bleeding in most CABG studies (Sethi 2010, Mangano 2002)
  • Thrombotic risk: ACS patients have very high event risk if aspirin stopped
  • ESC 2018 / AHA 2021: Class I recommendation to continue aspirin through CABG
  • Effect on bleeding: mainly increased transfusion, not reoperation or mortality

⚠️ When to stop aspirin before surgery

  • High bleeding-risk procedures (retroperitoneal, neurosurgery, posterior eye)
  • Prior significant aspirin-related GI haemorrhage
  • Severe aspirin-induced platelet dysfunction on assay
  • Patient with primary prevention aspirin only (lower thrombotic risk)
  • Centre-specific protocol after shared decision-making
If aspirin stopped before CABG: restart within 6 hours post-operatively when haemostasis confirmed (AHA 2021 Class I).
Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT) — Special Considerations

📊 Recent stent — high-risk timing

  • BMS within 4 weeks: catastrophic stent thrombosis risk if DAPT stopped
  • DES within 3 months: very high stent thrombosis risk
  • DES within 6 months: significant stent thrombosis risk
  • If CABG within these periods: continue aspirin, continue P2Y12 or switch to cangrelor bridging
  • Decision should involve interventional cardiologist + cardiac surgeon + anaesthesiologist

🔅 Bridging strategy with cangrelor

Cangrelor bridging: Switch oral P2Y12 to IV cangrelor 1–3 days before surgery. Provides continuous P2Y12 protection. Stop infusion 1–2 h before incision. Platelet function recovers within 30–60 min.

Restart oral P2Y12 as soon as haemostasis confirmed post-operatively.

Evidence: BRIDGE trial (Bhatt DL, NEJM 2013). Cangrelor bridging non-inferior for safety; maintained antiplatelet efficacy throughout hospital stay.

Adjunct Haemostatic Agents

💊 DDAVP (Desmopressin) 0.3 mcg/kg IV

Releases vWF and factor VIII from endothelial stores → enhances platelet adhesion and aggregation. Partially reverses aspirin, clopidogrel, and uraemia-related platelet dysfunction.

Dose: 0.3 mcg/kg IV over 20–30 min (max 20 mcg). Onset 30–60 min, duration 4–8 h. Tachyphylaxis after 2 doses.
Use: Aspirin ± clopidogrel bleeding; uraemia; von Willebrand disease type 1.
Caution: Hyponatraemia, fluid retention, cardiovascular events at higher doses.

💊 Antifibrinolytics

  • Tranexamic acid (TXA): Lysine analogue → blocks plasminogen binding → inhibits fibrinolysis. Standard prophylaxis in CABG (reduces transfusion 30–40%). Dose: 1–2 g IV loading → 0.25–0.5 g/h infusion during surgery. Risk: seizures at high doses.
  • Epsilon-aminocaproic acid (EACA): Less potent than TXA; similar mechanism. Loading 5–10 g IV then infusion.
  • Aprotinin: Serine protease inhibitor (broad). Withdrawn in many countries; under reassessment. Higher antifibrinolytic efficacy but thrombotic risk.
Emerging Reversal Agent

🔬 Bentracimab — Investigational Ticagrelor Reversal Agent

Bentracimab (PB2452) is a fully human, antigen-binding fragment (Fab) antibody that binds and sequesters ticagrelor and its active metabolite with high affinity, reversing platelet inhibition within minutes. Not yet widely available (as of 2025).

Status: Phase 3 clinical trials completed; NDA submission in progress. Will represent the first specific antidote for ticagrelor. Until available, platelet transfusion + DDAVP remain the mainstays of reversal, despite their limitations (ticagrelor redistribution to new platelets).
Urgent Cardiac Surgery on Antiplatelet Therapy — Practical Approach

🚨 Decision framework for urgent CABG on P2Y12 inhibitor

  1. Determine true urgency: STEMI / haemodynamic instability → operate; stable NSTEMI → consider 48–72 h wait
  2. Perform platelet function assay (VerifyNow PRUtest, Multiplate ADP): >50% inhibition → higher bleeding risk; <20% → may be safe to proceed
  3. Antifibrinolytic prophylaxis: Tranexamic acid 1–2 g IV loading dose before incision in ALL cases
  4. Alert blood bank: Reserve platelets, FFP, cryoprecipitate, fibrinogen concentrate
  5. Intraoperative cell saver: Use routinely if on P2Y12 inhibitor
  6. Post-CPB: Assess haemostasis with TEG/ROTEM → targeted therapy based on viscoelastic results
  7. Platelet transfusion: If on clopidogrel/prasugrel (effective) or ticagrelor (partially effective) — administer after CPB and protamine
  8. DDAVP: 0.3 mcg/kg IV at wound closure if excess microvascular bleeding
Platelet function testing result interpretation:
<20% residual P2Y12 inhibition → proceed without platelet transfusion
20–50% inhibition → consider timing or 1 platelet pool
>50% inhibition → platelet transfusion + antifibrinolytic; consider delaying if haemodynamics permit
Clopidogrel vs Ticagrelor urgent surgery:
Clopidogrel: platelet transfusion effective (irreversible but drug not redistributed)
Ticagrelor: active drug redistributes to transfused platelets, reducing efficacy. Allow 3 days if at all possible; if not, multiple platelet transfusions and DDAVP.

🔬 Platelet Function Testing

Point-of-care and laboratory assays for monitoring antiplatelet therapy in the perioperative cardiac surgery setting.

Why platelet function testing matters in cardiac surgery: Standardized waiting periods are conservative estimates. Individual pharmacokinetics vary widely (especially for clopidogrel: CYP2C19 polymorphisms; renal clearance for GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors). Assay-guided timing allows earlier surgery in patients with low residual inhibition, and identifies high-risk patients who need longer waiting periods or prophylactic platelet transfusion.
Point-of-Care Assays

VerifyNow PRUtest (Accumetrics)

Optical aggregometry using ADP as agonist. Reports P2Y12 Reaction Units (PRU) and % inhibition.

  • PRU <85: High platelet inhibition (>50% inhibition)
  • PRU 85–208: Moderate inhibition — intermediate risk
  • PRU >208: Low inhibition (<20%) — may be safe to proceed
  • Also: VerifyNow Aspirin (ARU) for aspirin monitoring
Validated for clopidogrel, prasugrel, and ticagrelor monitoring pre-CABG. Widely used in STS database studies. Rapid result (10 min).

Multiplate Analyzer (Roche)

Multiple electrode aggregometry (MEA). Uses whole blood. Several agonist channels: ADP, ASPI (aspirin), TRAP, RISTO.

  • ADP test: P2Y12 inhibitor assessment
  • ASPI test: COX-1 inhibition (aspirin)
  • TRAP test: GP IIb/IIIa or thrombin receptor assessment
  • Result: Aggregation units (AU) — <19 ADP-AU = significant P2Y12 inhibition
Advantages: whole blood, multiple agonists, widely available in European cardiac centres. Validated for perioperative transfusion guidance.

TEG 6s Platelet Mapping (Haemonetics)

Thromboelastography-based viscoelastic assay. Maps percentage platelet contribution via ADP and AA pathways.

  • ADP % inhibition: P2Y12 pathway (clopidogrel/ticagrelor)
  • AA % inhibition: COX-1 pathway (aspirin)
  • >70% ADP inhibition: Significant P2Y12 blockade
  • Simultaneous: Fibrinolysis, coagulation factor assessment
Advantage: simultaneous coagulation and platelet assessment. Allows targeted transfusion strategy. Common in cardiac surgery centres with TEG platforms.

ROTEM Platelet Module (Instrumentation Laboratory)

Rotational thromboelastometry with platelet mapping channels using ADP and ASPI agonists.

  • ROTEM APTEM/EXTEM: Fibrinolysis, clot strength
  • ROTEM Platelet ADP: P2Y12 inhibitor assessment
  • ROTEM Platelet ASPI: Aspirin effect assessment
Integrates with ROTEM SIGMA for comprehensive point-of-care haemostasis. Used in ESC/EACTS perioperative bleeding guidelines for targeted therapy algorithms.
Laboratory (Reference) Platelet Assays

Light Transmission Aggregometry (LTA)

Gold standard platelet aggregation assay. Uses platelet-rich plasma + various agonists (ADP, arachidonic acid, collagen, ristocetin, epinephrine). Quantifies % maximal aggregation.

Limitations: labour-intensive, PRP preparation required, not available as point-of-care. Results affected by platelet count and storage conditions.

Flow Cytometry (P-selectin / PAC-1)

Measures surface expression of P-selectin (platelet activation marker) and PAC-1 binding (GP IIb/IIIa activation). Most sensitive method for detecting low-grade platelet activation and inhibition.

Research and specialized centres; not practical for routine clinical decisions. Useful for pharmacodynamic studies of novel antiplatelet agents.
